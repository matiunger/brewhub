# Chilling & Separating Wort

*From Gordon Strong, "Brewing Better Beer" — Mastering Equipment. Part of [[matching-equipment-to-the-task]]. Chilling technique is covered under [[managing-the-boil]] and [[whirlpooling]].*

## The Choices

Two base chillers, each optionally with a **recirculating** system, a **pre-chiller** for feed water, a heat exchanger/ice bath for the recirculation loop, and optionally a **hopback**:

- **Counterflow chiller** — wort clears/settles, then chills on the way to the fermenter; allows **late hops at knockout**. Strong used this for years with great results.
- **Immersion chiller** — Strong sees little advantage *without* recirculation: slower than counterflow and bulkier to store.

**Your decision points:** how fast to chill · where the cold break ends up · whether to add late hops during chilling · how wort is separated from trub.

- **Fastest chill:** either chiller **with recirculation**, **pre-chill the feed water**, and chill the recirculated wort.
- **Natural clearing first:** counterflow chiller.

## Where the Cold Break Lands

| Setup | Cold break ends up in |
|-------|-----------------------|
| Counterflow chiller | the **fermenter** |
| Immersion chiller | the **boil kettle** |
| Counterflow **+ recirculation** | the **boil kettle** |

Remove break material by **careful racking** or **filtering through whole hops**. Significant trub in the fermenter? Treat it as a **settling tank** — rest 1–2 hours, rack clear wort to another fermenter, and **defer pitching** until it's in the final fermenter.

## Pre-chilling Feed Water

Depends on how cold you want wort entering the fermenter. For **lagers**, pre-chill to get cold sooner and pitch immediately (vs chilling the fermenter in a fridge first). For **ales**, usually unnecessary unless it's very hot out — **check feed-water temperature**; if it's above pitch temp, run it through an ice bath or heat exchanger.

## Hopback & Trub Separation

- A **hopback** helps filter wort to the fermenter and **always adds late-hop character** — **hot wort must hit the hops** for best effect.
- Strong's kettle has a **false bottom**: whole hops form the filter bed, so he can add hops whenever he likes; for a hopback-like effect he adds hops to the kettle and immediately recirculates/chills. **Pellets must go in a tight-mesh bag** or they clog it — the one part of his system he's not fully happy with (he'd prefer pellets for variety and storage).
- He's tried curved pickup tubes, screens, and filters **without much luck** — fine particles and whole hops eventually clog them. Even **bagged pellets + a whirlpool cone** make it hard to draw clear wort. Lesson: when used **as designed**, his system works great; fighting its design causes trouble. See [[whirlpooling]].
