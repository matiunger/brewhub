# Fermenting & Conditioning Equipment

*From Gordon Strong, "Brewing Better Beer" — Mastering Equipment. Part of [[matching-equipment-to-the-task]]. For fermentation practice, see [[managing-fermentation]].*

## Fermenter Types

| Type | Strong's take |
|------|---------------|
| **Carboy** *(preferred)* | Easiest to clean; can see inside without opening. Brews 5.5-gal (20.8-L) batches in **6.5-gal (24.6-L)** carboys with **handles** for lifting, to contain fermentation. |
| **Cylindro-conical** | Loves the concept (easy trub dump, racking, yeast capture for repitch) but **hard to move and harder to clean**; since he ferments away from where he brews, he'd still need carboys to carry wort. Owns one — eyeing it as a dedicated lager system. |
| **Plastic bucket** | Good for **top-cropping** yeast or heavy **blowoff** (WY3068, WY3787), and fruit meads (frequent access). **Not just for beginners** — enables **open fermentation** and experiments with **fermenter geometry / head pressure** to manipulate esters. |

## Airlocks

All types work — pick what you like and **don't let them run dry**. **Tip:** use **"universal" stoppers** instead of numbered ones — they can't be pushed into the carboy and fit a wider range of openings.

## Aeration / Oxygenation

- **Oxygen (preferred):** sintered stainless **diffusion stone** + O₂ tank, regulator, and filter (bought as a kit; tank from a hardware store).
- **Air:** a **mix-stir on a drill** (designed to de-gas wine) — work up a vortex and whip air in. Aquarium pumps also work, but if setting up anyway, Strong uses O₂ for better results.
- **Inline aeration** while filling the fermenter (wort sprays through small openings) is simple and effective — just **control the foam**, since the carboy fills while air is added. Post-fill aeration lets you *see* when to stop. See [[managing-fermentation]] and [[experiment-pitching-rate-and-oxygenation]].

## Starters

Get a **stir plate and an Erlenmeyer flask** — growlers and bottles work, but the stir plate's advantages are too big to ignore (much more predictable results). See [[starters-with-dry-yeast]] and [[preparing-yeast]]; build one via [[pwm-controlled-stir-plate-design]].

## Temperature Zones

Strong adjusts his **brewing schedule to ambient temperatures** and (lacking a basement) uses dedicated fridges: a **walk-in at 48–50°F (9–10°C)** for lager fermentation and cold cellaring, a **chest freezer at 34°F (1°C)** for lagering, and house spots in the 60s (16–20°C) for ales.

## Pressurizable Plastic Cylindro-Conicals

*Via the Birra Podcast, T1E09.* A plastic cylindro-conical fermenter that can hold **300–500 g of CO₂ pressure** (e.g. Roto King, Infinity, or similar) gets you most of the practical benefit of a steel isobaric fermenter at a fraction of the cost: it lets you retain aromatic volatiles in hoppy beers and prevents oxygen pickup during cooling — when the beer contracts as it chills, positive internal pressure stops it from drawing in outside air the way an unpressurized vessel would. If your fermenter is cylindrical rather than conical, a side port fitted low near the bottom (with a valve) gives you a way to purge trub, dead yeast, and settled hop material, similar in effect to a true cone; tilting the vessel to concentrate sediment toward the outlet is a low-tech fallback.

**Cooling a plastic fermenter down to cold-conditioning temperature is the single riskiest moment for oxygen pickup**, even more than transfers — as the beer contracts going from ~21°C down to ~4°C, that lost volume gets replaced with something, and in an unpressurized plastic vessel that something is air. *(Via the Hernán Castellani interview.)* Counter it with a **thin, continuous trickle of CO₂ fed in through the top of the tank while it cools** — you don't need to build positive pressure, just make sure the contracting volume gets backfilled with CO₂ instead of air. CO₂ is cheap enough that this is a low-cost fix. For a pressure-rated vessel, **0.5 bar is enough purely for anti-oxidation purposes**, and even 1 bar (14.7 psi) is more than sufficient headroom for any homebrew process step — pressure-rated fermenters that hold more than that are more convenient, not more necessary.

**A simple DIY CO₂-push transfer for a bucket-style fermenter**: fit the lid with two tank-style bulkhead connections with O-rings — one feeding CO₂ in, the other holding a dip tube down to the bottom to draw liquid out. As little as 0.1–0.2 bar is enough to push beer to a conditioning vessel without introducing oxygen; the volume of beer leaving is replaced by CO₂ entering, so the fermenter never pulls a vacuum or draws in air. A standard wide-mouth lid generally tops out around 100–150 g of pressure, but that's plenty for this kind of gentle transfer (see [[cold-dry-hopping-and-oxygen-control]] for the same principle applied to dry hopping).

## Individual Temperature Control per Fermenter

*Via the Birra Podcast, T1E09.* Rather than relying on ambient room or single-chamber cooling, wrap each fermenter in its own cold jacket or coil ("torpedo") fed by a cold-water or glycol bath (which can be as simple as a chest freezer). This gives per-vessel control independent of what else is fermenting nearby, and works even on plastic fermenters (lower thermal conductivity than steel, but still effective) provided the vessel is well-sealed and insulated.

**Always probe the wort/beer itself, not the surrounding air**, when controlling fermentation temperature — air is a poor conductor and fermentation is exothermic, so a fermenter can run 3–7°C warmer than the surrounding air suggests. Neither a walk-in cold room nor a bedroom air conditioner reliably fixes this on their own if the temperature probe is reading air rather than liquid.

## Other Gear

Wine thief (sampling), hydrometer + jar, infrared thermometer, **pH meter** ([[phmeter-buying-guide]]), blowoff tube + catch jar, siphon, **CO₂ tank** (purging/blanketing), brushes + carboy spray wand. **Cleans with PBW, sanitizes with StarSan or One-Step** — see [[cleaning]]. Secondary/conditioning uses the same carboys; he **transfers to a keg for lagering** (easier to load than carboys) fitted with an **adjustable pressure-relief valve**.
