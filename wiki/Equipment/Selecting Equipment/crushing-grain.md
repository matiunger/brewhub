# Crushing Grain — The Mill

*From Gordon Strong, "Brewing Better Beer" — Mastering Equipment. Part of [[matching-equipment-to-the-task]]. For crush technique and wet-milling, see [[preparing-malt]].*

Buying grain in bulk and crushing as needed is far more convenient than precrushed — it requires a **mill**. The goal: **crush the endosperm without shredding the husk.**

## Selecting a Mill

| Consideration | Questions |
|---------------|-----------|
| **Quality of crush** | Crushes without tearing husks? Performance on different grains? Built to crush grain, not make flour? |
| **Adjustability** | Gap adjustable, measurable, stable through a session? How many rollers, all adjustable? |
| **Capacity** | Hopper size; integrated or add-on? |
| **Throughput** | Pounds per minute |
| **Automation** | Drive with a motor or drill? |
| **Output** | How grain ejects; dust containment? |
| **Mounting** | Permanent vs set-up each time; extra equipment needed? |
| **Quality** | Build robustness, lifetime, replaceable parts, maintenance |

> **Buying advice:** most commercial mills are good quality. **Avoid Corona-type mills** (designed to crush corn). If buying new, look for **large-diameter rollers** plus the features above.

## Strong's Setup

A **Listermann PhilMill I** used since he started — table-mounted, drill-driven, modified with a **5-gal plastic jug hopper** (holds 20+ lb / 9.1 kg) and a **dryer-hose output chute** into a 6-gal bucket to control dust. Heavily modified, but performs so well he won't replace it.

**Mill-station tips:** locate it by your grain storage, with scales, good lighting, electricity for the drill, and tools handy (utility knife for sacks, wrenches/screwdrivers for adjustments). **Check off grains from the recipe** as you weigh and add them to the hopper.
