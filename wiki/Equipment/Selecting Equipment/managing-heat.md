# Managing Heat

*From Gordon Strong, "Brewing Better Beer" — Mastering Equipment. Part of [[matching-equipment-to-the-task]].*

Considered together with [[moving-liquid]] as the **core of system design** — together they set how many vessels you use, their materials, and how they link. Heat drives how you **mash, sparge, and boil**.

## What to Evaluate

| Factor | Questions |
|--------|-----------|
| **Heat source** | Fuel type, availability, ease of use, safety; how many sources, and tied to specific vessels? |
| **How applied** | Direct-heat the vessel, or heat ingredients separately? |
| **Control** | Adjust heat level; hold a specific temperature? |
| **Conservation** | Prevent heat loss; insulated vessels? |

Key questions it forces: direct-fire kettles or add externally-boiled water? If you miss mash temp, can you adjust in place or must you move the grain? How many burners and vessels, and are any reusable in other roles?

## Strong's Evolution

- **Started** with picnic coolers + stovetop hot water. Coolers held temperature well (insulation), but **infusing boiling water didn't always raise temp enough**, so he often had to **decoct** to hit targets, and worried about mash consistency changing.
- **Now:** a **three-vessel, half-barrel keg system**, each keg with its **own adjustable propane burner** (tanks easily refilled/exchanged). Total control; can work multiple steps — even a second batch — at once.
  - HLT, mash/lauter tun, and boil kettle.
  - To **decoct**, uses an external pot on the burner from an idle vessel (usually the empty boil kettle).
  - **Insulated wraps** on mash tun and HLT cut heat loss from **10°F+ (4.5°C+) per hour to ~1°F (0.5°C) per hour**.
  - Lids conserve heat; can direct-fire and hold temp by **recirculating with the pumps** while burners are lit.
