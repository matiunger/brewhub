# Mashing — The Mash Tun

*From Gordon Strong, "Brewing Better Beer" — Mastering Equipment. Part of [[matching-equipment-to-the-task]]. For mash technique, see [[the-theory-of-mashing]] and [[single-infusion-mash]].*

The mash tun needs only to **hold all your crushed grain and water** and **maintain temperature for an hour**. It may or may not double as the lauter tun (see [[lautering-equipment]]).

## Three Common Options

| Option | Notes |
|--------|-------|
| **Rectangular picnic cooler** | Cheap, holds temp very well, but **can't be direct-heated** — takes practice to hit mash temps (preheat with boiling water; measure water temps/volumes; brewing software helps) |
| **10-gal cylindrical Gott cooler** | Same no-direct-heat tradeoff |
| **Large metal pot (≥9 gal) or cut keg (half-barrel)** | **Direct-fireable** to change temp; large pots won't fit most kitchen stoves → need an outdoor burner |

## Sizing to Your Batches

Match the tun to your batches: can it make a 5-gal batch? a 10-gal batch? a 5-gal batch of your **highest-gravity** beer? Strong's **half-barrel** tun tops out at ~**32 lb (14.5 kg)** of grain — enough for a very high-gravity 5-gal batch, with flexibility to make 10-gal batches to split or run side-by-side experiments.

## Tips

- **Tight budget/space → picnic cooler.** Holds temp well; usable as a lauter tun too (takes some effort in some configs).
- **Decoction → an extra pot**, smaller than the tun (you pull only ~30–40% of the grain mass, no false bottom needed). A **heavy-bottom stockpot** reduces scorching. See [[decoction-mash]].
- **Transfer grain** with a kitchen saucepan as a scoop — holds grain + liquid and maneuvers well.
