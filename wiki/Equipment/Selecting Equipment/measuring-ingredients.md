# Measuring Ingredients

*From Gordon Strong, "Brewing Better Beer" — Mastering Equipment. Part of [[matching-equipment-to-the-task]].*

Accurate measurement is critical to making your recipe: **grains and hops by weight, water by volume.**

## Choosing Scales

Strong uses **two scales**: a large one (up to 13 lb / 5.9 kg in 0.1-oz / 2.8-g steps) for grain, and a small **0.1-gram** scale for hops and additives. Big capacity *or* fine resolution — rarely both in one unit.

Attributes to weigh when selecting:

| Attribute | Question |
|-----------|----------|
| **Capacity** | Max total weight it holds |
| **Resolution** | Increments it displays |
| **Units** | English and/or metric |
| **Portability** | Easily moved? |
| **Speed** | How fast it reads |
| **Calibration** | How it's adjusted to a reference weight |
| **Taring** | Can it zero out the container's weight? |
| **Size** | Fits your containers and still readable? |
| **Durability** | Handles your conditions, including getting wet? |

## Tips

- **Approximate bulk weights with marked, standardized containers** — Strong uses two scoops (~3 lb and ~1 lb) to transfer grain from sacks to weighing containers.
- Weigh hops in small bowls (glass for pellets/additives, larger plastic for whole hops) directly on a **tared** scale.
- **Water:** measure with cups (2-qt / 1.9-L and 1-pint / 473-mL) and 5-gal jugs, but mostly measure *in the brewing vessel* — use a **calibrated measuring stick** (or a sight glass) to read the water level. See [[learning-your-system]] for calibrating volume readings.
