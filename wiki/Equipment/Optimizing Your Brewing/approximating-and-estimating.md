# Approximating & Estimating

*From Gordon Strong, "Brewing Better Beer" — Mastering Equipment. Part of [[optimizing-your-brewing]].*

Extend "focus effort where it matters" to **measurement itself**. Many steps need a real measurement — but a **well-reasoned estimate from experience** can often substitute. Knowing **when** is tied to understanding what's truly critical versus what tolerates a wider margin and still succeeds.

## Estimating Is Measuring — With Your Senses

- Tell the **mash is done** by the changes (cloudy → dark and clear, starchy → sweet) plus elapsed time. See [[iodine-test]].
- **Estimate grain** from past usage: if 10 lb (4.5 kg) makes 5 gal of ~1.054 on your system, you can estimate the grain for a 1.040 beer.
- **Estimate hops** in **AAUs** (alpha-acid % × ounces — e.g. 2 oz of 5% AA = 10 AAU). Strong finds that on his system, with whole hops, a **60-minute addition adds ~3.5 IBU per AAU** — so those 2 oz of 5% AA used for bittering give him roughly **35 IBU**. Calibrate the same multiplier for *your* system and you can skip the utilization formulas. See [[hopping]] and [[selecting-hops]].

Like chefs who rarely measure while cooking — they know by sight and feel, and know they can correct later. Brewing is **like cooking, not baking**: you can correct as you go, so you don't need constant measurements. (Even top chefs measure when **baking** — no way to fix ratio errors after.) **Measure to confirm your estimates while learning**; once mastered, you can skip many readings and brew faster.

## Accuracy vs Precision

Often confused, but completely different:

- **Accuracy** = how close a reading is to the **actual** value.
- **Precision** = the **exactness** of how it's specified (significant digits / decimal places).

> A thermometer that reads sea-level boiling water as **208.43692°F** is **very precise but inaccurate** — it needs calibration. Five decimal places fool people into trusting it. **A precise instrument is worthless unless calibrated to read accurately.**

Both measurements and estimates are **models** of a physical value — not the value itself. Models must be **tested**. Instruments need calibration and still carry a **tolerance** (e.g. ±0.1°F); estimates carry a tolerance too, set by your judgment. Understand the **sources of error** in any instrument — tool or your own senses.

## Address Accuracy Before Precision

When measuring, know **where and how**. Probe your mash at different depths and distances — are readings consistent? Then consider the enzymes:

- Enzymes **don't stop abruptly** at a temperature; they decline slowly, so **152 vs 154°F (67 vs 68°C) barely matters**.
- But **wide readings across the tun** reveal you have **less temperature control than you thought**. Fix the **accuracy** (get consistent readings) **before** chasing a degree or two of precision.
- If you won't address tun-wide variation, **don't waste time** heating/chilling for a few degrees — go with average temperatures; they level out over time. It's a matter of **scale**.

> **Mastery is knowing when to leave things alone** because they're working — not constant correction. *Sometimes the correct action is to do nothing.* Trust your palate and let experience guide you.
