# Small-Batch (Nano) Brewing

*Via the Birra Podcast, "Nano homebrewing" (T2E14). Practical notes for brewing at very small scale (6–10 L finished), where measurement, sourcing, and equipment choices all look different than at a standard 20 L+ homebrew batch. Complements [[matching-equipment-to-the-task]] and [[measuring-ingredients]].*

## A Working Nano Setup

A workable small-batch kit: a 20 L pot with a false bottom for mashing, plus a 10 L stainless pot for the boil, with manual recirculation — this combination lands around 6–8 L finished. Prioritize a **good pH meter and thermometer** over kettle upgrades — at this scale, tight control of the variables that actually drive quality matters more than bigger or fancier cook vessels.

**Ferment in steel, not plastic.** Swapping plastic carboys for **10 L Cornelius kegs** as fermenters is one of the highest-impact upgrades available at nano scale: steel lets you ferment and condition in the same vessel, do closed CO₂-pushed transfers, and dry hop with minimal oxygen pickup — see [[cold-dry-hopping-and-oxygen-control]] for why that matters so much for hoppy beers specifically.

### Closed Dry Hopping Between Two Kegs

1. Put the hop charge (in a mesh bag — loose pellets can jam the dip tube during transfer) into a second, smaller receiving keg (6 or 10 L).
2. Purge the receiving keg with CO₂.
3. Transfer beer from the fermentation keg into the receiving keg, pushing with CO₂ the whole way.

**Limit hop contact to ~24 hours**, then transfer again to a third, clean keg — leaving hops in contact for a full week at this scale risks vegetal/astringent character. Since transfers between differently-sized Corny kegs need it, **swap dip tubes** so the tube length matches the destination keg (e.g. use the short dip tube from a 6 L keg inside a 10 L keg) so it doesn't reach all the way to the bottom and pick up sediment; cutting a tube to length is the other option. Avoid silicone floating dip tube attachments for sanitary reasons.

## Why Small Batches Accelerate Learning

**Batch size barely changes the time/effort per brew day** — an 8 L batch and a 50–100 L batch take roughly the same hands-on time and attention. The real advantage of a small system is **brewing far more often** for the same total time investment, and repetition is what actually accelerates learning. Expect **50–60% brewhouse efficiency** at nano scale (well below the 80%+ target of larger systems) — a 10 L mash tun might net only 6 L finished — and size recipes accordingly rather than assuming standard-scale efficiency numbers apply.

**Small volumes also give better process control, not just faster iteration**: a pH sample from an 8 L batch is proportionally far more representative of the whole batch than the same sample size from 100 L, and fermentation temperature and pitch rate are both easier to hit precisely at small scale.

**Split-batch testing is the single best experimental design available**: brew one larger wort, then divide it across several small fermenters (kegs) and change exactly one variable per vessel — yeast strain, hop variety, or the dose of one ingredient. Because everything else is identical, any sensory difference you find can be attributed to that one variable with real confidence — see [[recipe-formulation]] for how this fits into building ingredient familiarity.

## Precision Tools That Matter More at Small Scale

- **A precision scale (gram-resolution, kitchen-calculator-sized) is close to mandatory.** Nano batches routinely call for 3–4 g hop additions and 2–3 g water salt additions — a standard kitchen scale can't resolve that reliably, and the margin for error is much larger proportionally than at a bigger batch size.
- **Prefer a refractometer over a hydrometer for in-process readings.** Pulling a 50 mL hydrometer sample from a 6–10 L fermenter is a meaningful volume loss; a refractometer needs only a few drops. Reserve the hydrometer for the readings that matter most — OG going in, FG coming out — and remember to apply the alcohol correction to any refractometer reading taken after fermentation has started.
- **Nutrient doses shrink to near-unmeasurable amounts.** 2 g/hL of a product like Servomyces or SpringFerm works out to roughly 0.15–0.16 g for an 8 L batch — below what most home scales resolve reliably. In practice this becomes "a knife-tip's worth" rather than a precisely weighed dose; a precision scale helps but doesn't fully solve the problem at this size.

## Estimating Gravity Without Sampling: The Bubble Curve

Correlate blow-off bubble rate against measured gravity the first time you use a given strain: route the blow-off through a tube into a jar of alcohol/sanitizer, count bubbles per unit time, and log it alongside gravity readings at the same points in fermentation. On later batches with the same strain and a similar starting gravity, that bubble-rate curve gives a reasonable gravity estimate **without pulling a sample** — useful for knowing when you're at the "~5 points remaining" mark for a warm dry hop (see [[hopping]]) without wasting sample volume on a batch this small.

## DIY Cell Counting: "Yeastocrit"

A microscope-free way to estimate yeast concentration, borrowed from the clinical hematocrit test: let a sample settle in a capillary tube and read the settled-cell percentage against total liquid volume. Calibrate against a known reference — a dilution series made from baker's dry yeast, counted under a microscope or cell counter if you have access to one — to convert the settled percentage into an actual cell-density estimate.

## Sourcing Fresher Hops at Small Scale

Off-flavors that read as **solvent/acetone** in NEIPAs are frequently traceable to old or poorly-stored hops — if the same solvent character shows up across multiple recipes, check whether hops are the common ingredient before looking elsewhere; a glue/nail-polish note specifically points to oxidized hop material. Small home purchases (50–100 g, fractionated from a larger bag by a supplier) often come with no reliable harvest-date information, so sensory-check hops before using them (see [[malt-production-and-character]]'s malt tea test for the analogous hop technique in [[selecting-hops]]).

**Buy small quantities directly from friendly local breweries** when they open a large hop bag for their own dry hopping — the quality is far higher than fractionated hops from a retail supplier, since it comes from a bag that was just opened rather than one that's been split and reheld multiple times. Asking a brewery for 50–200 g out of a 50 kg dry-hop addition is imperceptible to them and a meaningful upgrade for the homebrewer; vacuum-seal it immediately on receiving it (see [[storing-hops]]).

## Water and Temperature Without Dedicated Equipment

Know your local water's ion profile before deciding what styles are practical to brew, and run it through an activated carbon filter to remove chlorine at minimum (see [[water-chemistry]]). If a fellow local homebrewer has already measured the same municipal source, their numbers are a usable reference point rather than needing your own lab test.

**Without a dedicated fermentation fridge**, workable options include: pointing a room air conditioner at the fermenter, manually moving the vessel in and out of a household fridge based on an adhesive strip thermometer reading, or choosing a heat-tolerant strain (Kveik ferments well around 35°C) to sidestep the cooling problem entirely. A shared household fridge (~3–5°C) can work for lager fermentation specifically — experiment with pitch rate and timing to manage yeast stress at that low a temperature without a purpose-built setup.

## Bonus: Kettle Souring Technique Notes

*From the same episode — general technique, not nano-specific, but covered alongside the small-batch content.*

- **Boiling after lactobacillus souring vs. not boiling**: in a side-by-side Berliner Weisse comparison, flavor came out nearly identical either way, but the unboiled version had a strong, unpleasant live-lacto aroma that dissipated over time — the boiled version lost those volatiles immediately through evaporation.
- **Pasteurize instead of boiling to protect foam, in a kettle sour**: heating to 80°C for 5 minutes kills the lactobacillus (ending souring) without fully boiling — this matters because above ~85°C, a lactobacillus enzyme drives lactic proteolysis that breaks down the same proteins responsible for foam retention (see [[the-science-of-foam]]). Pasteurizing at 80°C avoids triggering that enzyme while still stopping fermentation, preserving foam potential that a full boil (or an unpasteurized, over-soured kettle) would otherwise sacrifice.
- **A commercial probiotic drink (e.g. Actimel-style products) can serve as a lactobacillus source** for a kettle sour — some brands list bacterial unit counts on the label, which helps with dosing.
