# Planning Your Brew Day

*From Gordon Strong, "Brewing Better Beer" — Mastering Equipment. Part of [[optimizing-your-brewing]].*

Plan the brew day like a chef: ***mise en place*** — all ingredients measured and available, all equipment cleaned and set up, all instructions ready **before** you start.

## Sequencing

Break the day into steps; consider order, equipment setup, ingredients, and time. **Interleave prep with brewing** to shorten the day, and do some **setup the night before** for a fast start.

- **Start the longest task first.** Identify what eats the most time (heating strike water? setting up burners/kettles? milling? mashing?) and get it going early — and take preliminary steps to start it quickly.

## Critical Path

Borrow the project-management idea: the **critical path** is the sequence of dependent tasks that sets the **minimum** time to finish. Any delay on the critical path ("the weakest link") delays everything; off-path tasks have slack. You only have to work this out **once** — then repeat the sequence every brew, and see where optional steps or problems would ripple.

## Checklists

If you keep **forgetting tasks** (the classic missed Irish moss), at least walk the planning thought-process. A printed, ordered **checklist** of your common steps — checked off per batch and kept handy — prevents skips, especially if you brew infrequently.

If you're not a planner, at least **think several steps ahead**:

- Keep brewing equipment **organized in one area**; buy extras of anything you routinely borrow from the kitchen, so a forgotten item is close by.
- Keep **spare consumables** — always an extra propane tank, an extra container of RO water.
- **Take notes during sessions** on what to improve: dumping extra water? reheating water repeatedly? adding things to correct earlier mistakes? Most of these vanish with planning.

## Prioritize, Don't Just Sequence

Not every step matters equally. Brewers waste disproportionate effort on low-consequence steps.

> **Sanitation example:** on the **hot side** it matters far less — you don't need to sanitize the mash paddle when the wort will be boiled. That wasted effort carries an **opportunity cost**: time not spent on what actually mattered. Strong would rather a brewer nail **mash pH** than obsess over a [[water-chemistry]] spreadsheet.

Optimize and you may brew in a **half day**, or fit **two or three batches** into a little more than one used to take (extend the critical-path model to overlap batches rather than running them sequentially). And remember: brewing is **a series of small course corrections** — a good plan means you assess and adjust calmly when something goes wrong. People taste the **finished beer**, not your process.
