# Cleaning & Sanitization

## Why Clean & Sanitize?

Proper cleaning and sanitization are crucial for successful brewing.
Cleaning removes dirt, debris, and organic matter, while sanitizing
kills microorganisms that can spoil your beer.

## Cleaning

- Rinse equipment immediately after use
- Use PBW (Powdered Brewery Wash) or similar cleaner
- Soak for 15-30 minutes for stubborn residue
- Scrub with soft brush - avoid scratching plastic
- Rinse thoroughly with hot water

## Sanitization

- Clean first, then sanitize
- Use Star San, Iodophor, or similar sanitizer
- Follow manufacturer's dilution instructions
- Contact time: usually 1-2 minutes
- No rinse needed for most sanitizers
- Sanitize anything that touches wort/beer after boiling

## Tips

- Never use abrasive scrubbers on fermenters
- Replace scratched plastic equipment
- Store equipment dry to prevent mold
- Make fresh sanitizer solution for each brew day

## Know What Your Equipment's Materials Can Tolerate

*Via the Birra Podcast, T2E05.* Every material has chemical and thermal limits, and pushing past them degrades equipment and can leach off-flavors into beer:

- **Aluminum** doesn't tolerate acids or bases well — avoid caustic, nitric, phosphoric, and peracetic cleaners on aluminum parts.
- **Plastic** can handle caustic only at low concentration and low temperature — it won't tolerate something like a 4% caustic solution at 70°C, even though that's a normal commercial CIP condition for stainless.
- **Copper** needs care around high temperatures.
- **Don't transfer hot, acidic wort into a rotomolded plastic fermenter.** Around 40°C, acidic hot wort can leach phenolic compounds out of the plastic. Chill to ~20°C first, even if that takes longer (an extra 45 minutes chilling is not a problem worth avoiding by cutting this corner) — the same caution applies to pumping hot wort or hot caustic (80°C) through hoses that aren't rated food-grade/high-temperature.
- **Replace clear ("cristal") hoses once they've discolored** from clear to brown — that's embedded biofilm, and a hose in that state can no longer be reliably cleaned or sanitized. Expect these hoses to have a shorter service life than higher-grade tubing and budget for regular replacement.

## Validate, Don't Assume

*Via the Birra Podcast, T2E05.* Validating means actually confirming a process works, not just assuming it does because you followed the steps:

- **Cleaning validation**: run clean water through the equipment and check that it comes out colorless, odorless, and tasteless.
- **Sanitation validation**: run something sterile across the sanitized surface and confirm nothing grows from it afterward — see the forced wort test in [[diy-quality-control-tests]] for a practical version of this check applied to a whole fermenter/brewhouse.
