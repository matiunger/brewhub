# Adjuncts

*From Gordon Strong, "Brewing Better Beer" — Mastering Ingredients. Part of [[mastering-ingredients]].*

Adjuncts are sugar sources **other than malt**. Modern use is about adjusting **flavor, fermentability, residual sweetness, and body** — not the historical role of cheapening or diluting six-row protein.

## Two Families

| Type | Examples | How to add |
|------|----------|------------|
| **Sugary** | sugar, honey, molasses, treacle, maple syrup, candi syrup | Directly to the boil or after |
| **Starchy (unmalted)** | oats, rye, wheat, corn/maize, rice, barley | Must be **mashed** with grain for sugars; **no enzymes of their own** |

> If a grain is *malted*, it counts as malt, not an adjunct.

## Sugary Adjuncts

- **Plain sugar** raises gravity, **lightens body, boosts attenuation** — e.g. up to **20% in a tripel**, **10% in a double IPA** for a drier finish.
- Other sugars are chosen for **flavor**; fermentability varies, so they don't always raise attenuation (always raise gravity).
- **Raw sugars** tend to increase fermentability and add flavor. **Honey** varies (sometimes thins body, sometimes not — depends on variety/amount; can give a rounder flavor). **Molasses, maple, treacle** add strong flavor (blackstrap especially) and take time to age out.

## Starchy Adjuncts — Gelatinization Is the Key Question

Unmalted starch granules must be **burst** to hydrate and become accessible to enzymes. Adjuncts have **no enzymes**, so the **base malt's DP must convert both itself and the adjunct** — pair adjuncts with **six-row**, *not* with Munich. See [[malt-modification-dp-and-protein]].

**By form (easiest → most work):**

- **Flaked** — pre-gelatinized on heated rollers. Add **directly to the mash**, no milling. Easiest.
- **Torrefied** — also pre-burst (e.g. torrefied wheat, "Grape-Nuts" taste). Mill and add to the mash.
- **Quick-cooked** (quick grits/oats, converted rice) — pre-steamed, more flavor than flaked, add directly.
- **Raw** (steel-cut oats, grits, cracked wheat) — strongest flavor but require a **cereal mash**.

## Cereal Mash (for raw grits)

About as time-consuming as a decoction:

1. Mill ~**10% of your base malt** with the milled grits.
2. Cover with warm water (**2–3 qt/lb, 4–6 L/kg**), slowly heat to **158°F (70°C)** stirring continuously, hold **15 min**.
3. Bring to a **gentle boil 15 min**, stirring.
4. Add to the main mash to finish conversion.

> Why add base malt? Its enzymes keep the gelatinized starch from setting into glue, so it mixes into the main mash. Boiling fully bursts the granules; the cereal-mash enzymes are denatured by then, so the **main mash completes conversion**.
