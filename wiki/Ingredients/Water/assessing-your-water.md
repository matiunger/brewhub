# Assessing Your Water

*From Gordon Strong, "Brewing Better Beer" — Mastering Ingredients. Part of [[water-philosophy]].*

Before adjusting, understand what you have. Treat it as **triage**: is the water good as-is, does it need **adjusting/blending**, or is it **unusable** and must be replaced?

## Step 1 — Smell & Taste

Does it taste good and clean, free of off-aromas? If it smells of **chlorine**, run it through a **charcoal (Brita) filter** and retaste — that removes chlorine and some calcium. If the filtered water tastes good, a charcoal filtration system can likely treat your brewing water.

## Step 2 — Ions & pH

- **Municipal:** request a free analysis from your supplier (often poorly formatted and full of extraneous data, but it should give pH, alkalinity, hardness, and key ion concentrations).
- **Well water, or report lacking detail:** send a sample to **Ward Labs** (Household Complete Mineral Test) — inexpensive, reliable, the best choice for homebrewers without their own analysis.
- Store the profile in your brewing software.

## Step 3 — Decide

- **Slightly high in desirable ions but low carbonates** → **dilute with RO/distilled/DI water** (straight algebra: same ions, more volume, lower concentration).
- **Too much effort to fix** (high carbonate, high pH, iron, contaminants, salty/off taste) → **start from RO** and build the profile you want. This is what Strong does (his water has excess carbonates, high pH, high TDS — "minerally"). He may add yeast nutrient for trace elements.

## Practical Notes

- **Aim for a reliable, predictable supply** — constant testing/adjusting is tiring. A steady source (e.g. RO) means you don't recalculate pH/calcium adjustments every batch.
- **RO ≠ distilled/DI** — it can retain some minerals depending on membrane condition. Treat RO as roughly neutral, but **check TDS** if you want certainty (Strong assumes any TDS reading is calcium carbonate from his supply).
- **Water changes by season** — consider an analysis per season; ask the water department how it varies.
- If you don't analyze every brew, you're **approximating** — so don't calculate salt additions to three decimals when you don't know your baseline. *Precision ≠ accuracy.*
