# Adjusting Your Water

*From Gordon Strong, "Brewing Better Beer" — Mastering Ingredients. Part of [[water-philosophy]]. Includes Strong's "So What Do I Do?" approach. See also [[salts]], [[various-water-recipes]].*

Strong thinks in three phases: **chlorine removal → ion adjustment → pH adjustment**. Focus on **mash pH chemistry** and **finished flavor**; everything else is secondary.

## Chlorine Removal (do this first)

Chlorine/chloramine → medicinal **chlorophenolic** flavors.

- **Chlorine:** active carbon filtering, or **boil before use** (chlorophenolics form the moment chlorine meets malt).
- **Chloramine:** boiling **won't** remove it — **filter it out** or neutralize with **Campden** (1 tablet / ~695 mg potassium metabisulfite per **20 gal / 75.7 L**).
- **Bypass any water softener** — it swaps sodium (unwanted) for calcium/magnesium (wanted). It's fine only as **pre-treatment before RO**.

## The Core Rules

- **Be aware you're adding a salt, not an element** — calcium comes with sulfate, chloride, or carbonate. Weigh every ion's effect on flavor, process, and pH.
- **The mash settles to a proper pH on its own — don't force it.** If paranoid, ensure **≥50 ppm calcium** in the mash and **sparge water below pH 5.8**.
- **Hardness good, alkalinity bad.** High alkalinity → use RO/distilled/DI. **Color is *not* a reason to adjust water.**
- **Add minerals to the mash for pH/calcium only**; everything else is for finished flavor and can go in the **kettle**. (Salts in the mash don't all carry to the kettle; sparging dilutes them — and you want some calcium in the boil for break and yeast health.)
- **Don't mash dark grains just to buffer them** — add dark/crystal malts at **mash-out/vorlauf** or **cold-steep** them, and they won't disturb your mash pH. See [[handling-dark-grains]].

## Adjustment Methods

| Goal | Method |
|------|--------|
| **Increase hardness** | Gypsum (CaSO₄), calcium chloride, Epsom (MgSO₄) — pick the stylistic anion you want |
| **Decrease hardness** | RO, or blend with RO/distilled |
| **Increase alkalinity** | Chalk (CaCO₃) — poorly soluble, mash only |
| **Decrease alkalinity** | Boil (temp. hardness), **phosphoric acid**, or acidulated malt; husks reduce it naturally in the mash |
| **Lower pH** | **Phosphoric acid** (most flavor-neutral); salts shift pH only *in the mash*, not in water |
| **Raise pH** (rare) | Blend with unadjusted water; KOH solution if truly needed |

## Chloride : Sulfate Ratio (British approach)

Sulfate favors hoppy beers, chloride favors malty. Starting points:

- **2:1 SO₄:Cl** — bitters · **1:2** — milds · **1:3** — stouts/porters · **even** — balanced.

Think about the **total** salt added, not just the ratio. (Esslinger suggests a 3:1 noncarbonate:carbonate hardness ratio — i.e. favor CaCl₂/CaSO₄ over carbonate.)

## Strong's RO Starting Points (per 5 gal / 19 L)

| Beer | Addition |
|------|----------|
| Malty | **1 tsp CaCl₂** |
| Hoppy | **1 tsp CaSO₄** |
| Dark (if mashing dark grains) | **2 tsp CaCO₃** |

> Both malty *and* hoppy? Use **both** CaCl₂ and CaSO₄ in a ratio matching the balance, keeping total ~1 tsp.

**Teaspoon → ppm approximations (in 5 gal):** CaSO₄ ≈ 59 ppm Ca + 141 ppm SO₄ · CaCO₃ ≈ 38 ppm Ca + 57 ppm CO₃ · CaCl₂ ≈ 61 ppm Ca + 107 ppm Cl. (Weighing is more accurate; volume is fine for ballpark.)

## Strong's Routine ("So What Do I Do?")

His tap water is bad (high alkalinity/carbonates + a softener), so he **buys RO** and builds up:

1. Test **TDS** to decide whether to measure strike-water pH.
2. **Phosphoric acid** to bring mash water to ~**pH 5.5**.
3. Add **CaSO₄ or CaCl₂** to the mash by style; **check mash pH** with a good meter.
4. Sometimes add salts to the **boil**.
5. Add dark/crystal malts at **vorlauf** or cold-steeped, so they don't affect mash pH.
6. At **kegging**, confirm final beer **pH ≤ 4.5** (adjust down with phosphoric if not — rare); tweak stylistic ions only if flavor's off (rare).

> Sierra Nevada treats **all** brewing liquor (strike, sparge, hot, cold) with phosphoric to **pH 5.5** once — easier than adjusting at multiple points, with no negative mash effect (their mash pH lands 5.1–5.3). They use gypsum + CaCl₂ in mash *and* kettle (kettle salts restore what lautering strips).

**Bottom line:** soft water turns into almost any profile, and often just **tastes better**. Even a Burton ale made with soft water (vs gypsum-loaded) may taste "better" rather than "authentic." Brewing is art *and* science — don't overemphasize the science. **Trust your palate.**
