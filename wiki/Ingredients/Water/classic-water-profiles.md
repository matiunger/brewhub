# Classic Water Profiles — A Cautionary Tale

*From Gordon Strong, "Brewing Better Beer" — Mastering Ingredients. Part of [[water-philosophy]].*

Homebrewers love to copy published profiles from famous brewing cities — Burton (English pale ale), Dublin (stout), Plzeň (Pilsener), Munich (dunkel), Dortmund (export). It's reasonable **when replicating a historical style**, but understand the data first.

## Why the Data Is Suspect

- Regional water **did** shape which styles worked where — but brewers learned to **adjust** their water (that's why bitters can be made in carbonate-rich London). So a regional profile **doesn't reliably map to a style**.
- We rarely know **what breweries actually used**: public vs treated water? changed over time? Is a *modern* municipal report any guide to historic conditions?
- Breweries draw from **multiple, variable sources** (municipal, wells, springs) that change by season. Reports may be **averaged across seasons** or **not even sampled from the brewery's supply**.
- **Many published profiles are simply wrong** — internally inconsistent ion concentrations that couldn't exist without unreasonably high pH.

## The Takeaway

Don't chase classic profiles. At most, **favor certain minerals** for certain styles (e.g. **sulfate for Burton**). What you'd actually want is the water *as used by the breweries when the style was formed* — and **nobody reliably has that**.

> Strong's order of priority: **get mash pH right first**, *then* adjust flavor ions to suit a style. That's the modern approach and far less likely to ruin your beer.
