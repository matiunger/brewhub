# Water Basics

*From Gordon Strong, "Brewing Better Beer" — Mastering Ingredients. Part of [[water-philosophy]]. Deeper dives: [[water-chemistry]], [[how-ph-affects-brewing]], [[residual-alkalinity]], [[ph-buffers]].*

The aspects that matter: **pH, hardness, alkalinity, residual alkalinity, and the flavor-ion profile** — all driven by mineral content.

## pH

- A measure of acidity (H⁺ concentration), scale 0–14, 7 neutral at 25°C. US municipal water runs **6.5–8.5**.
- **pH varies with temperature** — a room-temp sample reads ~**0.3 higher** than at mash temp; by convention pH is always **expressed at room temperature** (cool samples first, or use a meter with ATC).
- It influences enzyme activity, efficiency, protein coagulation, viscosity, foam, haze, fermentability, attenuation, color, clarity, taste, bitterness perception, and spoilage resistance.
- **Targets:** mash pH **5.1–5.5, best 5.2–5.3**; keep **runoff below pH 5.8** to avoid tannin extraction (acidify sparge water with **phosphoric acid** to 5.5–5.8). See [[preparing-to-sparge]].

## Hardness

Calcium (Ca²⁺) + magnesium (Mg²⁺). Says nothing about anions. **~50 ppm calcium is desirable**, especially in the mash.

- **Temporary hardness** drops out when boiled (calcium carbonate precipitates) — lowers available calcium.
- **Permanent hardness** (sulfates, chlorides) survives boiling.

## Alkalinity & Residual Alkalinity

- **Alkalinity** = pH buffering capacity — carbonates, bicarbonates, hydroxide. At normal supply pH it's mostly **bicarbonate**.
- **Residual alkalinity (RA)** = alkalinity left after malt phosphates react with Ca/Mg and precipitate. Useful for *comparing* city profiles, **but overemphasized in everyday brewing** — misapplying Kolbach's research drives over-adjustment (hello, Alka-Seltzer). See [[residual-alkalinity]].
- **Goal:** get mash enzymes their proper pH. If mashes land in range, **stop fussing with alkalinity/pH** — that's separate from sparge/tannin concerns. **Worry about mash pH first, tannins second.**

## The Flavor Ions

Think of Ca/Mg and carbonates **together** (they drive pH/mash chemistry); Na/Cl/SO₄ are mainly **flavor** ("stylistic") ions.

| Ion | Effect |
|-----|--------|
| **Calcium (Ca²⁺)** | Most important — enzyme co-factor, protein coagulation (break/clarity), yeast health. **≥50 ppm** in mash; up to ~200 ppm fine. |
| **Magnesium (Mg²⁺)** | Accents flavor/sourness low; astringent/bitter high. Affects pH less than calcium. |
| **Sodium (Na⁺)** | Accents flavor low; salty/sour/harsh/yeast-toxic high. |
| **Chloride (Cl⁻)** | Fullness and sweetness, accents malty styles, aids stability/clarity; harsh high. |
| **Sulfate (SO₄²⁻)** | Dryness + hop bitterness; sulfury/harsh high. **Avoid with noble hops** / continental lagers. |
| **Bicarbonate (HCO₃⁻)** | Primary alkalinity source; carbonate disassociates to bicarbonate at brewing pH. Calcium carbonate is nearly insoluble at pH ≥ 7. |
