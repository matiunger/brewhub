# Selecting Malt

*From Gordon Strong, "Brewing Better Beer" — Mastering Ingredients. Part of [[malt-overview]]. Includes Strong's "So What Do I Do?" picks.*

Choose a malt for **both** its flavor/color *and* its brewing behavior: How does it mash? Does it hit your efficiency, clarity, milling, stability? How well does the raw grain store?

## Build a Flavor Vocabulary

As you taste, place each malt: *doughy, grainy, crackery, cookie-like, bready, bread-crust, biscuity, malty-rich, toasty, roasty, burnt.* Any dark/dried fruit (raisin, plum, cherry)? How sweet, how rich, how layered? Does "roasty" mean chocolate, coffee, or something else? **Taste for yourself** — only adopt a written description once you agree with it. Then compare your notes to the **style guidelines**.

## Watch the Color Spec

- **Color = flavor.** You can't change one without the other.
- **Chocolate malt ranges wildly** — 200 °L (pale) to 500 °L. Crystal malts differ by maltster too.
- Check °L/EBC on what you actually bought, and make sure your **brewing software's grain database** uses the same color.

## Strong's Picks ("So What Do I Do?")

**Base malts — use indigenous ingredients for authentic beer.** UK, Belgian, and German malts have more character than US/Canadian.

- US/Canadian two-row → neutral or grainy profiles.
- Pale ale malt → English or Belgian (UK = more bready/biscuit).
- Pils → German or Belgian (Belgian can be slightly estery; pick German when you want *totally* clean). Pils adds sweetness and a rounder, more elegant flavor than two-row.
- **Mix base malts** (pale+Pils, two-row+Maris Otter, Vienna+Maris Otter) to dilute strong flavors, add complexity, or build a **house character**.

**Crystal malts** — crystal 40 ≠ half-20 + half-60 (color matches, flavor doesn't). Multiple crystals add depth but go **muddy if overdone**. For pure caramel-candy flavor Strong reaches for American crystal 40. Don't overlook very dark crystals (120, Special "B") and pale chocolate — 100–200 °L gives caramelized, dark-fruit, nutty notes; use as **accents**, not primary flavors.

**Specialty malts** — check two-row vs six-row (two-row = higher-quality flavor); prefer English/Belgian crystal over six-row American.

**Roasted malts** — Strong favors Carafa Special / debittered Belgian black to avoid harshness, but is careful with anything burnt in schwarzbier or black IPA.

**Smoked malt** — Weyermann rauchmalz only; **never peat-smoked** ("tastes like phenolic dirt" / "an open grave").

## Bottom Line

Develop a **workable set** of malts that covers a wide range of styles. Keep notes on storage, stability, milling, clarity, and efficiency problems. You don't need to know every malt — just make **reliable selections**.
