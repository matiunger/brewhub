# Preparing Malt — QC, Storage & Milling

*From Gordon Strong, "Brewing Better Beer" — Mastering Ingredients. Part of [[malt-overview]].*

Usually all you do is crush the malt — but check quality first, store it right, and dial in the crush.

## Quality Control

- **Inspect for insects** (weevils, beetles): flies, telltale fluffiness, or dusty webs. A few → only lower extract. **More than a few → dump it** (return if fresh; review storage if it's been a while; stop buying from a seller who won't take it back).
- **Taste the malt** — confirm the right profile, not stale or damp. Learn each malt's correct flavor *when you first get it* so you can catch problems before brew day.

## Storage

- Keep malt **dry** (insects struggle in low moisture) and **cool** (keeps them inactive).
- **Keep air out:** leave the sack's plastic liner in place, squeeze out excess air, twist closed, and tie with a rubber band or twine.

## Crushing

Goal: **break the starch into tiny pieces while keeping husks as intact as possible.** Maximum efficiency would turn endosperm to flour with husks untouched — hard on home gear, so compromise.

- Adjust the mill to crush endosperm **without pulverizing husks**. **Inspect the output after each adjustment** and keep tuning until satisfied.

## Wet Milling / Malt Conditioning (advanced)

Spraying grain lightly with warm water just before milling makes husks **pliable** so they shred less — letting you set the rollers tighter for a great crush, better runoff, and higher quality.

- Sierra Nevada conditions with strike water at **140°F (60°C)** under nitrogen, then mills.
- **Homebrew approximation:** lightly mist grain in a shallow pan (an airbrush on an air compressor works), turn once, repeat, then mill **immediately**. **Don't overwet** — soggy grain jams the rollers.
