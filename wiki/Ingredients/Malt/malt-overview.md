# Malt

*From Gordon Strong, "Brewing Better Beer" — Mastering Ingredients. Part of [[mastering-ingredients]].*

Malt is **germinated and kilned barley** (or other grain). Kilning temperature, time, and moisture set its flavor and performance. Once its starch is converted in the mash, malt is the primary sugar source in wort.

## How to Describe a Malt

Specify a malt by working through these layers — they tell you both how it tastes and how it behaves:

| Classification | Examples | Why it matters |
|----------------|----------|----------------|
| **Grain type** | two-row barley, six-row barley, wheat, oats, rye | Base behavior, protein, husk |
| **Malt type** | brewer's, Pilsner, pale ale, Vienna, Munich | The role it plays in the recipe |
| **Variety** *(optional)* | Maris Otter, Golden Promise, Harrington, Metcalfe | Like breed of dog — flagged when premium/desirable |
| **Maltster** | Crisp, Simpsons, Weyermann, Briess | Who malted it; profiles vary by maltster |
| **Origin** | US, UK, Germany, Belgium, Canada, Moravia | Terroir + style authenticity |
| **Color** | °Lovibond (°L) or EBC | How highly it was kilned |

## Actionable Tips

- **Variety is often unstated** — your malt may be a blend, or the variety simply isn't notable enough to advertise. Expect to pay more for a named variety (Maris Otter > generic pale ale malt, like Cabernet > red table wine).
- **Availability shifts** as growers change crops and new strains displace old ones (Klages was once the common two-row; now gone). **Be willing to substitute** when a favorite disappears.
- For **authentic** styles, favor malt from the **same region** as the style — see [[selecting-malt]].
