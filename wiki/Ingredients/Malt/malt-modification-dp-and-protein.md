# Modification, Diastatic Power & Protein

*From Gordon Strong, "Brewing Better Beer" — Mastering Ingredients. Part of [[malt-overview]].*

The malt analysis sheet (or maltster's website) carries three numbers that constrain your recipe and process. They won't tell you how the beer tastes, but they tell you how to mash it.

## Diastatic Power (DP) — Can It Convert Itself?

DP is the enzymatic power of the malt — its ability to convert its own and other starches. Measured in **degrees Lintner (°L)** or **Windisch-Kolbach (WK)**: `WK = (°Lintner × 3.5) − 16`.

- **35–40 °Lintner (106–124 WK)** is the threshold to **self-convert** (use up to 100% in the grist, full extract, no added enzymes).
- **Munich** ≈ 40–50 °L — self-converts, but may take longer. **Six-row** ≈ 160 °L or higher.
- **Lower DP → mash longer** (test for conversion with the [[iodine-test]]), **blend** with a higher-DP grain, or use a **[[decoction-mash]]** (wort passes through saccharification temps repeatedly, more α-amylase time).
- Swapping in a **higher-DP** malt than a recipe assumes? **Raise mash temp slightly** for similar results (and vice versa). See [[single-infusion-mash]].

## Modification — Mealy & Ready, or Not?

Modification = how fully soluble and ready-for-saccharification the endosperm starch is.

- On the sheet, **Grind Difference (% FG/CG)** is the proxy. **≤1% FG/CG = well-modified.** **1.8–2%+** is undermodified and likely wants a **protein rest** — see [[common-mash-rests]].
- **Modern reality:** most malt today is highly modified and fine for a **[[single-infusion-mash]]**. Truly undermodified malt (for decoction) is now rare — the maltster did the work brewers once did.

## Protein & Clarity

- High protein → consider a **protein rest** or use **adjuncts** to dilute it.
- Hazy beer with a given malt? Add a short rest at **131°F (55°C)** — Strong does this for continental Pils malts (Durst, Dingeman, Weyermann, Best) and for Munton's Maris Otter (oddly, Crisp Maris Otter always clears for him).
- **Malt varies lot to lot** — check the analysis sheet. But if a specific malt+maltster *always* behaves a certain way, plan your mash schedule around it.
