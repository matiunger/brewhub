# How Malt Production Shapes Character

*Via the Birra Podcast, "Maltas especiales con Luis Ramirez de BOORTMALT" (T1E15), an interview with a maltster. Complements [[characterizing-malt]] (Strong's four-category framework) and [[malt-modification-dp-and-protein]] — this page explains *why* those categories behave the way they do, from the production side.*

## Base vs. Specialty, Restated From the Maltster's Side

Base malt's job is fermentable extract: starch plus the enzymes to convert it. Specialty malt's job is organoleptic — color, aroma, flavor. The caramelizing and roasting processes that give specialty malt its character also denature its enzymes and convert part of its starch, so specialty malts yield somewhat less extract per kilo than base malt and contribute **no enzymatic power** to the mash — they depend entirely on the base malt's diastatic power to get converted. See [[malt-modification-dp-and-protein]] for how to check whether a grist has enough enzyme capacity left after adding specialty malt.

**Diastatic power is a direct consequence of modification**: the longer barley germinates before kilning, the more enzymes it generates, and the higher its diastatic power. A well-modified base malt converts easily on its own; specialty malts, having had that enzyme-building process cut short (or destroyed by heat afterward), can't.

## Two Different Chemistries, Two Different Flavor Worlds

Specialty malt color and flavor come from one of two distinct chemical processes — knowing which one a given malt uses explains why it tastes the way it does.

### Maillard Reactions (roasted/toasted malts)

A reducing sugar reacts with an amino acid — **not an enzymatic reaction**, so high heat doesn't stop it (heat actually drives it). It produces melanoidins (color) plus hundreds of aroma/flavor compounds. The exact compounds produced depend on **pH, temperature, humidity, protein type, and sugar type** — change any one variable and you get a meaningfully different flavor outcome, which is why two malts from different maltsters (or different process tweaks at the same maltster) can taste very different even starting from similar barley and similar color.

- **Munich malt's** characteristic biscuit/bread-crust aroma comes from a sulfur-derived aldehyde, which requires **high-protein barley** (more sulfur-bearing amino acids) as an input — more protein in the source barley generally means more malty flavor development. The extra protein this brings, which would be a clarity concern in a base malt used at 100%, isn't a practical problem when Munich is used as a smaller-percentage specialty addition.
- **Barley variety and growing region affect specialty malt flavor directly** — some maltsters select barley varieties and growing regions specifically for flavor character rather than extract yield or yield-per-hectare, similar to how wine grape varietals are chosen. (Example cited: Admiral Maltings, California, producing Pilsner and Munich malts with unusual red-berry/cherry notes from flavor-selected barley varieties.)
- **Toasted/roasted malt production**: either a conventional static-bed dryer (hot air from below, maxing out around 100–110°C — used for malts like Aromatic and Victory), or a rotating-drum roaster (borrowed from coffee roasting technology, reaching 150–200°C, starting from an already-dried base malt) — used for Chocolate, black, and other deeply roasted malts. The roaster's constant tumbling gives more even, homogeneous roasting than a static bed.

### Caramelization (crystal/caramel malts)

A genuinely different process: **starch/sugar + water, heated** — the same chemistry as making caramel for flan, not a Maillard reaction. Production starts from **green (undried) malt at ~40–50% moisture**, which is held at mashing temperature (67–70°C) *inside the intact grain* to convert starch to sugar internally, then heated further (150–200°C) to caramelize that sugar and lock in the crystallized result. This produces a genuinely different flavor palette than Maillard-driven roasted malts, even at matched color.

**Crystal and caramel malt are not quite the same thing, despite the interchangeable naming**, because of how each is actually produced:
- **Crystal**: made in a rotating roaster, like the toasted-malt process above — the tumbling gives a homogeneous result, so effectively 100% of the kernels share the same caramel profile.
- **Caramel**: made on a static bed (often ~2 m deep) that doesn't move — the bottom of the bed caramelizes more than the top, so the finished product is a heterogeneous mix of caramelization levels within the same bag. A "caramel" malt ends up tasting like a blend of high-caramel, Munich-like, and pale/lightly-toasted character all in one product, whereas a crystal malt of the same rated color reads as a purer, more clearly-defined caramel flavor.

**Caramel color-to-flavor mapping isn't linear or interchangeable** — reaching the same color with a different caramel grade gives a different flavor, so pick caramel grade by the flavor you want, not just by color math:
- **Caramel 30** — dulce de leche, toffee.
- **Caramel 60** — more toasted, flan-like.
- **Caramel 80–120** — raisin, plum, dark dried fruit.

A recipe built from a lot of low-color caramel malt tastes meaningfully different from one that hits the same color target with a small amount of high-color caramel malt — treat caramel grade selection as a flavor decision, not just a color-matching exercise.

## Melanoidin Malt

Described in the episode as a favorite: melanoidin malt is essentially the maximal expression of the Maillard reaction while *retaining* base-malt-like diastatic power, because production uses an extended germination (to build amino acids, sugars, and enzymes) before kilning, rather than caramelizing or roasting a specialty base. This means it can be used at much higher percentages than a typical Maillard-driven specialty malt — successfully tested up to 50–60% of a grist — while still contributing real conversion capacity to the mash. It needs high-protein source barley, same as Munich.

## Carapils / Dextrin Malt: A Skeptical Look

Carapils (a Bayermann brand name; "dextrin malt" is the generic term) is marketed for dextrins, viscosity, and foam support — but its actual contribution is disputed. It comes from an **interrupted malting process**, landing somewhere between raw barley and fully malted grain, with a light caramelization between starch and aleurone layer that's claimed to give viscosity. It should be priced below Pilsner malt, reflecting that incomplete process.

**A practical test worth running yourself**: comparisons published by well-known brewers found that a 50% Pilsner / 50% Carapils grist finishes at nearly the same final gravity as 100% Pilsner — if Carapils were genuinely contributing significant unfermentable dextrins, final gravity should come out meaningfully higher. Before locking Carapils into a recipe as a fixed ingredient, brew a side-by-side test batch at a modest 1–2% inclusion and evaluate whether you can actually detect its contribution.

**Where it might still make sense**: heavily-hopped beers where you want some malt support/complexity without competing with hop character. It's largely redundant in dark beers that already carry crystal/caramel malt — those already supply viscosity and dextrins, so stacking Carapils on top of Caramel 30 + Caramel 60 in the same recipe is usually double-dipping on the same effect rather than adding something new.

## Recipe Design: Avoid Diluted, Overlapping Specialty Malts

- **Many specialty malts at tiny percentages (e.g. 0.2% each) don't register as distinguishable flavor.** A dominant malt (e.g. 5% Black Patent) can completely mask several other specialty malts included at trace levels — a Robust Porter with 3–4 caramel malts, 2–3 roasted malts, and 0.5% oats is a real example where the oats contribute essentially nothing against that much chocolate/black/roasted barley. Prefer fewer specialty malts at meaningful percentages over many at token amounts.
- **Check for profile overlap before finalizing a grist.** Maltsters publish sensory "spider chart" profiles for their malts — lay several out and look for redundancy. If two malts in a recipe have very similar profiles, one is likely masking the other rather than adding something distinct; make sure every specialty malt in a recipe is earning its place with a differentiated contribution, and avoid stacking multiple "toasty" malts without a specific reason.
- **Evaluate any high-specialty-percentage grist for conversion capacity.** Above roughly 20–30% specialty malt (e.g. a 30% caramel-malt grist), verify the base malt's enzyme reserve can still fully convert the batch — a shortfall shows up as a higher-than-expected finishing gravity, lower alcohol, sluggish fermentation, and yeast short on available nutrients. An iodine test (see [[iodine-test]]) confirms complete conversion when specialty percentage is high.

## Evaluating Malt Before You Brew: The Malt Tea Test

Grind 25–50 g with a coffee grinder, steep in 3–4 parts hot water (50–60°C), let it rest, then evaluate the liquid by smell and taste — supplement by chewing a few unground kernels directly. This lets you compare malts side by side and get a real sense of their contribution before committing them to a recipe. (Documented on EBC and other brewing-association resources.)

## Malt Varies Lot to Lot — Track It, Don't Ignore It

Watch trends across a maltster's quality-control sheets over time, not just whether a single lot is in spec. A protein reading that drifts from 9% to 11%, while still technically in spec, can still call for mash or boil adjustments. Malt composition shifts across the growing season and between harvests — as the brewer, plan to adapt your process to that natural variability rather than assuming every bag behaves identically. As Charlie Bamforth put it: **"don't blame the maltster"** — the variability is inherent to an agricultural product, and it's the whole production chain's job to compensate so the end consumer never notices.
