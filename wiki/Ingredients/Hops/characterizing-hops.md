# Characterizing Hops

*From Gordon Strong, "Brewing Better Beer" — Mastering Ingredients. Part of [[hops-overview]].*

## Forget "Bittering vs Aroma" Varieties

The old "bittering / aroma / dual-use" split **isn't really true today** — many high-alpha hops have amazing flavor and aroma. When Strong says *bittering, flavor, aroma* hops he means the **addition's purpose**, not a fixed category for the variety. Substitute freely (and you'll *have* to during hop shortages).

## Noble Hops

Traditional low-alpha, high-aroma central-European hops: **Saaz, Hallertauer, Tettnang, Spalt**. High in **humulene**, low in **cohumulone** and harsh components. **Terroir matters** — these are only "noble" when grown in their original regions. Noble-*like* modern varieties (Sterling, Crystal, Santiam, Vanguard) suit continental lagers and any floral/spicy beer.

## Four Ways Strong Categorizes a Hop

1. **Aroma/flavor profile** (primary — see below)
2. **Region** — terroir shapes character. Hallertauer grown in the US ≠ Hallertau-grown; American Fuggles ≠ UK Fuggles. **Native versions are generally superior.** Beware similar names for different hops (*Pacific Hallertauer* is a NZ cross, not Hallertauer). **Read the package for country of origin** — don't assume from the name. For an authentic Bohemian Pilsener, US Saaz won't do.
3. **Bitterness contribution (% AA)** — drives recipe math and helps avoid excess hop mass (grassy/vegetal flavors).
4. **Character of bitterness** — clean vs harsh (personal preference). **Magnum, Warrior, Summit** = clean; **Cluster, Chinook** = coarser/harsher. **High cohumulone → harsher** bitterness.

## Aroma & Flavor Profile Vocabulary

Light hops give only the general category; intense hops let you get specific.

| Category | Notes |
|----------|-------|
| **Citrusy** | Most assoc. with American hops; grapefruit, lemon, lime, orange, tangerine; also tropical (pineapple, mango, passionfruit), stone fruit, berries. Orange marmalade in some English hops. |
| **Floral** | Noble hops & English (esp. Goldings); a rounded brightness that triggers "hops." |
| **Spicy** | Desirable in many European hops; exotic, complexity-adding (≠ yeast clove/pepper spice). |
| **Herbal** | Less common, can be stronger — mint, thyme, rosemary. |
| **Earthy** | Some English hops (esp. Fuggles); "forest after rain," not muddy. |
| **Piney** | Fresh evergreen / woody; American "Pacific Northwest" character, often with citrus. |
| **Sulfur** | Pungent, catty, onion, tomato-leaf — usually from less-desirable aroma hops or as a background note. |

> Raw hops carry a **grassy** character that can show up in beer. Many hops are complex (noble = floral + spicy; many American = citrus + pine) — weigh **all** perceptible notes, their intensity and balance, and how they'll blend with the rest of the beer.
