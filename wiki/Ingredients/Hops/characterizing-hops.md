# Characterizing Hops

*From Gordon Strong, "Brewing Better Beer" — Mastering Ingredients. Part of [[hops-overview]].*

## Forget "Bittering vs Aroma" Varieties

The old "bittering / aroma / dual-use" split **isn't really true today** — many high-alpha hops have amazing flavor and aroma. When Strong says *bittering, flavor, aroma* hops he means the **addition's purpose**, not a fixed category for the variety. Substitute freely (and you'll *have* to during hop shortages).

## Noble Hops

Traditional low-alpha, high-aroma central-European hops: **Saaz, Hallertauer, Tettnang, Spalt**. High in **humulene**, low in **cohumulone** and harsh components. **Terroir matters** — these are only "noble" when grown in their original regions. Noble-*like* modern varieties (Sterling, Crystal, Santiam, Vanguard) suit continental lagers and any floral/spicy beer.

## Four Ways Strong Categorizes a Hop

1. **Aroma/flavor profile** (primary — see below)
2. **Region** — terroir shapes character. Hallertauer grown in the US ≠ Hallertau-grown; American Fuggles ≠ UK Fuggles. **Native versions are generally superior.** Beware similar names for different hops (*Pacific Hallertauer* is a NZ cross, not Hallertauer). **Read the package for country of origin** — don't assume from the name. For an authentic Bohemian Pilsener, US Saaz won't do.
3. **Bitterness contribution (% AA)** — drives recipe math and helps avoid excess hop mass (grassy/vegetal flavors).
4. **Character of bitterness** — clean vs harsh (personal preference). **Magnum, Warrior, Summit** = clean; **Cluster, Chinook** = coarser/harsher. **High cohumulone → harsher** bitterness.

## Aroma & Flavor Profile Vocabulary

Light hops give only the general category; intense hops let you get specific.

| Category | Notes |
|----------|-------|
| **Citrusy** | Most assoc. with American hops; grapefruit, lemon, lime, orange, tangerine; also tropical (pineapple, mango, passionfruit), stone fruit, berries. Orange marmalade in some English hops. |
| **Floral** | Noble hops & English (esp. Goldings); a rounded brightness that triggers "hops." |
| **Spicy** | Desirable in many European hops; exotic, complexity-adding (≠ yeast clove/pepper spice). |
| **Herbal** | Less common, can be stronger — mint, thyme, rosemary. |
| **Earthy** | Some English hops (esp. Fuggles); "forest after rain," not muddy. |
| **Piney** | Fresh evergreen / woody; American "Pacific Northwest" character, often with citrus. |
| **Sulfur** | Pungent, catty, onion, tomato-leaf — usually from less-desirable aroma hops or as a background note. |

> Raw hops carry a **grassy** character that can show up in beer. Many hops are complex (noble = floral + spicy; many American = citrus + pine) — weigh **all** perceptible notes, their intensity and balance, and how they'll blend with the rest of the beer.

## American Hops: Primary and Secondary Profiles

*Via the Birra Podcast, "Conociendo los lúpulos para poder decidir" (T1E12).*

American aroma/flavor hops group into three big sensory families — **citrus**, **fruity**, and **resinous** (floral and spicy exist too, but most varieties fall into the first three). Within citrus there are sub-notes worth distinguishing: lime, grapefruit, mandarin, bergamot, fresh lemon, oxidized/candied lemon, zest — a fresh note reads very differently from a "stewed" one.

No hop is purely one family. Each variety has a **primary profile** (what dominates at normal dosing) and a **secondary profile** that emerges as the addition gets larger — and the addition *technique* shifts which profile shows up (see [[hop-stand-chemistry-and-technique]] and [[cold-dry-hopping-and-oxygen-control]]).

- **Cascade** (Argentine-grown vs. US-grown) — Argentine leans more grapefruit, US leans more lemon; both share a floral/resinous secondary that appears above ~5–6 g/L total.
- **Citra, Mosaic** — big tropical fruit, but with citrus and resin underneath; for fruit *without* drifting resinous/herbal/piney (e.g. for NEIPA), reach for **El Dorado, Amarillo, or Azacca** instead — more fruit, a floral edge, less resin.
- **Equanot** — tropical fruit + a spicy/resinous secondary. In dry hop (especially cold) it reads sweet melon/watermelon; in whirlpool that same spicy note shows up instead — a cheaper hop like Chinook can substitute for that whirlpool spice note.
- **Amarillo** — very high geraniol, a key biotransformation precursor; the go-to variety for early, active-fermentation dry hopping (see biotransformation timing in [[hopping]]).

**Watch secondary profiles when blending.** If every hop in a blend shares the same secondary note (e.g. Cascade + Equanot + Simcoe + Chinook all carry resin as a secondary), the beer can tip resinous even though each variety was chosen for its primary. Pushed to extremes, blends can also expose unwanted tertiary notes (onion, garlic).

**A practical evaluation method:** read the supplier's "hop profile" spec sheet, cross-check English-language forum experience, then brew a **single-hop APA** to taste it in beer — 90% Pilsner malt / 10% Munich, OG 1.055, US-05, 3 g/L in whirlpool + 3 g/L dry hop, all one variety. [Brulosophy's Hop Chronicles](https://brulosophy.com) is a useful public reference for this format. For a faster (if rougher) filter before committing a whole batch, the **ASBC cold-steep protocol** works too: grind 10 g of pellets, steep in 900 mL of 25°C water, press through a French press, and smell — it skips fermentation entirely but is good for triaging which varieties to test further.

> Terroir and handling matter as much as variety: the same variety grown in a different valley, pelletized differently, or handled differently post-harvest can taste meaningfully different. Treat any variety guide — including this one — as a starting range, not a guarantee.
