# Hops

*From Gordon Strong, "Brewing Better Beer" — Mastering Ingredients. Part of [[mastering-ingredients]]. For the practical IBU/variety/addition reference, see [[hop-guide]]; for in-the-brewhouse technique, see [[hopping]].*

Hops are the dried **strobiles (cones)** of the female *Humulus lupulus* vine. They give beer **bitterness, flavor, aroma, and antibacterial protection**. The **lupulin glands** hold bitter resins and aromatic essential oils:

- **Alpha acids** (% AA = weight of alpha acids ÷ total hop weight) isomerize when boiled into **iso-alpha acids** — the source of bitterness, measured as **IBUs**.
- **Essential oils** are a large set of volatile compounds responsible for each variety's distinctive character.

> Beta acids contribute minor bitterness — they don't isomerize well in the boil but can oxidize into a harsher bitterness during storage. Most homebrewers ignore them; avoiding oxidation makes their contribution negligible.

## Forms

| Form | Strengths | Weaknesses |
|------|-----------|------------|
| **Whole** | Best fresh aroma, easy to strain, great for dry hopping | Soaks up most wort, oxidizes easily, least stable |
| **Plug** (½ oz) | More stable than whole | Hard to break apart, not widely available |
| **Pellet** | Best utilization, stable, compact, easy to measure | Disintegrate to green sludge, hard to separate, less ideal for dry hopping |

Form drives **how you separate hops** (kettle/whirlpool setup) and affects **utilization** and **varietal availability**.
