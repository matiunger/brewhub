# Selecting Hops

*From Gordon Strong, "Brewing Better Beer" — Mastering Ingredients. Part of [[hops-overview]]. Includes Strong's "So What Do I Do?" picks.*

Strong's first inclination: use varieties **historically associated with the style's region** — but not rigidly. Noble hops work as an aroma component in almost any style, and American hops can cross into English/Belgian styles (mirroring craft experimentation).

## Bittering Hops

- Think about the **total IBU** the style needs and the **vegetal mass** it adds — easy to pick high-alpha for this.
- Strong's clean, neutral bittering hops: **Warrior, Magnum, Horizon** (Columbus/Chinook for bolder American; Challenger for stronger English; Saaz/Styrian Goldings for Belgian).
- **Cap total bittering hops at 3–4 oz (85–113 g) / 5 gal** boiled 60 min+ — more risks grassy/vegetal flavors and wort loss to absorption.
- **Low cohumulone (<25–30%)** → less harsh beer. Pair with water **low in carbonates and not too high in sulfate** to minimize harshness further.

## Flavor & Aroma Hops

Select by style. Though they add some IBUs, think in **ounces of hops** for the flavor/aroma level (per 5 gal):

| Amount | Contribution |
|--------|--------------|
| ¼ oz (7 g) | Background |
| ≤ ½ oz (14 g) | Light |
| ½–1 oz (14–28 g) | Noticeable |
| 1–2 oz (28–57 g) | Strong |
| > 2 oz (57 g) | Aggressive |

**Big-aroma varieties Strong calls out:** Cascade (floral, citrus), Centennial (bright, fruity), Amarillo (apricot, peach), Simcoe (grapefruit, pineapple, pine), Citra (grapefruit, pineapple, mango, pine), East Kent Goldings (floral, lemony), Sorachi Ace (lemony), Summit (orange, tangerine, grapefruit), Styrian Goldings (orange marmalade), plus any noble hop or noble-derived modern variety.

## Strong's Picks ("So What Do I Do?")

- Think in **flavor families**: "citrusy American," "noble," "floral English," "clean bittering." Many hops are interchangeable — don't fixate on one variety unless it's critical to the style.
- Favorite combos: **Cascade + Centennial**, **Amarillo + Simcoe**. Likes **Styrian Goldings** in both English and Belgian beers; sees Amarillo creeping into Belgian styles.
- **Use no more than four finishing varieties** — beyond that, complexity goes muddy (bittering hops with no noticeable character don't count).
- **High-alpha bittering:** Magnum (clean) or Tomahawk; Challenger for English (hard to find). Moderate bittering — often just reuses the late-addition hops so the character is compatible.
- **Noble:** Hallertauer, Tettnang; happy with American Crystal/Sterling. Czech Saaz ("fresh sea breeze") used sparingly — it jumps out.
- **Technique:** loves **first wort hopping** (huge flavor — use *less* than a normal flavor addition), prefers **knockout/whirlpool** over dry hopping (dry hops only IPAs and American barleywines). Leans on **all-late hopping** for superior flavor and lower harshness, while limiting total ounces. See [[hopping]].
- **Testing new hops:** single-hop batch with FWH + whirlpool to maximize flavor/aroma; compare beer against raw hops and a simple hop tea.

## Freshness Beats the Famous Name

*Via the Birra Podcast, T3EP01 ("Dry hopping en frío") and T1E12 ("Conociendo los lúpulos").*

Quality varies enormously between suppliers and even between lots of the *same* variety — old or poorly-held stock is common, and it shows up as flat, muted beer even at high dosing rates. A hop rated 9/10 for freshness beats a famous variety rated 6/10 every time. Don't marry a recipe to a name: if your Citra or Mosaic on hand is tired, a fresher Chinook or Cascade will make a better beer. Rebuild the recipe around whatever is actually fresh, not around the variety the style "should" use.

**A cheap, reliable sourcing test:** buy a single bag from a supplier, brew with it, and evaluate. If it performs, buy more from that source/lot. If not, try the same variety from a different supplier or lot before writing off the variety entirely.

**Evaluate every bag on opening**, using smell as your instrument:
- A sweaty-socks / Parmesan note means oxidized alpha acids — not worth using.
- If the aroma is faint but otherwise clean, steep 3 pellets in a cup of hot water ("hop tea") and smell both immediately and again once it cools ~5 minutes later — different volatiles show up as it cools, and that second read is often more telling than the first.

## Picking Varieties by Technique

Because whirlpool and dry hop extract different parts of a hop's oil profile (see [[hop-stand-chemistry-and-technique]]), the "best" variety for a beer often depends on *where* you're adding it, not just its listed profile.

- **Favor for whirlpool** (survives heat well — citrus, resin, floral come through): Centennial (lemony citrus + floral, "a super Cascade"), Bravo (candied orange + pine resin, efficient and cheap), Idaho 7, Columbus (herbal/earthy + citrus, very efficient but easy to overdo — cap around 2 g/L, and quality varies a lot by lot). Equanot and Sabro work here too, in smaller, supporting doses.
- **Favor for dry hop** (fragile tropical/fruity notes survive better without heat): Simcoe (stone fruit — apricot, peach — a standout for DH), Citra, Mosaic, Equanot (all reliable "never miss" choices for any addition), Sabro.
- **Favor for cold dry hop specifically**: Galaxy and Big Secret (Australian) bring intense fruit plus thiols with pleasant sulfurous notes — but those thiols are extremely fragile, which is why they show best at very low DH temperatures (~4°C) and why cold-side oxygen control matters so much (see [[cold-dry-hopping-and-oxygen-control]]).

## Buying and Fractionating as a Group

For homebrewers, buying a full box directly from an importer and splitting it among several people gets fresher hops at a better price than small retail packs. Vacuum-seal single-use portions sized to your batches (e.g. 100 g bags for 50 L at 2 g/L) and freeze — once a bag is opened, use it entirely rather than resealing and reopening it repeatedly. See [[storing-hops]] for handling an already-opened bag you can't finish in one brew.
