# Preparing the Yeast

*From Gordon Strong, "Brewing Better Beer" — Mastering Ingredients. Part of [[choosing-a-yeast-strain]]. See also the Fermentation section: [[starters-with-dry-yeast]], [[yeast-culturing]].*

Two cases: pitching **new** yeast (make a starter) or **reusing** yeast (wash it).

## New Yeast — Making a Starter

Pitchable packs (White Labs tube, Wyeast XL) work for average 5-gal, 1.048 wort when fresh and refrigerated — but a starter gets yeast **actively fermenting at pitch**, for a quick start and fewer off-flavors. **Bigger beers (1.060+) need a starter** to multiply yeast to the proper pitch rate.

**Strong's 1-liter starter:**

1. Boil **3 oz (85 g) extra-pale DME** + **¼ tsp yeast nutrient** in 1 quart water for **15 min**; cool in an ice bath, stirring.
2. At **72°F (22°C)**, transfer to a sanitized **Erlenmeyer flask**, add a sanitized stir bar, pitch the yeast.
3. Cover loosely with foil; run on a **stir plate** until you see circulation.

- Made **2–3 days ahead**: after ~1 day, turn off the plate, let the yeast settle, **decant the spent wort** (tastes bad, dilutes your beer), and pitch just the slurry.
- **Pitch within 10°F (6°C)** of the wort so the yeast aren't stunned.
- **Stepping up?** Repeat the process; bump DME to 4 oz for high-gravity beers but keep starter **OG ≤ ~1.060** (high gravity stresses yeast).
- **Dry yeast:** no starter — use **2 packets** for a 1.050 beer, +1 per 25 gravity points. Rehydrate each packet in 1 pint (437 mL) **104°F (40°C)** water with 1 tsp GO-FERM, 15 min, then pitch.

**A detailed hydration protocol** (via the Hernán Castellani interview): boil ~100 mL of water per sachet, cover with foil, and cool to ~30°C. Sanitize the sachet and scissors, open it next to an open flame, pour the yeast into the water, cover, and swirl gently so all the powder contacts water. Wait **exactly 15 minutes, timed**. Then step-feed with a little cold wort (~20°C) from the fermenter, repeating 2–3 times, before pitching the whole mixture — this acclimates both temperature and osmotic pressure gradually rather than shocking the yeast with a sudden change. Prepare the yeast last, once the wort is already cold in the fermenter and ready to receive it.

- **Never rehydrate with honey water** — it recreates the same osmotic stress that hydration is meant to avoid, defeating the purpose. If the wort isn't ready and the yeast has already been hydrating past 15 minutes, feed it a little cooled boiled wort instead to keep it going without stressing it.
- **Hydration itself matters for a concrete reason**: pitching dry yeast directly into wort creates an osmotic shock (a density/concentration mismatch across the cell membrane) that kills a meaningful fraction of cells outright. Hydrating in plain water first is much gentler on the cells before they ever meet wort.
- **Only attempt a starter with dry yeast if you actually have the equipment for it** (stir plate, flask, temperature control, sterile technique near a flame) — without that setup, skip the starter and just hydrate properly instead; a botched homebrew-scale starter is a real, documented source of contamination and lost batches.

## A Starter Needs Knowledge More Than Equipment

*Via the Birra Podcast, T1E09.* A starter is entirely doable in a home kitchen with just an Erlenmeyer flask and a direct flame — a stir plate helps but isn't required to get a usable result. **The skill that actually matters is aseptic technique**, not the hardware: work near an open flame, minimize how long anything is open to air, and don't set caps down on unsanitized surfaces. You can propagate up from a very small amount of starting biomass by stepping through progressively larger flasks. For gentle warmth without a stir plate, resting the flask on top of a running fridge (near the compressor) and giving it a swirl whenever you pass by works in a pinch. A useful planning habit (per Charlie Papazian): start the starter several days ahead on a fixed schedule (e.g. Thursday for a Saturday brew day) so it's simply part of the weekly routine rather than something to remember ad hoc.

## Reusing Yeast — Washing / Rinsing

Best source is a **brewpub** slurry (clean, thick, straight from the cone). Otherwise reuse from your own batch — but **first taste the beer it fermented** to confirm it's good, and only reuse yeast that **wasn't stressed** (high temps, long bulk conditioning); otherwise get fresh.

**Don't pitch onto an old yeast cake** (trub, dead yeast, hop debris). Instead:

1. After transferring the beer off, add **preboiled, chilled sterile water** to the remaining solids, shake, pour into a tall 2-L Nalgene bottle, top up partway, shake to mix.
2. Let it settle: discard the **heavy dark dregs** (fast-dropping) and the **light frothy top layer**. Keep the **creamy middle** — it has a sheen — that's what you repitch.
3. Use soon, or store cool for next-day use; maintain sanitation throughout.

> Repitched yeast can be **zinc-deficient** — add ½ tsp Wyeast Yeast Nutrient or one Servomyces capsule to the next batch. Rinse yeast during the boil so you're ready after chilling.

**A rough dosing estimate for reharvested slurry without a microscope**: about 1 kg of good, creamy (not dried-out) slurry per hectoliter of new wort. This is only a starting approximation — it depends heavily on knowing your specific strain, disciplined purging habits, and excellent cleaning/sanitation, since any bacteria present in the harvest gets carried forward and reproduces alongside the yeast in the next batch. Treat this as an advanced-homebrewer technique, not a default. *(Via the Hernán Castellani interview.)*

**Don't harvest yeast cake from a batch that was dry-hopped.** *(Via the Hernán Castellani interview.)* Hop material mixed into the trub makes it impossible to judge how much real yeast biomass is there or what fraction is actually alive — always count/estimate viable cells before reusing a harvest, and plan your brewing sequence so a strain's reharvest cycle deliberately ends on a hoppy beer rather than starting fresh dry yeast there. A practical way to run this: when a fermenter yields two harvestable yeast cakes, send one to the hoppy beer (closing out that generation) and keep the other for a non-dry-hopped beer to continue reharvesting from.

**The risk in reuse isn't only contamination.** *(Via the Birra Podcast, T2E10.)* Repeated generations under accumulated stress (poor nutrition, poor oxygenation, being pushed too many cycles) raise the odds of small mutations building up — flocculation behavior is a common casualty, and a strain that used to drop bright and clean can gradually lose that ability over mismanaged generations. This is a reason to actively support reused yeast (see [[managing-fermentation]]'s periodic-nutrient guidance), not just to watch for infection.
