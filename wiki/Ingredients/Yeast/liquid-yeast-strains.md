# Liquid Yeast: Advantages and Strain Notes

*Via the Birra Podcast, "Levaduras líquidas" (T2E11), an interview with Mariano Balbarrey. Complements [[choosing-a-yeast-strain]] and [[preparing-yeast]]; for the oxygenation technique liquid strains demand, see [[oxygenating-wort]].*

## Why Choose Liquid Over Dry

- **Precision of style match.** Liquid yeast banks preserve strains actually domesticated in the regions where specific beer styles were developed (Moravian, Belgian, various German regional strains, etc.) — a much wider and more specific selection than the dry-yeast catalog offers.
- **No drying stress.** Liquid cultures haven't been through a drying process, so they adapt to the wort they're pitched into somewhat more readily than a rehydrated dry strain.
- **The tradeoff**: liquid strains are less forgiving of process mistakes, especially oxygenation — see [[oxygenating-wort]] before switching from dry to liquid.

## Sizing the Pitch to the Actual Wort

When ordering a lab pitch (e.g. from a yeast bank), specify the **target original gravity accurately** — labs adjust the cell count they ship based on the gravity and viability you report. Reporting 1.050 when you'll actually brew to 1.075 gets you an under-sized pitch for the real wort.

**Lager pitch rate rule of thumb**: for a lager fermented around 6°C, target roughly **2 million cells/mL/°Plato or more** — the lower the fermentation temperature, the higher the cell count needed, since cold slows yeast metabolic activity and a larger population compensates.

## Temperature Sensitivity

Liquid strains are more sensitive to sudden temperature drops than dry ones. A tank drifting from, say, 18°C down to 16°C overnight can push some strains into a low-activity phase they don't reliably recover from — this risk compounds badly when it stacks with an oxygen deficit and an already-extended lag phase. Insulate fermenters against overnight ambient swings, especially during lag phase.

## Nutrient Dosing for Liquid Strains

For zinc specifically, **2 g/hL of Servomyces (or SpringFerm BR2) is a safer baseline than the 1 g/hL usually printed on the package** — measured across several breweries, 1 g/hL consistently failed to reach the 0.3 ppm zinc minimum in the finished wort. Zinc only becomes toxic above roughly 2 ppm, which would require 40–50 g/hL of nutrient — so 2–5 g/hL leaves a wide, safe margin. See [[managing-fermentation]] for the general nutrient framework this sits inside.

## Fractioning a Liquid Pack

*Via the Hernán Castellani interview.* A liquid yeast pack can be split across more than one fermentation, but **every opening is a fresh contamination and oxygen-exposure risk** for the remaining portion (oxygen exposure inside the pack also raises cell mortality before the next use) — cap fractioning at **2–3 uses per pack**, and always open it near a flame in as sanitized an environment as you can manage. This applies at small commercial scale (200–350 L batches) as much as at homebrew scale.

## Strain Profiles Discussed (Vermont Yeast Labs)

- **Bermondsey** — English-origin strain. Leaves a higher finishing gravity (more body), gives a silky mouthfeel, accentuates fruity/citrus/tropical esters, and holds up well under heavy hopping — a strong NEIPA choice, but versatile enough for Porter/Stout too (its original use case before its hop synergy was discovered). Needs **~16 ppm dissolved O₂**, roughly double what air alone can deliver — see [[oxygenating-wort]].
- **San Luis Obispo** — American strain, roughly equivalent to US-05/WLP001. Clean West Coast profile that subtly lifts malt and ester character without dominating. Flexible, easy to harvest and reuse, suited to a wide range of styles.
- **Sonora Game (Mexican Lager)** — crisp, sparkling mouthfeel, high attenuation, clean of sulfur and esters. Works well for high-drinkability lagers and also for India Pale Lager (IPL). Flexible across fermentation temperature and pressure for different flavor outcomes — needs solid carbonation to show its best side.
