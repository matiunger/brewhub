# Managing Fermentation

*From Gordon Strong, "Brewing Better Beer" — Mastering Ingredients. Part of [[choosing-a-yeast-strain]]. See also [[experiment-pitching-rate-and-oxygenation]], [[fermenting-lagers]]. For the underlying biochemistry of why these levers work, see [[yeast-metabolism-and-flavor-byproducts]].*

You don't command yeast — you **create the conditions** for them to do what they do naturally. *Think like yeast.* Strong's four keys: **sufficient oxygen, sufficient nutrients, a large healthy pitch, and proper temperature control.**

**Goals of a well-managed fermentation:** rapid onset · complete attenuation · correct flavor/aroma compounds (including reduction of intermediates) · expected flocculation and sedimentation. If yours don't look like this, investigate.

## Oxygenation

- Strong runs **pure O₂** on full for **1 minute** through an inline filter into a sintered stainless diffuser. (At Sierra Nevada he learned filtered *air* can also reach target levels.)
- No diffuser? Use a **mix-stir aerator on a drill** until foam reaches the carboy top — superior and safer than shaking.
- **Post-pitch oxygen** supports yeast growth but risks off-flavors (the Yorkshire Squares method causes the diacetyl in Samuel Smith beers) — if used, add it **before the wort is halfway to attenuation**. **Less/no oxygen → more esters.**
- **Target dissolved oxygen: 8–10 mg/L (ppm)** at the start of fermentation *only* — oxygen before or after that window (rather than at pitch) tends to hurt the beer rather than help it. High-gravity worts need more, since yeast needs extra oxygen to tolerate the added stress. *(Via the Birra Podcast, T1E11.)*
- **Dry yeast needs roughly half the oxygen** that reharvested/reused yeast does — dry yeast already ships with built-in sterol reserves that partially substitute for oxygenation.
- **Olive oil is not a working substitute for oxygen**, despite the theory that its micronutrients could support cell membrane synthesis anaerobically — commercial breweries that tried it abandoned the practice, and the added fat actively works against foam formation and retention on top of not delivering the claimed benefit (see [[the-science-of-foam]]). *(Via the Hernán Castellani interview.)*
- **A dissolved-oxygen meter is expensive** (roughly USD 1,000 — not to be confused with a TPO/dissolved-oxygen-in-finished-beer meter measuring parts-per-billion, which runs closer to USD 20,000) but you only need one reference measurement to calibrate your own system (e.g. "1 min/hL at a 2 L/min flow rate" as a baseline), then adjust ±10–20% by style/gravity from there. Splitting the cost of one meter across several homebrewers is a practical way to get that one calibration point. Because so many variables affect oxygen pickup (gravity, flow rate, diffuser stone, hose length, tubing diameter, temperature), another brewery's numbers won't transfer directly to your system — calibrate on your own fermenter.

## Nutrients

- A proper mash with limited adjuncts usually supplies enough — but **commercial yeast nutrient is cheap insurance** against sluggish fermentation. Strong uses **½ tsp Wyeast nutrient per 5 gal**, added near the end of the boil.
- The one mineral deficient in barley is **zinc** — make sure your nutrient contains it. *(Via the Birra Podcast, T1E11.)* A 100%-malt wort is short only on zinc: yeast needs 0.25–0.5 ppm, and unamended wort typically supplies just 0.1–0.3 ppm — top up with roughly 0.2–0.3 ppm of added zinc (≈0.13–0.15 g/hL of zinc sulfate heptahydrate, adjusted for the salt's actual composition). If dosing a packaged nutrient blend instead, check the label dose against this target — some lose enough zinc in the whirlpool/plate-chiller pass that the package-recommended amount undershoots it, and need roughly double the labeled rate to land in range at the fermenter.
- **Boost nutrient dose for complex worts**: worts with adjuncts (dextrose, honey, candy sugar), added enzymes, or high gravity need more — those fermentables don't bring their own nutrients along, and under-nourished yeast in that situation stalls and harvests poorly for future generations. Applies to NEIPA, Brut IPA (enzyme-driven), Imperial Stout (>1.100 OG), and any adjunct-heavy recipe.
- **Timing**: for a standard wort (<1.060 OG, 100% malt), adding nutrient in the boil is sufficient. For complex worts, add (at least part of) the nutrient cold, at the fermenter — some nutrient vitamins are heat-sensitive and degrade in a boil addition. Cold additions raise contamination risk, so sanitize carefully. A useful rule of thumb: an already-opened nutrient package should go in the boil (to sterilize it); a sealed, single-use sachet can be handled and added at the fermenter like pitching yeast.
- **If you plan to reharvest yeast, add nutrient (and oxygenate) even on the first generation** — even with dry yeast, and even though the manufacturer says it's unnecessary for a single fermentation. It produces healthier yeast for the generations you'll reuse it for.
- **Magnesium is a genuinely disputed topic**: malt alone supplies 200–400 mg/L, enough for normal fermentations. For high-gravity, adjunct-heavy worts, some brewers add 30–40 ppm magnesium to help yeast tolerate osmotic and alcohol stress, but the supporting evidence is inconclusive — treat it as an experimental lever, not a default.
- **Why 100%-malt wort is usually nutritionally complete** (*via the Birra Podcast, T2E10*): malt-derived wort naturally carries free amino nitrogen (FAN — the essential amino acids yeast can't synthesize itself) and the other basics yeast needs — unlike wine musts (which need added nutrients) or straight honey musts for mead (which lack nearly everything). Zinc remains the one mineral malt doesn't supply enough of, especially for reused yeast (see above) — don't try to formulate a zinc supplement yourself, since the toxic threshold is close enough above the target that it needs lab-grade precision; use a commercial nutrient blend instead.
- **But "100% malt is enough" only holds at the correct pitch rate.** Adequate nutrition in the wort doesn't rescue an under-pitched fermentation — e.g. a single 11 g dry yeast sachet in 150 L of wort is a nutrition-irrelevant problem; the yeast will ferment, but under heavy stress regardless of how well-fed the wort is. Symptoms of under-pitching specifically: under-attenuation, a spike in higher/fusel alcohols, ester-forward "stressed" flavor, and possible residual diacetyl/acetaldehyde — and the yeast harvested afterward will carry poor viability into the next batch.
- **Adjuncts dilute nutrient density, not just fermentable sugar.** Oats, dextrose, and maltodextrin bring fermentable extract without malt's nutrient load — a high-adjunct, high-gravity wort (example cited: a 1.105 triple NEIPA with 20% oats plus dextrose and maltodextrin) is nutritionally thin relative to how much yeast growth it demands, and needs nutrient support scaled to that gap, not just to gravity alone.
- **When reharvesting yeast repeatedly, add a FAN-containing nutrient periodically even on an otherwise simple, all-malt wort** — roughly 2–3 g/hL every 4 generations is enough to matter. Example: a W-34/70 lager strain reharvested without supplementation showed progressively longer fermentation curves and residual sulfur character by generation 6–7; with nutrient added every 4th generation, the same strain ran cleanly out to generation 32. The visible payoff is a shorter lag phase, less sulfur carryover, and faster time to final gravity.
- **What changes when a nutrient-deficient wort gets the nutrients it needs**: shorter lag phase, more biomass, a smoother exponential growth curve (no plateaus), a forced diacetyl test (see below) turning negative much sooner, and better vitality/viability in the yeast harvested afterward.

## The Fermentation Health Triangle

*Via the Birra Podcast, T1E11 — interview with Clara Bruzone and Julieta Burini.*

Three interacting legs hold up a healthy fermentation: **pitch (quantity and quality), nutrients, and oxygen.** They're not independent — pulling on one changes what the other two need:

- Slightly over-pitching reduces the oxygen requirement.
- Under-pitching raises both the nutrient and oxygen requirements.
- If you don't oxygenate at all (some homebrewers skip it), don't compound that by under-pitching too — especially at high gravity. An unoxygenated, under-pitched high-gravity beer (the example given: a 1.090 barleywine) is close to a guaranteed defective fermentation.
- Study each strain's own requirements — pitch rate and optimal fermentation temperature aren't universal, and some strains simply want a higher pitch rate than others.
- **Results don't scale linearly.** A trial at 500 mL doesn't predict what happens at 30 L, and 30 L doesn't predict 2000 L — small-scale trials are a useful reference point, but real process knowledge comes from experience at your actual production scale.

## Purging Trub and Dead Yeast

*Via the Hernán Castellani interview.* Whatever your fermenter — carboy, cylindro-conical, food-grade plastic drum — **it needs to be purgeable**, ideally via a side-mounted valve near the bottom. This matters more than the vessel's looks: some aesthetically nice glass fermenters are poor performers here specifically because they don't allow a good purge.

- **Why it matters**: dead yeast and settled trub release off-flavor compounds the longer they sit — sulfur (rotten egg, sewage), musty/fungal notes, and autolysis character are the typical symptoms of skipping this step.
- **First purge at 24–48 hours post-pitch**, to clear "cold break" (lipid-rich material that coagulates as the wort cools from boiling to fermentation temperature, which was soluble while hot) along with any yeast that died on pitching.
- **Purge through a thin hose (~3/8"), at least a meter long, rather than straight out an open valve.** A wide-open valve lets flow channel — trub gets pulled along the sides while clear liquid runs down the middle — while a thin hose creates a gradual pressure drop that pulls the settled layer down evenly. If you see channeling (clear liquid running alongside visible trub), stop and resume the purge later rather than continuing to force it.
- **If you're not harvesting yeast for reuse**, small daily micro-purges (a little each day) keep the fermenter free of dead sediment without needing a big single purge event — continue until the purge runs clear.

## Tracking and Verifying a Fermentation

*Via the Birra Podcast, T1E11.*

- **Log gravity at least once a day, ideally twice**, alongside temperature and pH, to build an actual fermentation curve — not just an OG/FG pair. A curve lets you compare a current batch against past ones and catch a deviation while it's still fixable.
- **Normal lag time is 12–18 hours** (strain-dependent). Past ~24 hours without visible activity, treat it as a contamination risk, not just a slow start — unfermented wort sitting without active yeast is a favorable growth medium for competing bacteria, which generally outgrow yeast in that window.
- **Don't fully trust a dry yeast packet's stated viability** — transport and storage conditions between the factory and your hands can degrade it, and quality varies lot to lot. Under-pitching from an unverified packet (whether from ignorance or to save cost) affects both this fermentation and the health of any future generations harvested from it. This isn't theoretical: direct cell counts on rehydrated commercial dry yeast (per the Hernán Castellani interview) have found viability as low as ~40% — meaning a nominal 0.5 g/L dose was functionally closer to 0.2 g/L of *live* cells. **A conservative default that absorbs this uncertainty**: at least 1 g/L of dry yeast for standard-gravity beers, 1.5+ g/L above ~1.070 OG — deliberately erring high rather than trusting the package rate at face value.
- **Forced wort test** (equipment/sanitation check): incubate sterile wort in a sterile container at a temperature that favors microbial growth, without pitching any yeast. Any fermentative activity that shows up confirms your equipment/sanitation isn't clean. Doesn't require a lab — just careful sterile technique to avoid a false positive from the test itself.
- **Forced diacetyl test** (packaging readiness check): pull a sample from the fermenter, hold it warm, and check for a buttery diacetyl aroma. If it's present, extend the warm conditioning/diacetyl rest before packaging — this is what catches a "green" beer before it develops butter character in the package. See [[diy-quality-control-tests]] for the full protocol and more forced tests (attenuation, aging, sanitation).
- **A fast, practical vitality check**: track the pH drop over the first ~18 hours post-pitch (at least 3 readings in that window) — a healthy, vital pitch drives pH down promptly; a sluggish drop is an early warning sign worth catching before gravity readings would show a problem. *(Via the Birra Podcast, T2E10.)*
- **Taste and smell constantly, not just the finished beer** — evaluate raw ingredients and in-process wort/beer too. Your senses are a cheap, fast instrument; calibrate them by tasting systematically, and use them alongside (not instead of) instrument readings.

## Pitching

- Prep the wort first: nutrients added, gravity on target, trub/settling handled, oxygenated, at pitch temperature. Yeast **at or slightly below** wort temp.
- Strong **doesn't fret pitch rate** (always fresh yeast + a starter). Two deliberate manipulations: **underpitch → more esters** (some do this for wheat beers; he dislikes the slow start); **overpitch in hoppy beer → lower bitterness** (yeast take up some bitterness).

## A Simple Dry-Yeast Dosing Default

*Via the Birra Podcast, T1E09.* For beers under 1.060 OG, **1 g of dry yeast per liter of wort** is a conservative dose that covers biomass needs without extra fuss — it's higher than the strict minimum, but at that rate you don't also need to add dissolved oxygen or supplemental nutrients (Servomyces/SpringFerm/zinc), since a healthy dry-yeast pitch at that rate already brings what it needs. Going leaner works too, but then those extra considerations (oxygenation, nutrients) become mandatory rather than optional. **When genuinely unsure, lean toward a slight over-pitch rather than under-pitch** — the downside of a little too much yeast is much smaller than the downside of too little (see under-pitching's symptoms above). Beginners in particular should start fresh yeast each batch rather than reusing/reharvesting until they have more experience managing that process.

## Don't Let Fermentation Crash Cold Too Early

*Via the Birra Podcast, T1E09 and the Hernán Castellani interview.* When fermentation visibly slows (airlock/blow-off activity dropping off), **raise the setpoint by about 2°C** rather than letting the vessel coast down passively — this keeps fermentation's own exothermic heat carrying the temperature slightly upward through the tail end, instead of losing warm conditioning time to a premature, passive temperature drop (a particular risk brewing through a cold season/climate). This directly supports the diacetyl/byproduct cleanup work described above.

**A concrete version of this curve**: hold tight, precise temperature control through roughly the first 50% of fermentable extract (e.g. US-05 held at 19.5°C), then once about 10–25 gravity points remain, release the controller setpoint up to ~23°C — the beer itself typically only drifts up 2–3°C from its own exothermic heat once uncontrolled, landing it comfortably above 21–22°C for the finish with most strains. Example for an American IPA (1.060 → 1.010): control tightly from 1.060 down to about 1.035, then release. **Release based on observed activity, not gravity alone** — if fermentation visibly stalls (bubbling stops) before your gravity-based release point, especially in a cold ambient environment where outside cold can outpace the fermentation's own heat output, release the controller anyway. Know your specific fermenter and strain well enough to judge this by eye.

**A practical diacetyl-rest schedule for a weekend brewing routine**: hold the beer at or slightly above fermentation temperature for **2–3 days** after reaching final gravity, or — as a more general rule — **at least half of however many days primary fermentation took** (a 4-day fermentation gets a minimum 2 more warm days) — suspended yeast degrades diacetyl and other cleanup compounds far faster than settled, cold yeast does. Extend this further if the beer shows noticeable sulfur (not very soluble, and clears faster warm) or diacetyl. For many home schedules (brew day on a weekend), roughly 4 days of primary fermentation plus a few days of warm conditioning lines up conveniently with a weekly rhythm, giving a natural, repeatable cadence without needing to babysit the calendar. Chilling a beer down while it still shows active blow-off is treated as a serious process mistake, not a minor shortcut.

**Temperature controller precision matters more than it looks like on a spec sheet.** An STC-1000-class controller with roughly 0.3°C hysteresis holds a tight setpoint (e.g. 19.5–19.6°C) far better than a wider-hysteresis controller (~1°C) at similar cost — with wide hysteresis, the cooling unit stays on longer once triggered, and thermal inertia can carry the beer several degrees below the actual setpoint (set 19°C, coast down to 17°C) before the system reacts. This matters most right at the end of fermentation, when the yeast's own heat output has dropped and there's less exothermic push fighting back against that overshoot — finishing fermentation below ~17°C is a common, avoidable source of residual diacetyl and acetaldehyde traced directly to controller hysteresis rather than anything else in the process.

**No dedicated temperature control at all?** Kveik strains ferment cleanly across a wide 18–38°C range, which makes them a practical choice when you can't hold a tight setpoint — cool the wort to somewhere in the 25–30°C range and pitch without the same precision other strains demand. Temperature still shapes the final profile with Kveik (28°C and 35°C won't taste identical), so it's not a total substitute for temperature control, but it dramatically de-risks fermenting through, say, a hot Southern Hemisphere summer where a strain like S-04 would almost certainly produce diacetyl and fusel alcohols without cooling.

## Temperature Control

- **Measure the beer, not the room** — infrared thermometer or a carboy cap with a thermowell.
- Fast chill to pitch temp (ice the chiller feed or recirculation loop) — a fast chill gives a **better break**.
- **Cooling a fermenter:** water bath with 2-L ice bottles (large thermal mass, change ice 2×/day); a fan or wet T-shirt for a smaller drop; Brew Belt or warmer room to raise it.
- **Strong's default: start cool and let it rise** — better attenuation and diacetyl reduction. Constraining temperature can stall some strains (especially Belgian), so start cool *and* pitch large, active, healthy yeast.

## Fermenter Geometry (frontier topic)

Poorly understood, but **shallower fermenters seem to generate more esters** (possibly via lower peak temperature, or physical pressure on the yeast). Strong explores this with Belgian styles — eyeing large food-grade restaurant pans — to understand how Belgians ferment so warm with good character.
