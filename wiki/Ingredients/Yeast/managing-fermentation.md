# Managing Fermentation

*From Gordon Strong, "Brewing Better Beer" — Mastering Ingredients. Part of [[choosing-a-yeast-strain]]. See also [[experiment-pitching-rate-and-oxygenation]], [[fermenting-lagers]].*

You don't command yeast — you **create the conditions** for them to do what they do naturally. *Think like yeast.* Strong's four keys: **sufficient oxygen, sufficient nutrients, a large healthy pitch, and proper temperature control.**

**Goals of a well-managed fermentation:** rapid onset · complete attenuation · correct flavor/aroma compounds (including reduction of intermediates) · expected flocculation and sedimentation. If yours don't look like this, investigate.

## Oxygenation

- Strong runs **pure O₂** on full for **1 minute** through an inline filter into a sintered stainless diffuser. (At Sierra Nevada he learned filtered *air* can also reach target levels.)
- No diffuser? Use a **mix-stir aerator on a drill** until foam reaches the carboy top — superior and safer than shaking.
- **Post-pitch oxygen** supports yeast growth but risks off-flavors (the Yorkshire Squares method causes the diacetyl in Samuel Smith beers) — if used, add it **before the wort is halfway to attenuation**. **Less/no oxygen → more esters.**

## Nutrients

- A proper mash with limited adjuncts usually supplies enough — but **commercial yeast nutrient is cheap insurance** against sluggish fermentation. Strong uses **½ tsp Wyeast nutrient per 5 gal**, added near the end of the boil.
- The one mineral deficient in barley is **zinc** — make sure your nutrient contains it.

## Pitching

- Prep the wort first: nutrients added, gravity on target, trub/settling handled, oxygenated, at pitch temperature. Yeast **at or slightly below** wort temp.
- Strong **doesn't fret pitch rate** (always fresh yeast + a starter). Two deliberate manipulations: **underpitch → more esters** (some do this for wheat beers; he dislikes the slow start); **overpitch in hoppy beer → lower bitterness** (yeast take up some bitterness).

## Temperature Control

- **Measure the beer, not the room** — infrared thermometer or a carboy cap with a thermowell.
- Fast chill to pitch temp (ice the chiller feed or recirculation loop) — a fast chill gives a **better break**.
- **Cooling a fermenter:** water bath with 2-L ice bottles (large thermal mass, change ice 2×/day); a fan or wet T-shirt for a smaller drop; Brew Belt or warmer room to raise it.
- **Strong's default: start cool and let it rise** — better attenuation and diacetyl reduction. Constraining temperature can stall some strains (especially Belgian), so start cool *and* pitch large, active, healthy yeast.

## Fermenter Geometry (frontier topic)

Poorly understood, but **shallower fermenters seem to generate more esters** (possibly via lower peak temperature, or physical pressure on the yeast). Strong explores this with Belgian styles — eyeing large food-grade restaurant pans — to understand how Belgians ferment so warm with good character.
