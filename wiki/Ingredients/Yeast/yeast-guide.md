# Yeast Guide

Yeast is responsible for fermentation — converting sugars into alcohol, CO₂, and flavor compounds.

## Yeast Types

| Type | Fermentation Temp | Character |
|------|-------------------|-----------|
| Ale (top-fermenting) | 18–24°C | Fruity esters, faster fermentation |
| Lager (bottom-fermenting) | 8–14°C | Clean, crisp, lagering required |
| Kveik | 25–40°C | Fruity, very fast, tolerates high temps |
| Saison / Farmhouse | 20–30°C | Spicy, fruity, highly attenuating |
| Wheat | 18–24°C | Banana (isoamyl acetate), clove (4-vinyl guaiacol) |
| Wild / Brett | Variable | Funky, earthy, can be secondary |

## Attenuation

Attenuation is the percentage of fermentable sugars consumed by the yeast.

```
Apparent Attenuation = (OG - FG) / (OG - 1.000) × 100%
```

- Low attenuation (60–70%): fuller body, sweeter finish
- High attenuation (75–85%+): dry, thin body

## Pitching Rate

Under-pitching stresses the yeast and can cause off-flavors (fusel alcohols, excessive esters). General guidelines:

- **Ales**: 0.75 million cells/mL/°Plato
- **Lagers**: 1.5 million cells/mL/°Plato

A standard liquid yeast pack contains ~100 billion cells. Use a starter for high-gravity beers.

## Dry vs Liquid Yeast

- **Dry yeast** — longer shelf life, easy to store, often requires rehydration
- **Liquid yeast** — more variety, fresher, requires refrigeration and has shorter shelf life

## Temperature Control

Fermentation temperature affects ester and fusel production:

- Fermenting too warm → more esters and fusel alcohols (hot, boozy character)
- Fermenting too cold → sluggish fermentation, possible stall

Most ale yeasts benefit from a diacetyl rest at the end: raise temp to ~20°C for 24–48h before crashing.

## Flocculation

- **High** — drops clear quickly, may leave residual sweetness
- **Low** — stays in suspension longer, better attenuation, hazier beer
