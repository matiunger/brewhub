# Choosing a Yeast Strain

*From Gordon Strong, "Brewing Better Beer" — Mastering Ingredients. Part of [[mastering-ingredients]]. For the practical reference, see [[yeast-guide]].*

Brewer's yeast is a single-celled fungus that ferments wort sugar into alcohol, CO₂, and flavor compounds. Two main species: ***Saccharomyces cerevisiae*** (ale) and ***S. pastorianus*** (lager); **Brettanomyces** is the most notable other genus. Brewing strains aren't distinct in formal taxonomy — they're "breeds": same species, different traits. *"Brewers make wort, but yeast make beer."*

Yeast strain is **one of the most important choices a brewer makes** — it controls much of the final profile. Strong selects **primarily on flavor**, secondarily on off-flavor potential, flocculation, temperature range, alcohol tolerance, attenuation, and stability.

## Why Strong Down-weights Most Secondary Criteria

- **Alcohol tolerance / temperature range** — he ferments in normal ranges and avoids incompatible seasons; rather than chase tolerance, just **pitch more healthy yeast with enough nutrients and oxygen**.
- **Attenuation** — controlled mostly through **wort fermentability**, not strain. A sufficient, healthy pitch ferments most worts completely. (Dry styles tend to get a high-attenuating strain anyway, by style match.)
- **Flocculation** — minor (finings exist), but pick **powdery** for high attenuation or long lagering, **highly flocculent** for cask ale or naturally bright beer.
- **Stability** — affects repitching; he rarely repitches more than 4–5 generations, so it's seldom an issue.

That leaves **flavor** as the main focus.

## A Method for Picking on Flavor

1. **Match species to style** (ale↔ale, lager↔lager), then look at strains from **breweries/styles of the same country**. For a beloved commercial example, try **that brewery's strain** — e.g. Fuller's (WY1968), Sierra Nevada (WY1056), Ayinger (WLP833).
2. **Envision the profile:** malty or hoppy? sweet or dry? clean/neutral or with yeast character (esters, phenols, higher alcohols)? Which fruits/spices, and how intense?
3. **Trust your own tasting notes** over yeast-company descriptions (too broad). Source-brewery matching is a better guide.
4. **Screen for show-stoppers** — avoid strains known for excess sulfur, diacetyl, or finickiness/high needs (e.g. Ringwood's nutrient/oxygen demand).

> A strain's profile depends heavily on **fermentation temperature** — a big temperature change can significantly shift it. Going outside the recommended range usually means off-flavors. Learn favorites in detail by **splitting batches**, keeping a **control** fermented a known-good way, and trying strains across styles.
