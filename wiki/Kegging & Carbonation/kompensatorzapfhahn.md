# Kompensatorzapfhahn

*From German brewing and more*

A **Kompensatorzapfhahn** is a beer tap that allows for adjusting the pressure drop that happens inside the tap. Normally draft systems need to be statically balanced such that the pressure drop inside the beer line and the tap match the serving pressure. If that is not the case, a sudden pressure drop may happen at the tap, the CO₂ bound to the beer will suddenly be released and the beer foams up. With a Kompensatorzapfhahn (compensator tap) a built-in pressure drop can be adjusted to match the needed pressure drop, thus removing the need to balance the draft system through the length and diameter of the beer line.

## How It Works

![Photo of a Kompensatorzapfhahn](../brewing/images/kompensatorzapfhahn/fig-000.png)

*Figure 1 — Photo of a Kompensatorzapfhahn with flow rate adjustment knob, shank and beer line connection*

The mechanism works through a stainless steel **restrictor cone** that can be pushed into a cone-shaped opening via a lever movable from the outside. The further the cone is pushed in, the narrower the gap that the beer has to flow through — and as a result, the higher the pressure drop will be over that distance.

> Because the pressure drop is not sudden but gradual over a longer distance, the CO₂ will not suddenly escape from the beer.

After that pressure drop there is a valve that allows the beer to flow through the tap into the glass.

![Cross-section diagram of a Kompensatorzapfhahn](../brewing/images/kompensatorzapfhahn/fig-001.png)

*Figure 3 — Cross-cut diagram through a Kompensatorzapfhahn*

![Parts of the tap](../brewing/images/kompensatorzapfhahn/fig-002.png)

*Figure 4 — The parts of the tap. Note the restrictor cone which fits inside a cone-shaped space inside the shank. The adjustment knob has a nub that fits into the circular depression of the restrictor cone, allowing the cone to be moved forward and back to control the opening that the beer can flow through.*

A large pressure drop — as is necessary for short beer lines found in a keg-o-rator — requires a fairly strong restriction of the flow rate, which results in a slower overall beer flow rate. Nonetheless, the adjustable flow rate also allows for serving beer at different pressures without having to design the draft system as a compromise between different serving pressures.

## The Schaumtaste (Foam Button)

Another interesting feature is the **Schaumtaste** (foam button). It is not actually a button on the tap but a feature that is activated when the tap handle is pressed backward. In that position the beer is forced through a small opening which causes it to foam up, and the foam is directed into the glass.

While this feature is not a necessity — a good *Wirt* (host) should be able to produce a nice head on a beer without it — it is a nifty invention. Compared to the foam produced while dispensing beer in normal mode, this foam is much creamier and comprised of much smaller bubbles.

---

## Availability

These taps are widely used in Germany but largely unknown in America. They can be found at specialty homebrew retailers that carry European draft equipment.
