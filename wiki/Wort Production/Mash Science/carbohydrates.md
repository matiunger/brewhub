# Carbohydrates

*From German brewing and more — braukaiser.com*

Carbohydrates are organic molecules containing carbon, oxygen, and hydrogen — the most abundant organic compounds in nature. They serve as energy sources, storage forms, and structural components for plants and some animals. They have the basic formula (CH₂O)ₙ — hence the name "carbohydrate" (hydrate of carbon). Important carbohydrates in brewing include simple sugars (glucose, fructose), complex sugars (maltose, sucrose, maltotriose), dextrins, and starches. β-glucans, pectins, and gums are also carbohydrates with brewing relevance.

## Monosaccharides

**Monosaccharides** are the simplest carbohydrates with the basic formula CₙH₂ₙOₙ — the building blocks of more complex carbohydrates. The monosaccharides important to brewing (glucose, galactose, fructose) are **hexoses** containing 6 carbon atoms. They differ in their carbonyl group or the orientation of OH and H groups along the carbon chain:

- **Aldose sugars** (glucose, galactose): the carbonyl group is an aldehyde group
- **Ketose sugars** (fructose): the carbonyl group is a ketone group

Monosaccharides may exist in open-chain or ring configurations. For monosaccharides with more than five carbons, less than 1% of molecules are in the open-chain form — the rest are in ring configuration, formed by the oxygen atom from the carbonyl group binding to another carbon of the chain.

By convention, carbon atoms are numbered such that the highest-numbered carbon is farthest from the carbonyl group.

### Glucose

**Glucose** is by far the most important monosaccharide in brewing — the building block for starch and the primary product of mashing. When the oxygen of the aldehyde group binds between carbon 1 and 5, the molecule forms a ring (glucopyranose). Depending on the orientation of the OH group on carbon 1, the molecule is in the **α or β configuration**.

This difference becomes permanent when glucose binds to other molecules — it determines whether the compound can be converted by mash and yeast enzymes. As a free molecule, both configurations coexist in equilibrium, interconverting via the open-chain form.

![Three configurations of glucose](../brewing/images/carbohydrates/fig-000.png)

*Figure 1 — Three configurations of glucose. (1) Open-chain form. (2) α-glucopyranose. (3) β-glucopyranose. Shown in both Fisher projection (better for showing the oxygen bridge between carbons 1 and 4) and Haworth projection.*

### Fructose

**Fructose** is also a hexose but a **ketose** sugar (ketone carbonyl group at carbon 2). The ring formed by the oxygen of that group binding to carbon 5 has a pentagon shape in the Haworth projection.

Fructose has no importance to mashing — present in malt only in very small amounts. It enters beer in substantial amounts through fruit juices, sucrose, or invert sugar additions.

![Fructose configurations](../brewing/images/carbohydrates/fig-001.png)

*Figure 2 — Fructose in open-chain form (1), α-D-fructofuranose (2), and β-D-fructofuranose (3).*

### Galactose

**Galactose** has no importance to mashing — it is neither present in malt nor produced in wort. Its only brewing relevance is through **lactose** (milk sugar), used in milk stouts. Like glucose it is an aldose sugar, differing only in the orientation of the OH group on carbon 4.

![Galactose](../brewing/images/carbohydrates/fig-002.png)

*Figure 3 — Galactose is very similar to glucose, differing only in the orientation of the hydroxyl (OH) group on carbon 4.*

## Disaccharides

### The glycosidic bond

A **glycosidic bond** forms when the carbonyl group of one monosaccharide reacts with a hydroxyl (OH) group of another molecule, eliminating water. It is the "glue" holding monosaccharides together in more complex carbohydrates.

When a glycosidic bond forms, the molecule with the involved carbonyl group locks into the α or β configuration permanently. Enzymes can distinguish between these bond types:
- **α links** — hold together fermentable carbohydrates (disaccharides, trisaccharides) or carbohydrates convertible in mashing (dextrins, starches)
- **β links** — hold together structural malt components (cellulose, β-glucans), which are not fermentable

By convention, the carbon atoms involved are listed when specifying the bond type. An **α(1→4) glycosidic bond** is between carbon 1 (carbonyl) of one molecule and carbon 4 of the other.

![Two glucose molecules forming an α(1→4) glycosidic bond](../brewing/images/carbohydrates/fig-003.png)

*Figure 4 — Two glucose molecules linking with an α(1→4) glycosidic bond, liberating one water molecule per link. Because only one glucose's carbonyl group is involved, the resulting disaccharide (maltose) has one reducing and one non-reducing end.*

### Reducing vs. non-reducing sugars

A sugar with a free carbonyl (aldehyde or ketone) group is a **reducing sugar**. All monosaccharides are reducing sugars. Reducing sugars can participate in **Maillard reactions** (browning) with amino acids; non-reducing sugars cannot. Both types can caramelize.

In mashing it is important to distinguish the **reducing** and **non-reducing ends** of a glucose chain — beta amylase can only work on the non-reducing end.

- **Sucrose**: non-reducing (both carbonyl groups are involved in the α(1→2) link)
- **Maltose**, **lactose**: reducing sugars

### Maltose and cellobiose

Both maltose and cellobiose consist of two linked glucose molecules, but the type of link is critical:

| Sugar | Link | Fermentable? |
|-------|------|-------------|
| **Maltose** | α(1→4) | Yes — fermented by yeast |
| **Cellobiose** | β(1→4) | No |

**Maltose** is the most abundant sugar in wort, created by enzymatic hydrolysis of starch. **Cellobiose** is the building block of cellulose (β-glucans) and is relevant primarily to lauter problems from excessive β-glucan content.

![Maltose and cellobiose](../brewing/images/carbohydrates/fig-004.png)

*Figure 5 — Both maltose and cellobiose are composed of two glucose molecules. In maltose the glucose molecules are in the α configuration with an α(1→4) link; in cellobiose they are in the β configuration with a β(1→4) link. The result is that maltose can be metabolized by yeast while cellobiose cannot.*

### Sucrose

**Sucrose** (white/table sugar) is a disaccharide of glucose and fructose connected with an α(1→2) bond. It is present in malt in small amounts (~5% of carbohydrates), survives mashing largely intact, and may be added as a kettle adjunct or priming sugar. As a **non-reducing sugar** it does not take part in Maillard reactions.

**Invert sugar** is an equal mix of glucose and fructose made from sucrose by breaking the glycosidic bond via the enzyme invertase or by applying heat in an acidic environment.

![Sucrose](../brewing/images/carbohydrates/fig-005.png)

*Figure 6 — Sucrose is formed by an α(1→2) link between glucose and fructose. Both carbonyl groups are involved in the bond, making sucrose a non-reducing sugar.*

### Lactose

**Lactose** (milk sugar) cannot be metabolized by brewing yeast and is used in some styles to add sweetness. It is neither present in malt nor a result of mashing — it must be added in the kettle. Lactose consists of galactose and glucose linked with a β(1→4) bond.

![Lactose](../brewing/images/carbohydrates/fig-006.png)

*Figure 7 — Lactose: galactose linked to glucose with a β(1→4) glycosidic bond.*

### Raffinose and melibiose

Raffinose and melibiose are frequently mentioned in connection with the genetic difference between **ale yeast** (*S. cerevisiae*) and **lager yeast** (*S. pastorianus*). Both sugars are absent from or present only in trace amounts in brewing wort, but are biochemically significant.

- **Melibiose**: a disaccharide of galactose + glucose linked with a β(1→6) bond
- **Raffinose**: a trisaccharide — melibiose with a fructose molecule attached via an α(1→2) bond

Both ale and lager yeasts have **invertase** (between cell wall and membrane), which breaks the α(1→2) bond in raffinose to yield melibiose and fructose. The fructose is metabolized by both. Only lager yeasts produce **α-galactosidase**, which breaks melibiose into fermentable galactose and glucose.

> So when it is said that ale yeast can ferment "one third of raffinose," it means one third of **each** raffinose molecule (the fructose portion only), not one third of the total number of raffinose molecules.

![Raffinose, melibiose, and fructose](../brewing/images/carbohydrates/fig-007.png)

*Figure 8 — (1) Raffinose (galactose, glucose, fructose). Invertase splits the α(1→2) bond to create (2) melibiose and (3) fructose. Melibiose can only be fermented by lager yeasts.*

## Oligosaccharides

When more than 2 but fewer than ~10 monosaccharides are linked together, the result is an **oligosaccharide**. In brewing only glucose-based oligosaccharides are significant — fragments of starch chains.

| Oligosaccharide | Chain length | Bond type | Fermentable? |
|-----------------|-------------|-----------|-------------|
| Maltotriose | 3 glucose | α(1→4) | Yes (ale and lager) |
| Maltotetraose | 4 glucose | α(1→4) | No |
| Maltopentaose | 5 glucose | α(1→4) | No |
| Linear dextrins | 6+ glucose | α(1→4) | No |
| **Limit dextrins** | small, branched | α(1→4) + α(1→6) | No |

**Limit dextrins** stem from branch points in amylopectin and cannot be hydrolyzed by α- or β-amylase. The enzyme **limit dextrinase** can break them, but is quickly denatured above 60°C (140°F) — and at that temperature, little starch has yet gelatinized in infusion mashing, limiting the enzyme's access to branch points.

![Various oligosaccharides](../brewing/images/carbohydrates/fig-008.png)

*Figure 8 — Various oligosaccharides. (1) Maltotriose. (2) Maltotetraose. (3) Limit dextrin — a branch point from an amylopectin molecule that cannot be hydrolyzed by either α- or β-amylase.*

### Reaction with iodine

When glucose chains are long enough (more than ~9 glucose molecules for unbranched chains), they form a **helical structure** supported by weak bonds between hydroxyl groups. This structure absorbs triiodide (I₃⁻) ions, producing a color reaction — the basis of the **iodine test** for starch or large dextrins.

Iodine (poorly soluble in water) is made soluble through potassium iodide (KI), yielding triiodide (I₃⁻). The resulting color depends on chain length:
- **Black-blue**: amylose and very large dextrins
- **Reddish-brown** (erythrodextrins): shorter chains, ~9–60 glucose molecules in branched chains [Narziss, 2005]
- **No reaction**: chains shorter than ~9 glucose molecules

![Iodine reaction on chalk](../brewing/images/carbohydrates/fig-009.png)

*Figure 9 — Reaction between iodine and mash liquid on chalk. (A) Lots of starch and large dextrins present. (B) Large dextrins present. (C) Iodine-negative mash — no or very little large dextrins present.*

Brewing wort should show **no visible iodine reaction** (no glucose chains longer than ~9 glucose molecules). Otherwise the beer can develop a **starch haze** — actually caused by large dextrins that become less soluble as alcohol increases during fermentation [Narziss, 2005].

## Starch

**Starch** is a mixture of two glucose polymers: **amylose** and **amylopectin**. Glucose molecules are linked in their α configuration (**α-glucans**). Through photosynthesis, plants produce glucose which is stored in starch granules in seeds. Most plant starch (including barley) consists of **20–26% amylose** and the rest amylopectin.

**Amylose** contains on average 1600–1900 glucose molecules held together by α(1→4) bonds, with occasional α(1→6) branch points. With iodine, amylose gives a characteristic **black-blue** color.

**Amylopectin** contains 10–20× more glucose molecules than amylose and is highly branched. α(1→6) branch points account for 6–7% of all bonds [Kunze, 2007]. Structure:
- **C chain**: contains the only reducing end (oriented toward the hilum/center of the granule)
- **B chains**: attached to C chain via α(1→6) links; span multiple clusters (15–25 to 50+ glucose molecules)
- **A chains**: outermost chains, support no other chains (typically 6–15 glucose molecules)

In sections without branch points, neighboring glucose chains form **double helices** that arrange in a **crystalline pattern** held by hydrogen bonds. When thermal energy breaks these bonds, **gelatinization** occurs.

Branch point regions are **amorphous** and may contain amylose. Larger amorphous regions are the visible **growth rings** — shown to result from fluctuations during granule growth; they are absent when plants are grown under constant conditions [Donald, 2004].

![Cross-section of a starch kernel under electron microscope](../brewing/images/carbohydrates/fig-010.png)

*Figure 10 — Cross-section of a starch kernel under an electron microscope.*

![Structure of a starch granule at different length scales](../brewing/images/carbohydrates/fig-011.png)

*Figure 11 — The structure of a starch granule at different length scales.*

In barley, two size groups of starch granules exist:
- **Large A granules**: 22–48 μm diameter — 10–20% by number but 85–90% by weight
- **Small B granules**: 1.7–2.5 μm diameter — the balance [Briggs, 2004]

Starch is often associated with protein, which may hinder breakdown. In well-modified malts this is not a problem; in undermodified malts, a protein rest can improve conversion efficiency.
