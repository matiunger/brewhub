# Mash Fundamentals

*Part of [[transforming-grain]]. From Gordon Strong, "Brewing Better Beer."*

Mashing is mixing crushed malted grain (the **grist**) with water at a set ratio, held at specific temperatures (the **rests**). Enzymes developed during malting break complex carbohydrates into fermentable sugars and other desirable compounds.

## Key Takeaways

- **Enzymes work like a bell curve, not a switch.** They have a peak activity range but still function outside it. Heating *past* their range **denatures** them irreversibly — and denaturing happens faster on the hot side than the cold side.
- Enzymes are highly **substrate-specific** (named with the "-ase" suffix). **Calcium** is an important co-factor that helps protect α-amylase at mash temperatures.
- Enzymes need to be **concentrated enough** to work — too dilute a mash hurts conversion, which is why water-to-grist ratio matters. See [[doughing-in]].
- **Modern malts are very well modified.** The maltster has already done much of the protein/glucan breakdown, so a β-glucanase rest is usually unnecessary and low-temperature protein rests often *harm* head retention and thin the body.

## Actionable Tips

- Make sure your water carries **some calcium** to support and protect the amylase enzymes — see [[water-chemistry]].
- **Don't over-dilute** the mash; keep enzymes concentrated in the liquid fraction.
- With modern well-modified malt, **skip protein and glucan rests** unless you're using starchy adjuncts or undermodified malt.
