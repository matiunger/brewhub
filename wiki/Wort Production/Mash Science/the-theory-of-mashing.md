# The Theory of Mashing

*From German brewing and more — braukaiser.com*

> **Note:** This article has largely been superseded by *The Science of Mashing*. It provides a useful overview and reference tables but the newer article covers these topics in greater depth.

Mashing is the process in which milled grain is mixed with water, activating enzymes that were present in the barley seed or formed during malting. These enzymes work best in particular temperature and pH ranges. By varying mash temperature, the brewer controls enzyme activity. In essence, mashing is an extension of the malting process.

## Starch conversion enzymes

*(Beta Amylase, Alpha Amylase, Limit Dextrinase)*

Starch conversion is the most important aspect of mashing. In barley, starch makes up 63–65% of the dry weight. Starch is a polysaccharide (very large chains of glucose) that is insoluble in water. Brewer's yeast can only ferment monosaccharides (glucose), disaccharides (maltose), and trisaccharides (maltotriose — only completely fermented by lager yeast strains).

Two processes must happen for starch to become fermentable:
1. **Gelatinization** — starch becomes water-soluble. For barley malt starch this occurs above 60°C (140°F). Other starches (e.g. rice) gelatinize only above 90°C (194°F) and require a cereal mash.
2. **Enzymatic conversion** — amylase enzymes break long-chained starch molecules into shorter chains.

Barley starch is composed of **amylose** (17–24%, single chain of glucose linked by 1→4 bonds) and **amylopectin** (76–83%, branched structure with 1→6 bonds at branch points) [Narziss, 2005].

**Beta amylase** produces maltose (the main wort sugar) by splitting 2 glucose molecules from the non-reducing end of a glucose chain. It can completely convert amylose but cannot pass branch points — so amylopectin cannot be completely converted by beta amylase alone.
- Optimal pH: 5.4–5.6
- Optimal temperature: 60–65°C (140–150°F)
- Quickly deactivated above 70°C (160°F) [Narziss, 2005]

**Alpha amylase** can split 1→4 links within glucose chains at any point, exposing additional non-reducing ends for beta amylase to work on — enabling further conversion of amylopectin.
- Optimal pH: 5.6–5.8
- Optimal temperature: 72–75°C (162–167°F)
- Quickly deactivated above 80°C (176°F) [Narziss, 2005]

**Limit dextrinase** can split the 1→6 links found in amylopectin, reducing the amount of limit dextrins left over after α- and β-amylase activity.
- Optimal pH: 5.1
- Optimal temperature: 55–60°C (133–140°F)
- Quickly deactivated above 65°C (149°F) [Narziss, 2005]

Because its optimal temperature is well below common saccharification rest temperatures, limit dextrinase plays only a minor role in most mashing schedules. Extended rests in the lower 55°C range (upper 130s°F) benefit wort fermentability through increased limit dextrinase activity.

The relationship between saccharification rest temperature and apparent limit of attenuation [Narziss, 2005]:

| Temperature | 60°C (140°F) | 65°C (149°F) | 70°C (160°F) | 75°C (167°F) |
|-------------|-------------|-------------|-------------|-------------|
| Apparent limit of attenuation | 87.5% | 86.5% | 76.8% | 54.0% |

*Note: rests were held until conversion was complete. The exact fermentability depends on more than temperature — it is mainly determined by the time beta amylase was active.*

For a single temperature rest, temperatures between 65°C (149°F) and 69°C (158°F) are commonly used. **The higher the temperature, the lower the fermentability** of the resulting wort.

Additional factors affecting fermentability:
- **pH** — a given pH may favour one enzyme over the other
- **Time** — longer mash gives enzymes more time to break down starch and dextrins
- **Water/grist ratio** — with well-modified malts, mash thickness has little impact on fermentability [Narziss, 2005]
- **Crush** — finer crush makes starches accessible more quickly, giving beta amylase more time before deactivation, increasing fermentability
- **Mash schedule** — dough-in at temperatures below the enzyme optimum lets starch hydrate before enzymes are activated, giving beta amylase more time. Decoction mashing is even more effective: boiling gelatinizes starch immediately, making it accessible at beta amylase's optimal temperature (where barley starch is just beginning to gelatinize in a standard infusion)
- **Enzymatic power** — highly kilned base malts (e.g. Munich) or large adjunct additions lower diastatic power; less maltose is produced and fermentability suffers. Counteract with lower rest temperature or more intensive mash schedule

**German multi-step saccharification rests** are commonly applied for better fermentability:
- **Maltose rest** (60–65°C): gives beta amylase plenty of time on already-gelatinized starch. The lower the temperature, the longer beta amylase lasts and the more maltose is produced. 30–60 min typical.
- **Saccharification/dextrinization rest** (70–72°C): above gelatinization temperature for barley starch, within alpha amylase optimum — quickly converts remaining starches. Held until conversion is complete. Also promotes formation of foam-positive glycoproteins [Fix, 1999; Narziss, 2005].

For extremely high fermentability, multiple rests stepping from 60°C to 70°C over 2 hours can be used (similar to Anheuser-Busch's Bud Light mash schedule) — suitable for highly attenuated styles like Belgian Saisons.

### Mash pH targets

While the temperature targets for saccharification are well understood, the targets for mash pH are less consistent among authors — likely because enzymes work adequately in a fairly wide pH range and the effect of pH on beer character is less dramatic than saccharification temperature.

Different recommendations from the literature:
- **Palmer**: 5.4–5.8 (measured at 25°C) [Palmer, 2006]
- **Noonan**: 5.2–5.5 [Noonan, 1996]
- **Narziss**: references a normal wort pH of 5.4–5.6 (kettle full pH) and a mash pH of 5.5–5.6 for foam-positive rests at 70°C [Narziss, 2005]
- **Briggs**: pH optimum for most fermentable worts is 5.1–5.3 at mash temperature (65°C), equivalent to 5.4–5.7 at room temperature (25°C) [Briggs, 2004]

> **Practical recommendation:** Target a mash pH of **5.4–5.6 measured at room temperature (25°C / 77°F)**. This range works well across most styles.

> **Important:** The automatic temperature correction (ATC) of pH meters compensates only for the temperature-dependent response of the probe itself — not for the actual change in mash pH with temperature. The mash pH at 65°C is 0.35 units lower than the same sample at room temperature; this must be accounted for manually.

The mash pH has downstream effects: it determines the boil pH, which affects bitterness extraction and quality, and the cooled wort pH, which affects fermentation performance.

## Protein converting enzymes

*(Proteinase and Peptidase)*

Protein conversion is another aspect of mashing. A significant part of protein conversion may have already occurred during malting and must be considered when choosing a mashing schedule.

The goal is not complete protein conversion but rather a **balanced mix of short (amino acids) and medium-chain proteins**:
- **Amino acids**: necessary yeast nutrient; low FAN (free amino nitrogen) leads to sluggish fermentation
- **Medium-chain proteins**: important for body perception and foam stability

When a protein rest is held, it is typically between 50–55°C (122–133°F) where proteolytic activity is highest, though significant protein conversion also occurs at higher temperatures [Narziss, 2005].

- **Lower end (50°C / 122°F)**: produces more short-chain proteins (amino acids) while depleting long and medium chains. In well-modified malts, this depletion can reduce body and head retention.
- **Upper end (55°C / 131°F)**: emphasizes medium-chain protein formation. Better suited for modern European lager malts.

> A thicker mash protects proteolytic enzymes (they are more stable at higher solids concentration), so protein rests benefit from a thicker mash. This works well with step mash schedules where temperature increases are achieved by hot water infusions.

**Overmodified malts** (English, American Pale) do not require a protein rest. Since protein conversion was driven far during malting, a protein rest may actually deplete the head- and body-positive medium-chain proteins. For such malts, single infusion mashing is preferred.

### Maltase

**Maltase** converts maltose into glucose — important for yeast, which contains this enzyme. Maltase is also present in malt, but since its temperature optimum is 35–40°C (95–104°F) and it is deactivated above 45°C (115°F), it plays no significant role in most mashing schedules.

However, it is specifically exploited in the **Hermann mash schedule** (Markus Hermann, Weihenstephan brewing school):
1. Convert half the mash to build up a large amount of glucose (at 63–65°C)
2. Mix with the remaining unheated mash to reach a rest temperature of 35°C (95°F)
3. Maltase converts the existing maltose to glucose
4. Heat the entire mash through a regular mashing schedule to convert remaining starch to maltose and dextrins

The result is a wort with a very high glucose content (~40% of fermentable sugars). Yeast fermenting high-glucose wort generates more esters — a technique used to produce German wheat beers with high ester content.
