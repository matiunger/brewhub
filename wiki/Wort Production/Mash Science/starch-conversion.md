# Starch Conversion

*From German brewing and more — braukaiser.com*

90–92% of the solids in brewing wort are carbohydrates [Briggs, 2004]. Proteins make up only 4–5%, and the rest are vitamins, minerals, and trace elements. Of the carbohydrates, more than 95% are products of starch conversion that happens in the mash tun. As a result, **starch degradation is the main purpose of mashing** — it produces the fuel yeast needs for fermentation from the insoluble starch found in malt and mash tun adjuncts. The extent of this conversion determines efficiency, wort fermentability, and other quality characteristics.

In mashing, starch conversion is preceded by **gelatinization**. While gelatinization is not strictly necessary for conversion (plants germinate without it), it greatly speeds up the process by exposing amylose and amylopectin from the starch granule to the starch-converting enzymes (mainly α-amylase, β-amylase, and limit dextrinase).

## Gelatinization

Starch granules are insoluble in cold water and absorb only a little. They form a suspension that quickly settles when agitation stops. As water is heated (50°C+) more water is absorbed, granules swell [Narziss, 2005], and the water absorbed can be up to 30× the weight of the starch granule. This uptake initially occurs within the amorphous growth rings. At this point the granule begins to leach amylose, crystalline layers break open and separate as gelatinous sheets — the crystalline structure is lost and the process becomes irreversible [Shetty, 2006]. The starch granule has **gelatinized**.

While the temperature range for gelatinization of individual granules is quite narrow (~1°C), the range between the first and last granule gelatinizing across a sample can be large.

![Gelatinization temperature ranges for various starches](../brewing/images/starch-conversion/fig-000.png)

*Figure 1 — Temperature ranges for the gelatinization of various starches [Briggs, 2004]. Starches marked with (*) also benefit from boiling before being used in the mash. Note the separate ranges for large and small barley granules — 90% of barley starch (by weight) is large A granules, which gelatinize at common saccharification rest temperatures.*

Not all starches fully gelatinize at saccharification rest temperatures. If this is the case they must be gelatinized beforehand through a cereal mash or pre-gelatinized forms (e.g. flaked adjuncts). Also note that the small B granules in barley (~10% by weight) may not fully gelatinize until temperatures above the typical saccharification rest range — a factor that can explain some efficiency benefits from a mash-out or decoction mash.

Gelatinization requires **free water** for swelling and for breaking hydrogen bonds in crystalline structures. If free water is limited (overly thick mash), less swelling occurs and gelatinization temperature rises. For corn starch, a 25% sucrose solution increases gelatinization temperature from 70 to 78°C [Donald, 2004] — one reason thick mashes show lower efficiency.

As starch gelatinizes, **mash viscosity increases** — but this is quickly reduced by α-amylase breaking down the released amylose and amylopectin, a process called **liquefaction** [Kunze, 2007]. This is why a rest at 70–74°C before boiling a decoction mash limits the risk of scorching.

> In **cereal mashes** with rice starch (which swells very intensely), a short liquefaction rest at 75–80°C should be held before heating to boiling to prevent scorching or immobilization of mash agitators [Kunze, 2007]. Some malt should also be added to provide the necessary enzymes.

The gelatinization temperature also depends on the VZ 45°C malt analysis number — a ratio between the extract extractable at 45°C versus a congress mash. Lower VZ 45°C values indicate lower malt modification and potentially higher gelatinization temperatures (up to ~65°C for some Weyermann malts), which can be problematic for a maltose rest held at 63°C.

![VZ 45°C values vs gelatinization temperature for 48 malt samples](../brewing/images/starch-conversion/fig-001.png)

*Figure 2 — Plot of VZ 45°C analysis values over the gelatinization temperature for 48 different malt samples [Kessler, 2006].*

## Enzymatic starch breakdown

Four enzyme types can participate in starch conversion in the mash:

![Branched dextrin showing which links each enzyme can break](../brewing/images/starch-conversion/fig-002.png)

*Figure 3 — A large branched dextrin showing which links can be broken (hydrolyzed) by which mash enzyme. β-amylase acts on non-reducing ends; α-amylase cleaves internal α(1→4) bonds; limit dextrinase breaks α(1→6) branch points.*

**α-amylase** can cleave any α(1→4) bond in starch and dextrins except for those close to branch points. It is responsible for the rapid loss of viscosity and iodine reaction after starch gelatinizes (**liquefaction**). While α-amylase is very effective at breaking starch into smaller dextrins, it is not good at producing fermentable sugars due to its low affinity toward smaller glucose chains.

**β-amylase** is much better at producing fermentable sugars. It works from the non-reducing end of a glucose chain, clipping off one maltose molecule after another. It must stop when it approaches a branch point (α(1→6) link) — it relies on α-amylase to create new non-reducing ends beyond branch points, and on limit dextrinase to break the branches themselves. **α- and β-amylase work in concert**: α-amylase creates substrate (non-reducing ends) for β-amylase.

The process of creating sugars is called **saccharification** and happens mostly after liquefaction. Mashes targeting high fermentability always try to maximize β-amylase activity through rest temperature and time.

**Limit dextrinase** can break the α(1→6) bonds that form branch points in amylopectin — the only enzyme that can do this in the mash. It has a temperature optimum of 60–62.5°C, similar to β-amylase. Its instability at common mash temperatures (63–70°C) means most brewing wort contains a substantial amount of **limit dextrins** (residual branch points left by α- and β-amylase). Unless very high fermentability is desired, these residual dextrins are actually desirable — they contribute positively to beer character.

**Maltase** has an optimum between 30–40°C and splits a single glucose molecule from the non-reducing end of a glucose chain. It is generally of little interest in mashing as at its working temperature there is little maltose present. It is specifically used in the **Hermann mash schedule** (developed at Weihenstephan) to produce high-glucose worts for ester-rich Weissbiers — the mash is first converted at 63–65°C, cooled to 40°C, fresh malt added, and after a 30–45 min rest the mash is heated again to convert the added starch.

**Saccharase (invertase)** is also active in the mash — it breaks sucrose into fructose and glucose — but because sucrose content in malt is insignificant, its activity has little impact on wort composition.

The table below summarizes temperature and pH optima for the starch-converting mash enzymes:

| Enzyme | Temp optimum (°C) | Temp optimum (°F) | pH optimum (cooled sample) |
|--------|------------------|------------------|---------------------------|
| Maltase | 30–40 | 86–104 | 6.0 |
| Saccharase (invertase) | ~52 | ~126 | 5.5 |
| Limit dextrinase | 60–62.5 | 140–145 | 5.1 |
| β-amylase | 60–65 | 140–149 | 5.4–5.6 |
| α-amylase | 72–75 | 162–167 | 5.6–5.8 |

*Table 2 — Temperature and pH optima for starch-converting mash enzymes [Narziss, 2005], [Kessler, 2006]*

Starch conversion proceeds through **4 stages** [Kunze, 2007]:

1. **Dough-in**: starch present as a suspension of granules. No iodine reaction, low viscosity.
2. **Gelatinization**: granules release amylose and amylopectin. Viscosity rises sharply. Iodine reaction: blue-black.
3. **Liquefaction**: α-amylase and limit dextrinase rapidly break large molecules into large dextrins. Viscosity drops. Iodine reaction becomes reddish-brown.
4. **Saccharification**: β-amylase and limit dextrinase reduce dextrins to mostly fermentable sugars. Iodine reaction disappears completely.

![The 4 stages of starch conversion — iodine reaction and viscosity progression](../brewing/images/starch-conversion/fig-003.png)

*Figure 4 — The 4 stages of starch conversion in the mash and the progression of the iodine reaction and mash liquid viscosity.*

> **Water consumption:** The hydrolysis of starch to sugars consumes one water molecule per bond broken. For a typical 19L (5 gal) batch with ~3 kg starch this amounts to ~150 mL — about 1% of mash water and generally negligible in water calculations.

## Effect of mashing conditions

### Temperature and time

The saccharification rest temperature and time directly control wort fermentability by determining how long β-amylase and limit dextrinase are allowed to act before denaturing.

The higher the temperature, the faster β-amylase denatures and the fewer fermentable sugars are produced. At lower temperatures these enzymes work longer but reactions are slower, and a longer mash may be needed for full starch conversion. Note that some α-amylase activity is necessary to provide non-reducing ends for β-amylase — even at temperatures below α-amylase's optimum, enough activity exists.

![Qualitative alpha amylase activity for different mash temperatures](../brewing/images/starch-conversion/fig-005.png)

Only the 60°C mash lags significantly — due to partial starch gelatinization and slower enzyme kinetics at that temperature. Fermentable extract, however, levels off much more quickly at higher temperatures due to faster β-amylase denaturing.

The table below summarizes temperature optima for various wort quality parameters in 2–3 hour mashes:

| Quality parameter | Optimum (°C) | Optimum (°F) |
|-------------------|-------------|-------------|
| Highest extract (starch conversion) | 65–68 | 149–154 |
| Highest fermentability | ~63 | ~145 |
| Maximum α-amylase activity | 72–75 | 162–167 |
| Maximum β-amylase activity | 60–65 | 140–149 |

*Table 3 — Temperature optima for wort quality parameters [Briggs, 2004]*

In **multi-step saccharification rests** (e.g., the German Hochkurz mash), fermentability is primarily controlled through the length of the maltose rest at 60–64°C (140–148°F).

### pH and brewing water

As discussed in [Enzymes](enzymes.md), mash pH significantly affects enzyme activity. Mashing experiments have shown that suboptimal pH results in lower extract production and less fermentable wort.

| Quality parameter | pH at mash temp | pH of cooled sample |
|-------------------|----------------|---------------------|
| Greatest extract (infusion mash) | 5.2–5.4 | 5.55–5.75 |
| Greatest extract (decoction mash) | 5.3–5.6 | 5.65–5.95 |
| Highest fermentability | 5.1–5.3 | 5.4–5.6 |
| Maximum α-amylase activity | ~5.3 | ~5.7 |
| Maximum β-amylase activity | 5.1–5.3 | 5.4–5.6 |
| Maximum phytase activity | — | 5.5 |

*Table 3 — pH optima for certain mash characteristics [Briggs, 2004]*

> **Temperature correction:** The pH of a mash at 65°C is ~0.35 pH units lower than the same sample measured at room temperature (25°C), and ~0.45 units lower at 75°C [Briggs, 2004]. The pH optimum of α-amylase is commonly reported as 5.7 in brewing literature because the measurement is made on a **cooled sample** — at mash temperature the same wort would be at 5.3. Always specify whether a pH value is at mash temperature or room temperature.

The mash pH also determines the boil pH (at or slightly above mash pH) and cast-out wort pH (0.1–0.2 units below boil pH). Getting mash pH right affects bitterness extraction, protein coagulation, and fermentation performance.

**Calcium ions** stabilize α-amylase [Briggs, 2004] — useful in cereal mashing to keep α-amylase active during gelatinization and limit viscosity increase. β-amylase stability is not affected by calcium.

### Water to grist ratio

The concentration of the mash (water to grist ratio) significantly affects mash performance. Very thick mashes (<2 L/kg or 1 qt/lb) are difficult to stir and reduce extract recovery while slowing starch conversion [Briggs, 2004].

Amylase enzymes are **more stable in thicker mashes** — especially important for the more heat-labile β-amylase, which means thicker mashes can give more fermentable worts at high mashing temperatures. However, thick mashes also inhibit enzymatic activity through reduced availability of free water and competitive inhibition by dissolved sugars. Gelatinization is also slower and occurs at higher temperatures in thick mashes.

![Effect of mash thickness on extract, fermentable extract, fermentability, and permanently soluble nitrogen](../brewing/images/starch-conversion/fig-007.png)

*Figure 7 — Effect of mash thickness on extract, fermentable extract, fermentability, and permanently soluble nitrogen for mashes at 60°C (140°F) for 180 min. Note that thinner mashes convert more starch and produce more fermentable sugars, while thicker mashes show better proteolytic activity. Data by Windisch, Kolbach and Schild via [Briggs, 2004].*

![β-amylase activity at different mash thicknesses](../brewing/images/starch-conversion/fig-008.png)

*Figure 8 — Progression of β-amylase activity in mashes at 65°C at 3 different mash thicknesses. Data by Narziss via [Kunze, 2007].*

In practice, data from mashing experiments showed little to no dependency of **fermentability** on mash thickness with well-modified malts [Narziss, 2005] — but a strong effect on **conversion efficiency**, with thinner mashes converting more starch.

Typical mash thicknesses:
- **English mashes**: 2–2.5 L/kg (1–1.25 qt/lb) — thicker, traditional single-vessel systems
- **German mashes**: 3.5–5 L/kg (1.75–2.5 qt/lb) — thinner, to enable stirring and pumping in multi-vessel systems

### Preparation of the grist

The level of **crushing** has a substantial impact on mash performance. A finer crush results in more flour and smaller particles — the endosperm is more completely separated from husks, giving brewing water and enzymes faster access to starch. This results in:
- Faster conversion
- Greater extract recovery (better conversion efficiency)
- Higher wort fermentability

Brewers generally prefer the malt crushed as fine as their lauter system allows. Some commercial breweries use pulverized grists with mash filters (since pulverization destroys husks needed for filter support in lauter tuns).

> While finer crush clearly improves extract recovery, its effect on fermentability was not clearly observed in mashing experiments.
