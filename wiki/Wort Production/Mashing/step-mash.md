# Step Mash

*Part of [[transforming-grain]]. From Gordon Strong, "Brewing Better Beer."*

A step (step-infusion) mash is a [[single-infusion-mash]] variant using **two or more rests in the same tun**, always rising in temperature — reached by boiling-water infusion or direct heat. It's the modern German alternative to decoction for a malty-but-attenuated character.

## Key Takeaways

- Improves **attenuation and efficiency**, especially with starchy adjuncts or not-fully-modified malts. Needs no extra equipment — just more time.
- With direct heat, **recirculate the wort** to avoid scorching and even out temperature (the principle behind a RIMS).
- Strong uses it for **altbier, Kölsch, Belgian ales, IPAs, and oatmeal stout** (adding a protein rest for adjuncts). He would *not* step-mash highly modified English malt.
- **Hochkurz ("high-short") program:** mash-in ~145°F (63°C) for 30–40 min → step to 158°F (70°C) for 30–50 min → mash-out 168–170°F for 20 min, at ~2 qt/lb.

## Actionable Tips

- Add a **131°F (55°C) rest** when brewing with Pils malt, and always **step to mash-out at 170°F (77°C)**.
- For an attenuated German lager without decocting, try **131 → 145 → 158°F (55 → 63 → 70°C)**.
- Combine **step mashing with high sugar additions** (e.g. ~20% sugar) to drive a dry, highly attenuated Belgian tripel.
- Pair step mashing with a single decoction to capture decoction flavor with less time — see [[decoction-mash]].
