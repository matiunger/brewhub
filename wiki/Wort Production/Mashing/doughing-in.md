# Doughing-In

*Part of [[transforming-grain]]. From Gordon Strong, "Brewing Better Beer."*

Doughing-in is the initial combining of milled grain with strike water. Its real purpose is to **properly hydrate the starches** in the crushed malt, which drives mash efficiency.

## Key Takeaways

- **Avoid clumps (balls)** — dry starch inside a clump never converts. Stir thoroughly but **avoid excessive aeration** to prevent oxidizing the mash.
- **Mash thickness** (water-to-grist ratio) matters more for even temperature, easy stirring, and protecting enzymes than for fermentability — with modern malt it has little effect on fermentability. Thicker protects enzymes; thinner evens out temperature.
- Traditional ratios: English **thick** 1.0–1.25 qt/lb (combined mash/lauter tun); German **thin** 1.75–2.5 qt/lb (directly heated, often decoction). Strong's compromise: **1.5 qt/lb (3.2 L/kg)**, thinner for limited sparging.
- **Dough-in temperature:** at protein-rest temp only if you're doing one; otherwise dough-in *above* it. Most homebrewers dough-in straight at the saccharification temperature.

## Actionable Tips

- Add **rice hulls** (≈1 lb / 454 g per 5-gallon batch) to even out mash temperature and insure a smoother lauter.
- Use a **long-probe thermometer** — you'll be surprised how much temperature varies across the tun.
- Going thicker? Bump the ratio up slightly if you plan to decoct (see [[decoction-mash]]).
