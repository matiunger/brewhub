# Handling Dark Grains

*Part of [[transforming-grain]]. From Gordon Strong, "Brewing Better Beer."*

Brewing with dark grains is like brewing coffee: **longer exposure to higher heat yields more bitterness, acidity, and harshness.** Dark roasted grains (chocolate, black patent, roasted barley) have no starch left to convert — they don't need to be mashed, only steeped.

## Key Takeaways — Three Ways To Use Them

- **Traditional mash** — milled and mashed with the grist. Harshest and most astringent (the grain sees the most heat), but the added acidity can usefully **lower mash pH** if your water is high in carbonates.
- **Add at vorlauf / mash-out** — milled separately and added during recirculation. Avoids the long hot steep and most astringency; the extract is still boiled. Crystal malts can join here too.
- **Separate steep** — milled (even finely) and steeped apart, then blended in. Options: **hot** (165°F / 74°C, 5 min), **cold** (room temp, 24 hr), or **cold + short boil** (added the last 5–10 min of the boil).

## Key Takeaways — Steeping Details

- Ratio **1 lb : 2 qt** (454 g : 1.9 L) clean, chlorine-free water (RO is ideal); ~45–50% extract yield.
- **Black malt** tastes best cold-steeped; **roasted barley** best hot-steeped or cold + short boil. Roasted barley cold-steeped *only* tastes like cold coffee.
- The dark extract is **stable and storable** — refrigerate in a mason jar and blend to taste, even after fermentation (watch for oxygen).

## Actionable Tips

- **Cold-steep** for smooth dark beers without roasty bitterness — sweet stout, schwarzbier, oatmeal stout.
- Add the extract **in batches** to dial in color and roast precisely.
- Manage [[water-chemistry]] (avoid high bicarbonates / high pH) to keep dark beers from turning harsh.
