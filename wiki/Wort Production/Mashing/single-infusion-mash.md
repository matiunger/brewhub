# Single-Infusion Mash

*Part of [[transforming-grain]]. From Gordon Strong, "Brewing Better Beer."*

The traditional English method and the most popular modern technique for well-modified malt. It uses **one saccharification rest** in the 148–158°F (64–70°C) range — commonly **151–154°F (66–68°C)** — letting both diastatic enzymes work together.

## Key Takeaways

- **Mash temperature controls attenuation:**
  - **Low end (≤150°F / 66°C)** → less body, more fermentable, higher alcohol.
  - **High end (≥156°F / 69°C)** → chewier, sweeter, higher final gravity, lower alcohol.
- It's the **fastest, easiest, cheapest** method and works in any moderately insulated vessel that holds the full volume — best suited to well-modified (especially English) malt.
- **Mash pH** should sit 5.1–5.5, target **5.2–5.3** (measured at room temperature). It mostly self-regulates as long as your water has some calcium and isn't high in carbonates.
- The **same temperature gives different results across malts, maltsters, and even lots** — barley is an agricultural product. Keep careful notes.

## Actionable Tips

- Use ~**1.5 qt/lb** water-to-grist and a pound of rice hulls per 5 gallons for even temperature.
- **Don't obsess over pH** once you understand your water — only measure when troubleshooting or trying something unusual.
- If a beer drifts batch-to-batch, check whether the malt type, maltster, or lot changed before blaming your process. See [[mash-parameters-attenuation-efficiency]].
