# Decoction Mash

*Part of [[transforming-grain]]. From Gordon Strong, "Brewing Better Beer." For the full technical procedure see [[decoction-mashing]].*

The most complex, time-consuming method: pull a portion of the mash (a **decoction**), heat/rest/boil it separately, then return it to raise the main mash temperature — like a [[step-mash]] driven by hot grain instead of hot water. Programs run single, double, or triple decoction.

## Key Takeaways

- Boiling the mash drives the **Maillard reaction** — rich, complex malt flavor plus melanoidins (this is *not* caramelization). Best for **undermodified malts** and low-diastatic malts like Munich; it also bursts starch granules for better access.
- **Always pull a *thick* decoction** (the grain fraction, which carries the amino acids for Maillard) — *except the last one*. Enzymes live in the thin, watery main mash, so keep that protected. Main mash runs thinner (~2 qt/lb).
- **pH 5.2–5.5 prevents tannin extraction** even while boiling grain — so boiling the mash doesn't create harshness (unless you scorch it).
- Three independent ways to boost maltiness — **grist reformulation** (Munich/Vienna/melanoidin malt), **decoction**, and **no-sparge** (see [[lautering-options]]) — and they can be combined.

## Actionable Tips

- Don't preheat the decoction pot; heat slowly (**2–4°F / 1–2°C per minute**), stir constantly, and add pH-5.5 water if grain sticks.
- Pull roughly **33–40%** of the mash as the thick decoction.
- **Combine a step mash with a single decoction** to get decoction depth while saving time; keep boils short for pale beers to limit color.
- Beware **over-decoction** — a beef-broth note signals too much Maillard or too much dark Munich-type malt.
