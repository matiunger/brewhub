# Decoction Mashing

*From German brewing and more*

A **decoction mash** is a type of mash in which at least one rest temperature is reached by removing part of the mash, boiling it in a separate vessel, and mixing it back in to raise the temperature. It is traditional in many continental European beer styles, especially in Germany and the Czech Republic. Most breweries in these regions have since switched to the more economical directly heated step infusion mashing.

Decoction mashing has a reputation as time- and labour-intensive, but it is essentially a step infusion mash where some grist is heated and returned instead of infusion water. While it requires extra time and stirring, it is well within reach of most home brewers.

## History of the Decoction Mash

Decoction mashing originates from a time when malt quality was inconsistent and temperatures could not be measured. The long boiling of the grain makes starches more accessible for enzymes — particularly important for **undermodified malts** where cell walls are not as well broken down as in modern, well-modified malts. Boiling a defined portion of the mash and returning it to raise the temperature also ensured consistent mashing temperatures before thermometers were available.

---

## Chemistry of the Decoction Mash

Today, even most European malts are well modified and can be used in infusion step mashes or single infusion mashes, removing the technical necessity for decoction. However, decoction mashing remains widely used, particularly in smaller southern German breweries and for dark beers like Bocks and Dunkels.

Many brewers believe boiling the mash produces a flavour profile that cannot be achieved otherwise. In the home brewing community there is ongoing debate about whether the process delivers meaningful benefits with modern malts, or whether similar results could be achieved with specialty malts. Every brewer must determine this for themselves through experimentation.

---

## Decoction Mash Procedure

Water is added to the grist to reach the initial mash temperature. Once the first temperature rest is complete, a portion of the grain and water is scooped into the kettle or another heated vessel and brought to a boil. This portion — often as much as a third of the grist — is called the **decoction**.

The decoction may require stirring during heating to prevent scorching. It adds time to the mash process since it cannot be heated as fast as infusion water, and is typically boiled for 5–45 min. After boiling, the decoction is returned to the mash tun to achieve the next temperature rest.

## Sample Decoction Mash Schedules

### Triple Decoction

![Triple decoction mash schedule](../brewing/images/decoction-mashing/fig-002.png)

*Figure 1 — Triple decoction mash schedule.*

The triple decoction is the original decoction mash — how the first Pilsners were brewed in Pilsen and how German beer was brewed for centuries.

It employs three main temperature rests: **acid rest**, **protein rest**, and **saccharification rest**. At each rest, a decoction raises the mash to the next temperature until mash-out is reached.

**Calculating decoction volume:**

A simple formula for estimating the decoction volume:

```
decoction volume = total mash volume × (target temp − start temp) / (boil temp − start temp)
```

Add about 15–20% extra. When returning the decoction to the main mash, add it gradually in stages, checking the main mash temperature and stirring thoroughly after each addition. Once the target temperature is reached, let any remaining decoction cool and add it when its temperature is close to the main mash temperature. This approach accounts for evaporation, unexpected temperature drops, and other variables.

**Decoction thickness:**

The decoction should not be too thick (grain should remain submerged) to aid stirring and prevent scorching. At a main mash thickness of 1.5–2 qt/lb (3–4 L/kg), the decoction should be around 1–1.25 qt/lb (2–2.5 L/kg). With gentle heating at this thickness, only occasional stirring is needed.

**Heating rate and saccharification rest:**

All schedules here assume a decoction rise rate of 2–4 °F/min (1–2 °C/min). There is a saccharification rest at 155–162 °F (68–72 °C) to utilise the enzymatic power of the decoction before heat destroys the enzymes. This is especially important when brewing with a high percentage of enzymatically weaker dark base malts. Once the decoction is converted or nearly converted (iodine test), resume heating. To hold the rest, remove the pot from heat and insulate with blankets.

**Boil time:** 10–40 min. Shorter for light-coloured beers, longer for dark beers. Excessive thermal loading can produce burnt flavours. Evaporation losses can be compensated with water additions or by boiling with the lid on — any trapped DMS will be driven off during the wort boil.

**Protein rest:**

When returning the first decoction to reach the protein rest, base the duration on malt modification:
- **Undermodified malts:** rest closer to 122 °F (50 °C) to produce more amino acids (essential yeast nutrients), as these malts have insufficient FAN (free amino nitrogen) without a more intensive protein rest.
- **Well-modified modern malts:** rest closer to 133 °F (55 °C) and pull the next decoction 5–10 min after reaching temperature. This protects medium-chain proteins important for body and head retention.

**Second decoction (to saccharification rest):**

The same saccharification rest temperature used in a single infusion mash is a good starting point, but results may differ: boiling destroys more enzymes (shifting towards less fermentable) while simultaneously making starch more accessible (shifting towards more fermentable). Experimentation may be required to find the optimal temperature. Hold the saccharification rest for about 45 min, or longer if starch conversion is not yet complete.

**Final decoction (to mash-out):**

This decoction can be thinner and does not need a starch conversion rest since the starches are already converted.

### Single Decoction

In a single decoction mash, one decoction is used — most commonly to reach mash-out temperature, as a simple enhancement of a single infusion or step mash.

![Single decoction mash schedule](../brewing/images/decoction-mashing/fig-003.png)

*Figure 2 — Single decoction mash schedule suited for European and continental lager malts.*

This schedule features a short protein rest at the higher end of the proteolytic range, a single saccharification rest, and a decoction to mash-out.

**Procedure:**
1. Calculate strike water to reach a protein rest of 53–55 °C (129–133 °F) at a mash consistency of ~2.5 L/kg (1.2 qt/lb). Check the pH and correct to 5.3–5.6 if needed. Hold for 20 min.
2. During the protein rest, bring about half the dough-in water volume to a boil. Ladle boiling water into the mash, stirring and measuring temperature until the saccharification rest of 65–68 °C (148–155 °F) is reached. The thinned mash (now ~3.5–4 L/kg / 1.7–2.0 qt/lb) is easier to stir — typical for German-style beers. Hold for 45 min.
3. Check for full conversion. Calculate the decoction volume needed to reach mash-out at 74–76 °C (165–170 °F) and pull the decoction (include a good mix of liquid and grain to prevent scorching). Heat to boiling over 10–15 min with a gentle flame and the pot covered. Boil 10–30 min (shorter for light worts, longer for dark). Return to main mash to reach mash-out.

## Double Decoction

### Classic Double Decoction

The classic double decoction is a shortened triple decoction — it omits the acid rest and starts directly with the protein rest. Only 2 decoctions are then needed to reach mash-out. However, resting the main mash at the protein rest for a long time with well-modified lager malts may lead to overly extensive protein degradation. The schedules below avoid this issue.

---

### Enhanced Double Decoction

![Enhanced double decoction mash schedule](../brewing/images/decoction-mashing/fig-004.png)

*Figure 3 — Enhanced double decoction schedule [Narziss, 2005].*

This schedule achieves almost the same boiling intensity as a triple decoction but in less time and without a long protein rest. The key idea: pull a decoction large enough to go from acid rest to saccharification rest in one step, but return it in two stages — first to reach the protein rest, then to reach the saccharification rest.

**Procedure:**
1. Dough in at the acid rest and correct mash pH if necessary.
2. Pull a large decoction (~50–60% of the mash). Optionally add 5–10% extra water to compensate for boil-off and thin it slightly. Due to its size, rest for protein rest (optional) and saccharification rest at 155–162 °F (68–72 °C) for 15–20 min (insulate the pot). When fully or nearly converted, return to heat and bring to a boil. Watch for foam-up just before and after boiling. Boil 10–30 min.
3. Use a heat-resistant vessel to ladle decoction back into the main mash, stirring and checking temperature after each addition, until the **protein rest temperature** is reached. Hold for 15–20 min while the remaining decoction continues boiling.
4. Continue adding the decoction in stages until the **saccharification rest temperature** is reached. If any decoction remains, let it cool and add it when close to the main mash temperature.
5. Hold the saccharification rest; check conversion. Pull the 2nd decoction, heat to boiling, boil 5–20 min, and return to main mash to reach mash-out.

#### Variation: Maximum Fermentability

For highly fermentable wort where virtually all starch is boiled and converted in the decoction:

1. Dough in at acid rest in the boil kettle. Preheat the mash tun with boiling water.
2. After 10–20 min (and pH correction), remove the top 40% of the mash (mostly liquid, containing the enzymes) into the pre-heated mash tun. The kettle retains a thick decoction.
3. Heat, convert, and boil the decoction. Return part to the mash tun for a protein rest (optional), then the remainder for the saccharification rest.

> At the start of the saccharification rest, more glucose chain ends are available than in a conventional mash since almost all starch was converted in the decoction. This provides extensive opportunity for beta amylase to create maltose. For normal fermentability, hold closer to 160 °F (71 °C); for very high fermentability, hold closer to 140 °F (60 °C) where beta amylase remains active much longer.

Use a 2nd decoction to reach mash-out.

### Hochkurz Double Decoction

![Hochkurz double decoction mash schedule](../brewing/images/decoction-mashing/fig-005.png)

*Figure 4 — Hochkurz double decoction schedule [Narziss, 2005].*

The **Hochkurz** (*hoch* = high, *kurz* = short) double decoction uses a 2-temperature saccharification scheme. The first decoction raises the mash from the maltose rest to the dextrinisation rest; the second decoction achieves mash-out. Dough-in can be at the protein rest, an intermediate rest, or directly at the maltose rest.

**Maltose rest — 140–146 °F (60–63 °C):**

At this temperature, beta amylase clips maltose from the non-reducing ends of glucose chains. Sufficient alpha amylase activity at this temperature creates enough new chain ends to keep beta amylase supplied. The lower temperature keeps beta amylase active for longer than in a single high-temperature saccharification rest.

**Dextrinisation rest — 158–162 °F (70–72 °C):**

Beta amylase is quickly deactivated; only alpha amylase remains active on the starches. Hold until the mash is iodine negative. Narziss [Narziss, 2005] and Fix [Fix, 1999] both note that a rest at 158–162 °F benefits head retention and body through glycoproteins extracted from the malt that are not degraded enzymatically. Narziss recommends holding this rest up to 60 min.

**Mash-out — 167–173 °F (75–78 °C):**

Do not exceed 173 °F (78 °C) — some alpha amylase activity is still needed during lautering to convert any starch liberated from the grain during sparging before it reaches the kettle.

The fermentability of the wort is controlled by the **length and temperature of the maltose rest**: shorter duration and/or higher temperature produce less fermentable wort.

> This mash schedule can also be performed without decoctions — hot water infusions or direct heat can achieve the same temperature steps. The latter has become standard in most German breweries.
