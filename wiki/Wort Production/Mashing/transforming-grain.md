# Transforming Grain

*Mastering Techniques — the mashing phase. From Gordon Strong, "Brewing Better Beer."*

Turning grain into wort is the essence of brewing: *"Brewers make wort, but yeast make beer."* This page is the entry point for the mashing techniques — converting grain-based raw ingredients into fermentable wort.

## Key Takeaways

- The goal of this phase is to transform grain starches into sugars the yeast can ferment; grains also contribute **color, flavor, aroma, and body**.
- Starchy grains and malts must be **mashed** (crushed, combined with water, rested at temperatures so enzymes convert starch → sugar). Roasted and crystal grains can simply be **steeped** — see [[handling-dark-grains]].
- Mashing is **indirect control**: you create the conditions (temperature, pH, water-to-grist ratio, time) and let the enzymes do the work.
- There is no single "right" method — choose the mash technique to match your malt and your target beer.

## Actionable Tips

- **Learn your own system.** Don't blindly follow instructions — measure your grain water-absorption, evaporation rate, and efficiency, then plan recipes around your real numbers. See [[understanding-efficiency]].
- Develop an intuitive feel for how a beer turns out based on mash temperature and method before leaning on brewing software.
