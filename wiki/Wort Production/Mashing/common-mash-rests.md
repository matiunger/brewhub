# Common Mash Rests

*Part of [[transforming-grain]]. From Gordon Strong, "Brewing Better Beer."*

A mash program is built from one or more rests, each chosen to favor a particular enzyme. Always read pH at **room temperature** (a mash-temperature reading runs ~0.3 lower).

## Key Takeaways — The Rests

| Rest | Temperature | Purpose |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Protein** | 104–140°F (40–60°C); most active 122–131°F | Proteases/peptidases. **Avoid 122°F (50°C)** with modern malt — it destroys body/head proteins. |
| **β-amylase** (saccharification) | 131–150°F (55–66°C); optimal 142–146°F (61–63°C) | Makes fermentable **maltose**; lower/longer = more fermentable. Best at pH 5.0–5.6 (5.3 ideal). |
| **α-amylase** (saccharification) | 154–162°F (68–72°C); higher pH 5.1–5.9 | Makes **dextrins** → body and higher finishing gravity. Works slower than β-amylase. |
| **Mash-out / lauter** | 168–170°F (76–77°C) | **Denatures all enzymes** (fixes the wort) and dissolves sugars to ease lautering. |
| **Ferulic acid** | 111–115°F (44–46°C), optimal 113°F | 10 min for weizen — develops the clove-like 4-vinyl guaiacol. |
| **β-glucanase** | 98–113°F (37–45°C), 20 min | For gummy adjuncts (wheat, rye, oats). |

## Actionable Tips

- A short rest at **131°F (55°C) for 10–15 min** improves clarity and head retention with German/Belgian (especially Pils) malt; skip it for British and American malts.
- Choose the saccharification temperature to set attenuation — see [[single-infusion-mash]] and [[step-mash]].
- **Don't bother with an acid rest.** Use water treatment or acidulated malt instead — see [[how-ph-affects-brewing]].
