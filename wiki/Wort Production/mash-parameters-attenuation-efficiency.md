# Effects of Mash Parameters on Attenuation and Conversion Efficiency

*Evaluation of the Effect of Mash Parameters on the Limit of Attenuation and Conversion Efficiency in Single Infusion Mashing*

**Kai Troester — 2008**

*(This is a PDF version of the braukaiser.com article on the subject)*

It is commonly known that there are many factors that affect the fermentability (limit of attenuation) of brewing wort. The series of experiments conducted here are aimed at understanding not only the qualitative impact but also quantifying the changes of fermentability depending on the parameters considered for evaluation.

In the Understanding Attenuation article [braukaiser.com] it is mentioned that final attenuation of a beer mainly depends on 2 factors: **limit of attenuation** (i.e., attenuation potential) and the **yeast's ability** to come close to that limit. The limit of attenuation is only affected by mashing, and the Fast Ferment Test has been introduced to determine it.

These experiments focus on single infusion mashes and explore the effects of the following mash parameters:

- **Saccharification rest temperature**: For a single step saccharification rest, the mash temperature has a great effect on the fermentability. The lower the temperature (within a given range), the longer the beta-amylase will be able to work and produce maltose.
- **Time**: Determines how much time the enzymes will have to work on the mash.
- **Mash pH**: Beta and alpha amylase have different optimal pH ranges; the mash pH affects the activity balance between these enzymes.
- **Grind**: Larger grits make it harder for mash water to fully hydrate endosperm and make starches accessible to the enzymes. Starch released later — when beta amylase activity has already diminished — results in lower limit of attenuation.
- **Water to grist ratio**: Enzymatic activity is affected by mash thickness. Thinner mashes enhance maltose production and therefore increase fermentability.
- **Grain bill composition**: Mashes with high diastatic power (Pilsner, Pale) produce more fermentable worts due to larger amounts of beta-amylase compared to mashes with lower diastatic power (Munich or large amounts of unmalted grains), assuming the same saccharification rest temperature.

## Materials and Methods

All experiments were conducted at a relatively small scale with minimal overhead. The strike water was heated in a microwave and then added to a small stainless steel thermos bottle fitted with a Styrofoam stopper holding an alcohol thermometer — allowing temperature to be read without opening the bottle.

![Figure 1 — Stainless steel thermos bottle used for mashing](../brewing/images/mash-parameters-attenuation-efficiency/fig-062.png)

*Figure 1 — The samples were mashed in a stainless steel thermos bottle.*

Once the 60-minute mash was complete, a sample was tested for starch conversion with iodine on chalk. The mash was then lautered through a wire strainer over a pot. Sparge water was heated in the microwave, added to the grain, well mixed, and lautered again — resembling a batch sparge.

![Figure 2 — Wire strainer used for batch-sparge style lautering](../brewing/images/mash-parameters-attenuation-efficiency/fig-063.png)

*Figure 2 — The mash was strained batch-sparge style through a wire strainer.*

After boiling the wort for 15 minutes, it was strained through a paper towel set in a funnel into a clean 12 oz bottle. The wort was topped off with reverse osmosis (RO) water when necessary to reach the same final wort volume for all experiments.

![Figure 3 — Boiling the wort for 15 minutes](../brewing/images/mash-parameters-attenuation-efficiency/fig-095.png)

*Figure 3 — The wort was boiled for 15 min over medium heat.*

![Figure 4 — Paper towel funnel filter](../brewing/images/mash-parameters-attenuation-efficiency/fig-096.png)

*Figure 4 — After the boil was complete, the hot wort was filtered through a paper towel set in a funnel.*

After capping with aluminum foil and cooling at room temperature, the original extract was measured with a hydrometer (0–40°P range) and then pitched with ½ teaspoon of Fleischman (later Shaw's brand) instant bread yeast. Bread yeast was chosen for its low cost and consistency — in previous fast ferment tests it attenuated similarly to other ale yeasts.

![Figure 5 — Fermentation of the sample with bread yeast](../brewing/images/mash-parameters-attenuation-efficiency/fig-128.png)

*Figure 5 — Fermentation of the sample with bread yeast.*

Samples were fermented at ~20°C (70°F) for 4–6 days, occasionally shaken to rouse the yeast. Final extract (final gravity) was measured with a precision hydrometer (0.990–1.020 range). Both hydrometers were calibrated with various sugar solutions (0, 2.5, 5, 10, 20°P) and readings were corrected for temperature.

### pH experiments

Constant parameters:
- Grain: Weyermann Bohemian Pilsner Malt, 70g
- Mill gap: 0.55 mm
- RO water; pH adjusted with white distilled vinegar (5% acetic acid) or baking soda (5% w/w NaHCO₃)
- Strike water: 240 ml
- Starting mash temp: 73–74.2°C
- Mash time: 60 min
- Sparge water: 250 ml
- Lauter efficiency: ~90%
- Boil time: 15 min

pH was measured at the end of the mash (room temperature) using a Milwaukee pH53 meter (with external calibration correction) and validated with EMD colorpHast strips (read in tungsten light).

pH correction formula used:

```
pH_corrected = 4 + 3 × (pH_reading − pH_4.01_buffer) / (pH_7.01_buffer − pH_4.01_buffer)
```

### Mash thickness experiments

Constant parameters:
- Grain: Weyermann Bohemian Pilsner Malt; 70g (2.57 l/kg and 5 l/kg series), 120g (second 2.57 l/kg series)
- Mill gap: 0.55 mm
- RO water, no pH adjustment; mash pH 5.4
- Total water used: 5.6 l/kg for all experiments
- Lauter efficiency: ~80%
- Mash time: 60 min; boil time: 15 min

To keep lauter efficiency constant across mash thicknesses, this series used the **no-sparge** method — all malt and water were combined before straining into the boiling pot.

### Malt type experiments

Constant parameters:
- Grain weight: 70g
- Mash average temp: 70°C
- Mill gap: 0.55 mm
- RO water, treated as necessary to keep pH values comparable
- Strike water: 240 ml
- Mash time: 60 min
- Sparge water: 250 ml
- Lauter efficiency: ~90%
- Boil time: 15 min

One mash was done without pH adjustment; based on the resulting pH, the strike water for a second mash was treated to target mash pH 5.3–5.5.

---

### Calcium content experiments

Constant parameters:
- Grain: Weyermann Pilsner, 70g
- Mash average temp: 69°C
- Mill gap: 0.55 mm
- Strike water: 240 ml
- Mash time: 60 min; sparge water: 250 ml
- Lauter efficiency: ~90%
- Boil time: 15 min

To create mash water with varying calcium levels but constant residual alkalinity (so as not to change the mash pH), 1.05g CaCl₂ and 1.00g chalk were added to 2 l of distilled water (~343 mg/l Ca, RA ≈ 0). This treated water was blended with distilled water at varying ratios for strike water; the sparge water was blended at the reverse ratio to keep the Ca concentration in the final wort constant.

## Results and Discussion

### Time

![Figure 6 — Mash time vs. limit of attenuation and brewhouse efficiency at two temperatures](../brewing/images/mash-parameters-attenuation-efficiency/fig-253.png)

*Figure 6 — The mash time dependency of the limit of attenuation and brewhouse efficiency shown for 2 different mash rest starting temperatures (67°C and 72°C).*

Two trends were observed:

1. The **lower temperature mash** (67°C / 152°F) showed a slower initial increase in extraction efficiency and fermentability, but in the long run achieved a **more fermentable wort**. This is because beta-amylase is initially less active at lower temperatures but remains active longer, producing more maltose over time.

2. The **higher temperature mash** (72°C / 162°F) initially outperformed in extraction efficiency. This is due to stronger alpha-amylase activity and possibly better starch gelatinization. Alpha-amylase is the main enzyme for converting starch into water-soluble glucose chains.

At temperatures where beta-amylase denaturation dominates (70°C/160°F and above), beta-amylase becomes more of a "sprinter" — faster at maltose production initially, but denatured too quickly to sustain production over the full mash.

![Figure 7 — Iodine reactions of the mash liquid over time](../brewing/images/mash-parameters-attenuation-efficiency/fig-285.png)

*Figure 7 — The iodine reactions of the mash liquid as recorded after the mash time was over. The hotter mash was able to reduce the iodine reaction quicker than the 67°C mash, another result of increased alpha-amylase activity.*

![Figure 8 — Temperature profiles for the time series experiments](../brewing/images/mash-parameters-attenuation-efficiency/fig-317.png)

*Figure 8 — Temperature profiles for the various mashes done in this series. The average temperature drop was about 3°C per hour.*

### Temperature

![Figure 9 — Mash profiles for the temperature experiment](../brewing/images/mash-parameters-attenuation-efficiency/fig-318.png)

*Figure 9 — The mash profiles for the different mashes done for determining the attenuation dependency on the mash temperature. Average mash temperature drop was 4°C (8°F). Wort pH was 5.5.*

![Figure 10 — Attenuation and estimated efficiency vs. mash temperature](../brewing/images/mash-parameters-attenuation-efficiency/fig-350.png)

*Figure 10 — The limit of attenuation and estimated efficiency for the mash temperature experiment. Data points track along a curve that matches literature references closely.*

Key observations:

- All data points track along a curve with high repeatability and low statistical error.
- The curve matches the few data points listed in Narziss [2005] fairly closely.
- The attenuation slope after its peak is **almost perfectly linear** with a slope of **~4%/°C** (2.2%/°F). This means increasing the temperature by 1°C will lower the limit of attenuation by ~4%. This relationship holds over a fairly wide temperature range (12°C / 24°F).
- Lower mash temperatures (at 60 min) lead to lower mash efficiency, since not as many starches have gelatinized and alpha-amylase (the main starch-liquefying enzyme) is less active. This lower efficiency at lower temperatures can be compensated by mashing for a longer time.

![Figure 11 — Lower limit of final extract vs. mash temperature](../brewing/images/mash-parameters-attenuation-efficiency/fig-382.png)

*Figure 11 — The expected lower limit for the final extract of a beer brewed from a 12°P (1.048 SG) wort as a function of mash temperature.*

#### Mash results

The pH mashing experiments used a single infusion mash starting at ~73.5°C (164°F) — well on the attenuation slope established in the temperature series.

![Figure 13 — Iodine reaction, limit of attenuation, and brewhouse yield as a function of mash pH](../brewing/images/mash-parameters-attenuation-efficiency/fig-479.png)

*Figure 13 — Iodine reaction, limit of attenuation, and brewhouse yield as a function of the mash pH.*

Key findings:

- **Optimum for limit of attenuation**: pH **5.4–5.6** (matches the pH range for beta-amylase in Narziss [2005]).
- Outside this range, the limit of attenuation declines. The slope is steeper towards lower mash pH.
- **Optimum for brewhouse yield**: near pH **5.2–5.3** — slightly lower than the fermentability optimum. This may reflect better alpha-amylase activity, though Narziss reports alpha-amylase pH optimum at 5.6–5.8.
- At extreme low pH (~4.5), the iodine test at the end of the mash and at the beginning of the boil both showed native starch present — confirming that pH well below 5.0 provides insufficient enzymatic activity to convert the mash.

![Figure 14 — Wort color comparison at pH 5.5 vs. pH 6.5](../brewing/images/mash-parameters-attenuation-efficiency/fig-447.png)

*Figure 14 — A comparison of the wort colors after a 15 min boil for experiment 23 (pH 5.5) and experiment 22 (pH 6.5). The darker color of the higher-pH wort is remarkable — a result of stronger Maillard reactions at higher pH.*

![Figure 15 — Starch test for experiment 28 (pH 4.55)](../brewing/images/mash-parameters-attenuation-efficiency/fig-511.png)

*Figure 15 — For experiment 28 (mash pH 4.55), the wort at the end of the mash and the beginning of the boil both tested positive for starch.*

### Mill gap spacing

![Figure 16 — Limit of attenuation and brewhouse yield vs. mill gap spacing](../brewing/images/mash-parameters-attenuation-efficiency/fig-512.png)

*Figure 16 — Limit of attenuation and brewhouse yield as a function of the mill gap spacing.*

Key findings:

- **Limit of attenuation** shows **little dependency** on tightness of the grind (at 68°C mash temperature).
- **Brewhouse yield** shows more dependency: tighter crush → higher brewhouse yield. The difference between a 0.35 mm (13 mil) and a 0.95 mm (37 mil) crush was only about **10%**.
- A tighter crush produces more flour and smaller grits, making it easier for mash water to access starches.
- The literature reports that finer grist leads to higher fermentability — this was **not** confirmed in these experiments. However, the finer grist produces **more fermentable extract** (since more total extract is produced). In mashes that approach 100% conversion efficiency at any mill setting, a finer grist would likely still show higher fermentability.

![Figure 17 — Iodine test on spent grain from experiment 33 (0.95 mm)](../brewing/images/mash-parameters-attenuation-efficiency/fig-544.png)

*Figure 17 — Iodine test on a piece of spent grain from experiment 33 (0.95 mm gap). The deep black-purple reaction indicates native starch still present in the grain.*

### Mash thickness

![Figure 18 — Effect of mash thickness on attenuation and efficiency](../brewing/images/mash-parameters-attenuation-efficiency/fig-576.png)

*Figure 18 — The effect of mash thickness on attenuation and efficiency. Thick mash (2.57 l/kg) vs. thin mash (5 l/kg).*

Key findings:

- **Contrary to common belief**, no attenuation difference was seen between a thick mash (2.57 l/kg / 1.21 qt/lb) and a thin mash (5 l/kg / 2.37 qt/lb). This aligns with technical brewing literature: mash concentration doesn't much affect fermentability in well-modified malts.
- A **significant difference** was found in **efficiency**: the thick mash showed almost constant brewhouse efficiency of 58–60% across the temperature range, while the thin mash showed a strong temperature dependency and was always more efficient than the thick mash.
- **Conclusion**: thinner mashes perform better and allow for better extraction of the grain. Most of the conversion potential is reached at a water/grist ratio of ~2.5 l/kg [Briggs, 2004].

### Malt type

![Figure 19 — Limit of attenuation of mashes with 3 different malts at 69°C](../brewing/images/mash-parameters-attenuation-efficiency/fig-608.png)

*Figure 19 — The limit of attenuation of mashes with 3 different malts (Pilsner, Munich I, Dark Munich) at a mash temperature of 69°C.*

![Figure 20 — Brewhouse efficiency for 3 different malts at 69°C](../brewing/images/mash-parameters-attenuation-efficiency/fig-609.png)

*Figure 20 — The brewhouse efficiency achieved with 3 different malts at a mash temperature of 69°C.*

Key findings:

- **Light Munich** (Weyermann Munich I) and **Pilsner** malt show little difference in attenuation.
- **Dark Munich** shows a significant drop in limit of attenuation, due to significantly reduced beta-amylase (and to some extent alpha-amylase) content from higher kilning.
- Brewhouse efficiency was **not as strongly affected** by malt type, though higher-kilned malts showed slightly lower efficiency.

### Calcium

![Figure 21 — Limit of attenuation and efficiency for 4 mashes with different calcium concentrations](../brewing/images/mash-parameters-attenuation-efficiency/fig-641.png)

*Figure 21 — The limit of attenuation and efficiency for 4 mashes with different calcium ion concentrations in the mash water. Numbers above the data points show the measured pH.*

Key findings:

- A slight **downward trend** for fermentability was seen as calcium increased, but the difference (~1%) is well within experimental error. **Conclusion: calcium content has no meaningful impact on fermentability.**
- A slightly stronger effect was seen for efficiency (~3% span) with a trend of higher calcium → higher efficiency, but this is based on few samples with non-matching mash pH and cannot be considered conclusive.

## Conclusion

Attenuation and efficiency are affected by many mash parameters. For the single infusion mash, the key controls are:

| Parameter | Impact on attenuation | Impact on efficiency |
|-----------|----------------------|----------------------|
| Temperature | High (4%/°C slope) | Moderate (lower temp = lower efficiency at 60 min) |
| Time | Moderate | Moderate (more time = better conversion) |
| pH | High (optimum 5.4–5.6) | Moderate (optimum ~5.2–5.3) |
| Mill gap | Low | Moderate (~10% range 0.35–0.95 mm) |
| Mash thickness | None (for well-modified malt) | High (thin mashes convert significantly more) |
| Malt type | High (dark Munich significantly lower) | Moderate |
| Calcium | None (within experimental error) | Small |

**Practical recommendations:**

- **Temperature and time** are the best parameters for targeting specific fermentability. Ensure the rest is long enough for complete conversion (negative starch test) — this may take 15–30 min, but a longer rest may be needed for desired fermentability.
- **Mash pH** should always be controlled and kept at **5.4–5.7** (room temperature measurement) — equivalent to 5.05–5.35 at mash temperature. Control through brew water design (residual alkalinity) and/or acid/salt additions.
- **Mill gap**: If you can't control the mill, accept lower conversion efficiency. If you can, tighten the crush until efficiency is acceptable, but watch for stuck sparges.
- **Mash thickness**: Brewers with low efficiency may try using a **thinner mash** (3–4 l/kg / 1.5–2 qt/lb) — confirmed to improve conversion efficiency significantly.
- **Dark base malts**: When using large amounts of highly kilned malts, expect lower attenuation and efficiency. Counteract with lower mash temperature, longer mash time, or adding 10–20% enzymatically strong malt (Pale/Pilsner).
- **Water composition** beyond residual alkalinity has at most a small secondary impact on attenuation and efficiency.
