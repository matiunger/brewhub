# Managing the Boil

*Mastering Techniques. From Gordon Strong, "Brewing Better Beer." Hops are covered separately in [[hopping]].*

Once the wort is in the kettle, the boil concentrates it, develops color and flavor, drives off DMS, sterilizes, and forms break material for clarity.

## Key Takeaways

- **Skim** the hot-break foam (proteins and starch) to reduce boilovers and clarify the wort.
- **Avoid dumping bittering hops right before the break** — they add nucleation points and can cause a boilover. The froth expands then collapses; that collapse signals the hot break has formed.
- **Measure preboil gravity and volume** every brew. Calibrate the kettle with a marked measuring stick. Typical evaporation is **1–1.5 gal/hr** — measure your own.
- **Total gravity points = gravity × volume** and are constant. Use this to predict OG and correct it: add fermentables (sugar/DME ≈ 45 points/lb) or boil down further.
- **Boil length effects:** hot break forms in ~15 min, sterilization in 5–10 min, pH drops ~1 point. Longer/harder boils add color, melanoidin, and caramelization — and harsher hops past 90 min.

## Actionable Tips

- **Pils-malt beers:** boil **90 minutes** to drive off DMS; **don't cover the kettle** and chill quickly afterward. Pale non-Pils beers: ~75 min. Caramelizing styles (Scotch ale, barley wine): 120–150 min.
- Add **kettle finings** (Irish moss / Whirlfloc) in the last 15 minutes for a good cold break.
- **Chill fast and whirlpool:** recirculate the wort while running an immersion or counterflow chiller — it crash-cools, preserves hop character, limits DMS, and piles trub in a cone. See [[whirlpooling]].
- For intentional caramelization (strong Scotch ale), boil the first ~gallon of runnings down by ~75% in a side pot.
