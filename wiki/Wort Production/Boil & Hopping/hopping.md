# Hopping

*Mastering Techniques — using hops in the brewhouse. From Gordon Strong, "Brewing Better Beer." For variety selection see [[hop-guide]].*

Hops provide bitterness, flavor, and aroma. Your choices are the **form**, the **amount**, and the **technique** used during and after the boil.

## Using Hops — Fundamentals

- **Form (whole/plug vs. pellet) depends on your kettle.** A false bottom filters with whole hops; a kettle whirlpool handles pellets. Keep all hops **fresh and frozen**.
- **Water chemistry shapes hop character:**
  - Low-to-moderate **sulfate** accentuates hop bitterness and dryness; **high sulfate** turns harsh with a sulfur note.
  - **Carbonates / high pH** make bitterness harsh — avoid.
  - **Calcium chloride** gives a rounder, sweeter hop character.
- Strong's rule: **sulfate for English, chloride for German/Belgian, a mix for American** — and **avoid sulfate with noble hops.** See [[water-chemistry]] and [[salts]].

## Traditional Hopping

- **Bittering** 30–90 min (60 typical); **flavor** 10–20 min; **aroma** ≤10 min. But hops give *some* of all three whenever you add them.
- High-alpha aromatic varieties pose a dilemma — get aroma without over-bittering by using **all-late** and/or **first-wort** hopping.

## All-Late Hopping

- Add **all hops in the last 20 minutes**, increasing amounts to offset lower utilization. Result: smoother, cleaner bitterness with big, smooth late flavor and aroma.
- Keep total hops **under 8 oz / 5 gal**; watch for grassy/vegetal notes and volume loss from absorption.
- **Use the Tinseth method** for IBUs (pellet correction factor 1.24). Whole-hop utilization at 1.050:

| Time | Pellets | Whole |
|------|---------|-------|
| 60 min | 28.6% | 23.1% |
| 30 min | 21.9% | 17.7% |
| 20 min | 17.4% | 14.0% |
| 15 min | 14.1% | 11.4% |
| 10 min | 10.4% | 8.4% |
| 5 min | 5.7% | 4.6% |

  Rule of thumb: a 15-min addition ≈ half the IBUs of 60 min; a 5-min addition ≈ a quarter.
- Perceived bitterness can read *lower* than measured — bump the IBU target or sulfates. For >50 IBU beers, add some hops at the start to aid break formation.

## First Wort Hopping (FWH)

- Add up to **one-third of the hop charge** to the kettle **before lautering**; the hops steep in hot wort (140–158°F, pH 5.2–5.5) until the boil starts. Yields a smoother, refined bitterness and big flavor.
- **Calculate FWH as a 60-minute addition** (measured ~10% higher) but expect it to *taste* smoother. Strong finds **little aroma but huge flavor** — roughly 50% more flavor than the same weight at 20 minutes.
- Pick **flavorful or noble varieties** for hop-forward styles (German Pils, English bitters/IPA, American pale/IPA).

## Post-Boil Finishing — Hot vs. Cold

- **Hot techniques** (right after the boil, before chilling): knockout / "zero-minute" addition, **whirlpool hopping**, and the **hopback**. Less aroma loss than the boil — chill fast to keep the aromatics. See [[whirlpooling]] and [[hop-stand-chemistry-and-technique]] for oil chemistry and timing.
- **Budget for extra bitterness from late additions.** Whirlpool, hop stand, and dry hop additions all contribute real IBUs at today's dosing rates — discount your boil bittering charge accordingly (sometimes dropping the 60-minute addition entirely) rather than stacking a full boil bitterness on top.
- **Cold technique — dry hopping** (after primary): fresh aroma and a fuller impression, but risks grassy/vegetal notes. Tips:
  - Rack off the yeast first (if possible); hold **60–68°F (16–20°C)**.
  - Go by **gravity, not by day**: add when ~5 points of gravity remain to attenuate (e.g. dry hop at 1.015 for a beer finishing at 1.010). Residual fermentation CO₂ keeps the hops in motion and improves transfer; suspended yeast also scavenges some of the incorporated oxygen. This means knowing each strain's attenuation curve rather than using a fixed "day 3" or "day 5" rule.
  - Release the temperature controller around the same 5-points-remaining point so the beer finishes above 21°C (70°F) — combines the diacetyl rest with dry hopping and raises solubility of some hop oils. In cold ambient conditions, release the controller earlier so the beer doesn't stall below target.
  - After adding the hops, close fermenter to start getting more head pressure. Last fermentation (CO2) will stir the hops into the beer, and the accumulating pressure pushes the hop krausen back into the liquid — increasing contact area and keeping volatiles in solution. This only works in an isobaric/stainless fermenter; close it too early (mid-active fermentation) and esters/sulfur compounds get trapped, too late and there isn't enough fermentation left to build pressure.
  - **In plastic fermenters** (can't be pressurized), compensate with more hops for the same aromatic intensity, and improve the seal (more clamps/gasket work) to hold at least 200–300 g of pressure — even that modest amount helps. The riskiest moment is the transition from fermentation temperature to cold crash: the beer contracts, pulls a vacuum, and draws in air. For plastic-fermenter brewers, it's often better to lean on a bigger whirlpool/hop stand addition (more oxidation-stable, longer-lasting aroma) than to push dry hop rates.
  - **Biotransformation dry hop**: for NEIPA-style flavor from yeast-hop interaction, add a small amount (~2–3 g/L) 12–24 hours into active fermentation, once the blow-off is bubbling. This is a flavor-forward complement to — not a replacement for — the main end-of-fermentation dry hop. High-geraniol varieties like Amarillo are a strong pick here (see [[characterizing-hops]]).
  - Before opening the fermenter to add hops, **purge the trub/dead yeast cone first** — that material produces sulfur compounds and mercaptans, and a clean, hops-only cone maximizes aroma transfer area (hops settle out of suspension in under 24 hours). For a full cold-side, oxygen-controlled protocol (purging headspace, resuspending settled pellets, timing pulls), see [[cold-dry-hopping-and-oxygen-control]].
  - Let the hops come up to room temperature (weighed out, out of the freezer) before adding — frozen pellets tend to drop as a solid clump, while room-temperature pellets break up and disperse into the beer much more readily.
  - Avoid mesh bags/tubes for dry hopping — pellets compact into a low-surface-area paste inside a bag, and a smaller *unbagged* charge routinely outperforms a larger bagged one. Convenience of removal isn't worth the efficiency loss.
  - **Blanket with CO₂** to prevent oxidation; crash-cool to 32°F two days before pulling.
  - Contact **3–7 days** (10–14 max); **0.5–2 oz / 5 gal**; rouse without introducing oxygen.
  - **Hop pellets are never sanitized before dry hopping** — there's no practical way to sanitize them without destroying the properties you're adding them for, so they go in straight from the package. *(Via the Hernán Castellani interview.)*
  - **Watch for "hop creep"**: enzymes carried in hop material can convert some previously-unfermentable wort sugars into fermentable ones after dry hopping, triggering a small secondary fermentation. Symptoms: a beer finishing drier and more alcoholic than expected, and a real risk of diacetyl if the beer is chilled quickly right after this micro-fermentation kicks in (a newer, less settled theory also implicates hops' natural microbial load, not just their enzymes). Mitigate by giving the beer a few extra days at fermentation temperature after a warm dry hop, so any hop-creep activity has time to finish and the yeast can clean up diacetyl before cold-crashing — the same logic as the diacetyl-rest guidance in [[managing-fermentation]].
  - Cold dry hopping (post-crash, no fermentation activity) shifts the fruit character sweeter and more tropical (mango, papaya, pineapple) versus the more citrusy profile (lime, passionfruit, grapefruit) from hopping during active/warm fermentation — but it also tolerates zero oxygen pickup, since there's no yeast or CO₂ evolution to scavenge it.
  - Variations: the **Randall** (dispense-time) and Sierra Nevada's **hop torpedo** (recirculating cold-side hopback).
- The key decisions everywhere: what character to impart, prevent loss of volatile oils, prevent oxidation, and separate the hop mass. Common rules: **chill fast, maximize contact, avoid oxygen.**

## Multiple Additions

- Multiple additions add layered complexity — but **don't mix too many varieties** (flavors clash). For super-hoppy beers, try **multiple dry-hop rounds** (≈7 days each).
- **Single-hop beers** teach a variety's character better than anything. Strong favors **Cascade + Centennial** and **Amarillo + Simcoe**, usually keeping to fewer than four late varieties.
