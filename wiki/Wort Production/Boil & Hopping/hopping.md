# Hopping

*Mastering Techniques — using hops in the brewhouse. From Gordon Strong, "Brewing Better Beer." For variety selection see [[hop-guide]].*

Hops provide bitterness, flavor, and aroma. Your choices are the **form**, the **amount**, and the **technique** used during and after the boil.

## Using Hops — Fundamentals

- **Form (whole/plug vs. pellet) depends on your kettle.** A false bottom filters with whole hops; a kettle whirlpool handles pellets. Keep all hops **fresh and frozen**.
- **Water chemistry shapes hop character:**
  - Low-to-moderate **sulfate** accentuates hop bitterness and dryness; **high sulfate** turns harsh with a sulfur note.
  - **Carbonates / high pH** make bitterness harsh — avoid.
  - **Calcium chloride** gives a rounder, sweeter hop character.
- Strong's rule: **sulfate for English, chloride for German/Belgian, a mix for American** — and **avoid sulfate with noble hops.** See [[water-chemistry]] and [[salts]].

## Traditional Hopping

- **Bittering** 30–90 min (60 typical); **flavor** 10–20 min; **aroma** ≤10 min. But hops give *some* of all three whenever you add them.
- High-alpha aromatic varieties pose a dilemma — get aroma without over-bittering by using **all-late** and/or **first-wort** hopping.

## All-Late Hopping

- Add **all hops in the last 20 minutes**, increasing amounts to offset lower utilization. Result: smoother, cleaner bitterness with big, smooth late flavor and aroma.
- Keep total hops **under 8 oz / 5 gal**; watch for grassy/vegetal notes and volume loss from absorption.
- **Use the Tinseth method** for IBUs (pellet correction factor 1.24). Whole-hop utilization at 1.050:

| Time | Pellets | Whole |
|------|---------|-------|
| 60 min | 28.6% | 23.1% |
| 30 min | 21.9% | 17.7% |
| 20 min | 17.4% | 14.0% |
| 15 min | 14.1% | 11.4% |
| 10 min | 10.4% | 8.4% |
| 5 min | 5.7% | 4.6% |

  Rule of thumb: a 15-min addition ≈ half the IBUs of 60 min; a 5-min addition ≈ a quarter.
- Perceived bitterness can read *lower* than measured — bump the IBU target or sulfates. For >50 IBU beers, add some hops at the start to aid break formation.

## First Wort Hopping (FWH)

- Add up to **one-third of the hop charge** to the kettle **before lautering**; the hops steep in hot wort (140–158°F, pH 5.2–5.5) until the boil starts. Yields a smoother, refined bitterness and big flavor.
- **Calculate FWH as a 60-minute addition** (measured ~10% higher) but expect it to *taste* smoother. Strong finds **little aroma but huge flavor** — roughly 50% more flavor than the same weight at 20 minutes.
- Pick **flavorful or noble varieties** for hop-forward styles (German Pils, English bitters/IPA, American pale/IPA).

## Post-Boil Finishing — Hot vs. Cold

- **Hot techniques** (right after the boil, before chilling): knockout / "zero-minute" addition, **whirlpool hopping**, and the **hopback**. Less aroma loss than the boil — chill fast to keep the aromatics. See [[whirlpooling]].
- **Cold technique — dry hopping** (after primary): fresh aroma and a fuller impression, but risks grassy/vegetal notes. Tips:
  - Rack off the yeast first (if possible); hold **60–68°F (16–20°C)**.
  - Add on last days of fermentation (1015-1018). After adding the hops, close fermenter to start getting more head pressure. Last fermentation (CO2) will stir the hops into the beer. Pressure will acumulate volatile aroma compounds.
  - **Blanket with CO₂** to prevent oxidation; crash-cool to 32°F two days before pulling.
  - Contact **3–7 days** (10–14 max); **0.5–2 oz / 5 gal**; rouse without introducing oxygen.
  - Variations: the **Randall** (dispense-time) and Sierra Nevada's **hop torpedo** (recirculating cold-side hopback).
- The key decisions everywhere: what character to impart, prevent loss of volatile oils, prevent oxidation, and separate the hop mass. Common rules: **chill fast, maximize contact, avoid oxygen.**

## Multiple Additions

- Multiple additions add layered complexity — but **don't mix too many varieties** (flavors clash). For super-hoppy beers, try **multiple dry-hop rounds** (≈7 days each).
- **Single-hop beers** teach a variety's character better than anything. Strong favors **Cascade + Centennial** and **Amarillo + Simcoe**, usually keeping to fewer than four late varieties.
