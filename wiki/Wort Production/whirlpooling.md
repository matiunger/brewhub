# Whirlpooling

*From Braukaiser — German brewing and more*

Whirlpooling employs 2 methods of separating the trub from the wort. The first one is **sedimentation**, which means the trub will sink to the bottom when left undisturbed. The second one is **centrifugal force** which forces the trub into the center of the pot. If both methods are used, the trub will be collected in a nice trub-cone in the center of the pot. This is the main trub separation technique used in commercial brewing today before the wort is chilled. In a home brewing set-up, whirlpooling can be used before or after the wort is chilled. The latter also allows for the partial removal of cold break before the wort is transferred to the fermenter.

While sedimentation alone would work, the trub would be evenly spread on the bottom of the pot, preventing you from siphoning from as low a point as you could if the trub was collected in a cone.

## Whirlpooling After Wort Chilling

These are the steps for using whirlpooling after chilling the wort.

### Chilling the wort

The wort is chilled with an immersion chiller. Note that the spoon has been left in the boil which sanitizes it for later use. If you don't want to do that you can sanitize the spoon with a sanitizer or use other sanitary means of starting a whirlpool (racking cane, oxygenation wand, turkey baster…).

### Bring the wort into rotation

Then the pot is moved into an elevated position. The whirlpool can be started with a sanitized spoon or with an oxygenation wand. The latter is very practical since no spoon has to be sanitized and the oxygenation of the wort is already done during the whirlpool. After the whirlpool has been started it must not be moved or disturbed in order to allow for an even rotation of the wort. It is also important that there are no obstacles to the even rotation of wort in the kettle (e.g. fittings or pick-up tubes).

### Rest the wort

The wort needs to rest for at least 20–30 min. Make sure it is covered. Now is also a good time to sanitize the racking and fermentation equipment.

![Covered pot during the rest](../brewing/images/whirlpooling/fig-002.png)

*Covered pot resting — 20–30 minutes minimum*

![Rest phase](../brewing/images/whirlpooling/fig-003.png)

*The wort rests undisturbed while the trub cone forms*

### Rack to the fermenter

Now that the trub has settled enough, the wort can be racked to a fermenter. When the racking cane is initially put in, it should not reach all the way to the bottom to avoid early disturbance of the trub cone. As the wort level sinks, the end of the racking cane can be lowered further. The siphon can be started with an auto siphon or even simpler with a turkey baster — squeeze the bulb, attach the tip to the end of the hose and release the bulb. It will suck wort through the hose and start the siphon.

> If you are using a pot with a spigot, the spigot will have to sit higher than the trub for this to work.

![Racking setup](../brewing/images/whirlpooling/fig-004.png)

*Racking setup — racking cane positioned above the trub cone*

### As the wort level drops

As the wort level reaches the bottom and you peek into the pot you should start seeing the top of the trub cone. This cone will slowly spread as the wort level drops further. When trub starts to get sucked up, it is time to stop the flow.

![Trub cone becoming visible](../brewing/images/whirlpooling/fig-005.png)

*The trub cone becoming visible as wort level drops*

### Collecting the wort from the trub

Since there will be a considerable amount of wort left in the trub it makes sense to collect it. If it is not needed for primary fermentation (e.g. the post-boil volume was larger than what goes into the primary fermenter), sanitary conditions are not required for this collection. A large funnel and paper towels work very well for filtering the wort from the trub-slush. Keep this wort frozen and use it for future starters or Kräusening — but make sure it is boiled before use. If you have a pressure canner you can also pressure-can the wort in Mason jars.

## Whirlpooling Before Wort Chilling

As mentioned earlier, whirlpooling can also be used before the wort is chilled. In this case the wort is chilled on the way to the fermenter by a counterflow or plate chiller. Note that all the cold break will end up in the primary fermenter. Cold break in the primary is not as concerning as hot break or hop material.

There is however a concern regarding **DMS**. DMS is still formed as long as the wort is hot and its precursor (SMM) is present — and such DMS formation continues while the wort rests in the whirlpool. Unlike during the boil, this DMS is not driven off and remains in the beer. This is especially a concern for beers made from very lightly kilned malts (Pilsner malt, for example) which contain higher levels of SMM than darker kilned malts (Munich malt). If you are using a counterflow chiller and experiencing a DMS off-flavor, you may want to boil for longer or switch to an immersion chiller.

## How Does It Work: Whirlpool Dynamics

Why does the whirlpool move trub to the *center* rather than the outside of the pot?

Because of the rotation of the wort, wort is forced outward against the wall of the pot. This results in a **pressure gradient**, with pressure lowest in the center and highest near the walls. Due to friction between the bottom of the pot and the rotating wort, the wort close to the bottom rotates more slowly than the wort higher up — meaning its pressure gradient is also less. Since wort, like any liquid or gas, moves from high pressure to low pressure, there will be a spiraling inward motion of wort along the bottom. The wort gets "sucked" into the center of the pot and is then forced outward again above the bottom layer.

Friction on the wall and bottom causes the rotation to slow down. As that happens, the inward current along the bottom weakens — soon it can only move the break material into the center but no longer lift it up, and thus the characteristic **trub cone** forms.

> Since cold break is very fine, it is not really affected by the whirlpooling. Some settling of that material to the bottom occurs during the rest time.
