# Mash Time Dependency of Wort Fermentability

*From German brewing and more*

Does continuing alpha amylase activity after the mash has been converted increase the wort fermentability? The literature suggests that alpha amylase has only little affinity towards shorter chained dextrines, which should result in only a small effect on the fermentability of the produced wort. This experiment tries to answer this question.

## Abstract

This experiment investigates whether extended mash time — beyond iodine-negative conversion — increases wort fermentability through continued alpha amylase activity on residual dextrins.

---

## Equipment

- 2 gal cooler as mash tun
- Weyermann Pilsner malt (0.75 kg)
- Thermometer
- Ladle
- Pot for boiling the mash
- Strainer and paper towels for lautering the mash
- Bottles for fermenting the samples
- Lager yeast (Wyeast 2206 from a homebrew batch)
- Sanitized syringe for measuring the yeast
- Oxygenation stone and oxygen bottle

---

## Procedure

The bottles and funnel are kept in a pot of boiling water to sanitize them and keep them heated so they don't shatter when filled with hot wort.

2.4 L strike water were mixed with 750 g Pilsner malt to reach a mash temperature of 155 °F (68.3 °C). Since this was lower than the target, 0.2 L boiling water was added after 4 min to get to 159 °F (70.5 °C). A high mash temperature was desired to start with a poorly fermentable mash — one that is thus able to show any drop in fermentability over time.

The mash pH was measured at about 5.3 with a ColorpHast test strip.

The cooler was placed in an ambient temperature of 170 °F (77 °C) to avoid a temperature drop. Despite this effort the temperature dropped to 152 °F (66.6 °C) before the first sample was taken, then remained at that temperature.

Starch conversion was tested with iodine on chalk, and after 40 min the mash was iodine negative. At this point the first sample was pulled: 300 ml of mash were strained into a pot, the spent grains re-mixed with 300 ml water and strained again, and the resulting wort was brought to a boil for 15 min. After boiling it was filtered into a bottle through a paper towel (strainer and funnel sanitized in boiling water). Each bottle was capped with aluminium foil. The whole procedure took about 25 min per sample.

Four more samples were taken every 30 min. During this time the mash temperature remained at 152 °F (66.6 °C).

All samples were left to cool for 6 hrs (overnight). Original extract was then measured for each sample. 5 ml of Wyeast 2206 yeast slurry were injected into each bottle after 10 s of oxygenation through an oxygenation stone.

The samples were fermented at room temperature (~67 °F / ~19 °C) and shaken occasionally.

After 4 days the samples were fully fermented and their final gravity was measured with a precision hydrometer (range 0.990–1.020). All measurements were corrected for temperature, a systematic hydrometer error (determined by measuring the SG of water), and converted to degrees Plato.

## Conclusion

Though only slight, mash time does have an effect on the **limit of attenuation** (fermentability) of the produced wort. Mashing for an additional 2 hours after conversion is complete increased apparent attenuation by 4%. For a 12 °P (1.048 OG) wort this means a final extract difference of about 0.5 °P (0.002 SG). Though not substantial, this is significant and could easily result from a stuck sparge or other reason why the targeted mash time was not achieved. Most of this attenuation increase was achieved within the first 90 min; after that it started to level off.

The leveling off can be explained by the reduction of enzymatic activity (heat damage) and the reduced availability of extract that can be converted. Measurement errors must also be accounted for, since an actual decrease of fermentability should not be possible.

The key question is whether **beta or alpha amylase** caused the further reduction of dextrins. According to the literature, beta amylase should have been deactivated after the first 10 min at 70 °C (158 °F). If true, continued alpha amylase activity was the reason for the continued production of fermentable sugars. Though the drop in mash temperature was unplanned, it better emulates a real mash where temperature begins to drop over time. The brewer should therefore log mash time alongside the mash temperature profile.

> To further validate that alpha amylase produces fermentable sugars under extended mash conditions, further experiments should mash at an even higher temperature (71–73 °C) and maintain that temperature throughout the complete mash time to eliminate beta amylase activity as much as possible.
