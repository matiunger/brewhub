# Lautering Options

*Part of [[lautering]]. From Gordon Strong, "Brewing Better Beer."*

Four ways to run the sweet wort off into the kettle, trading efficiency against time and flavor.

## Key Takeaways — The Four Methods

- **Continuous (fly) sparge** — maintain a ~1" layer of water over the bed and run off slowly. **Highest efficiency**, but the most time and monitoring. Channeling and stuck sparges are the risks.
- **Parti-gyle** — make multiple worts/beers from one mash; the strongest beer comes from the **first runnings**. Pure second runnings taste grainy — blend them or cap the mash with crystal/roast malts.
- **Batch sparge** — add sparge water in one or two batches, stir, rest ~10 min, vorlauf, run off. **Easiest and fast**, but roughly **5–10 percentage points less efficient** than continuous.
- **No-sparge** — drain only the first runnings and top up the kettle with brewing liquor. **Least efficient** (needs +33–40% grist), fastest, but yields the **richest, maltiest, least harsh** wort and slightly deeper color.

## Actionable Tips

- Prevent **channeling** by sparging slowly and cutting a tight checkerboard in the grain bed; add **rice hulls** with gummy grains (oats, rye) to avoid a stuck sparge.
- For maximum maltiness, stack the three independent boosters: **grist reformulation, decoction ([[decoction-mash]]), and no-sparge.**
- If blending parti-gyle batches, **blend the wort** (before or after the boil), not the finished beer — first and second runnings differ in salts, lipids, and proteins.
