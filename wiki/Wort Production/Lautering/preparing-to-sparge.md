# Preparing to Sparge

*Part of [[lautering]]. From Gordon Strong, "Brewing Better Beer."*

Before running off, confirm the mash is fully converted and get your sparge water right — temperature *and* pH both matter.

## Key Takeaways

- **Confirm conversion** with an [[iodine-test]] (any purple = starch remains; never return test liquid to the mash) or by senses: a finished mash looks **clear and bright** (not hazy/white), smells and **tastes sweet**.
- **Sparge water temperature:** 168–170°F (76–77°C) at the tun. Too cold dissolves sugars poorly and leaves enzymes active; too hot (with high pH) extracts tannins, bursts starch, and causes a slow/stuck lauter.
- **pH matters more than temperature for tannins.** Treat sparge water to **pH < 5.8 (5.5 ideal)** at room temperature to avoid harsh, astringent tannins.

## Actionable Tips

- **Phosphoric acid** is the easiest way to lower sparge-water pH without off-flavors (lactic acid a distant second). **Gypsum does NOT lower water pH** — it only acts in the mash.
- Strong's benchmark: **¼ tsp phosphoric acid drops 5 gallons of RO water from ~6.5 to ~5.7.** Carbonates buffer pH, so RO water is easiest to adjust.
- **Measure pH at room temperature** (a mash-temp reading is ~0.3 lower, sparge-temp ~0.4 lower). Standardize one acid dose for your water and reuse it.
