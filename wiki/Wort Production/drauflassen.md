# Drauflassen

*From German brewing and more*

**Drauflassen** (German for "letting something flow onto") is a common practice in large and small breweries — especially when fermenter capacity greatly exceeds brewhouse capacity, or when fresh yeast is introduced into the brewery. It allows pitching at a proper pitching rate with a reduced amount of yeast.

## Overview

Cooled and aerated wort is added to fill only ⅓–½ of the fermenter capacity, then the yeast is pitched. After 24 hours, when the first signs of fermentation are visible and the first batch is at **low Kräusen**, the second batch — which has been fully aerated — is added. This technique is known as **Double Batch** in American brewing. Depending on how small the initial batch was, the process can be repeated multiple times.

Narziss reports that this technique is beneficial to attenuation and ester levels: esters are reduced because the yeast is kept longer in its growth phase, during which it consumes the ester precursor acetyl-CoA [Narziss, 2005].

---

## Home Brewer Adaptation

This technique can also be used by home brewers to deal with an insufficient yeast amount to pitch a full batch of wort. While commercial brewers brew the second batch shortly before it is needed, this approach is less practical for home brewers as it would mean two back-to-back brew days with only half the wort per day.

A better approach is **sanitary storage** of part of the wort. This is less problematic for lagers than for ales since the fermentation temperature — and thus the storage temperature — is lower. Lagers are also the styles most likely to need higher pitching rates.

## Step-by-Step Process

1. **Cool the wort** with extra attention to sanitation. Keep the pot covered as soon as the boil stops. Use a whirlpool to settle out trub and cold break.

2. **Transfer ⅓–½ of the wort** to the fermentation vessel and aerate it there. Avoid aerating the full volume at this point to minimize oxidation of the unpitched portion. If using a bucket or carboy, aeration by shaking is an attractive option due to the large headspace and lower weight.

3. **Pitch the yeast** and take a sample for a Fast Ferment Test.

4. **Store the remainder** in the covered kettle — place a trash bag over the lid to prevent contaminants entering through holes for immersion chiller lines. Intentionally avoid transferring to another vessel to minimize cleaning, sanitizing, and infection risk. Place the pot into the fermentation fridge at the same temperature as the beer.

5. **After 24 hours**, check for low Kräusen on the fermenting beer. Add the remainder of the wort. Aeration may be done in the kettle or after adding to the fermenter:
   - Aerating in the kettle will disturb the trub — let it settle again before transferring.
   - Aerating in the fermenter may release large amounts of CO₂ causing foam-over (though this is less common in practice).

6. **Return the fermenter** to the fridge. After a few days it should be at high Kräusen.

> **Sanitation check:** To assess the risk of infection from stored unpitched wort, perform a **forced ferment test**: add a sample of the stored wort to a sanitized vessel, keep it covered at a warm place. If it remains clear for more than 2–3 days, it was sanitary enough.

> **If no fermentation after 24 hours:** Still add the rest of the wort. Risk of spoilage increases at this point. Consider adding dry yeast to ensure viability for the next batch. If using a counterflow or plate chiller, transfer wort into two sanitized vessels — one for pitching and one for temporary storage.

> Introducing oxygen via the second aerated batch is not a concern — aerating additional batches of wort is standard industry practice, and such concerns would have been reported if they were an issue.

## Constant Drauflassen

An interesting practice found in some commercial breweries is a form of **constant Drauflassen** [Kunze, 2007]:

1. A batch of wort is pitched.
2. Once at **high Kräusen**, part of it is pumped to another vat and fresh aerated wort is added to replace the drawn-off young beer.
3. The yeast quickly absorbs the newly added oxygen and continues to grow.
4. Once at high Kräusen again, the process is repeated.

This method allows for constant pitching with very young, clean, and fresh yeast kept in the growth phase in the **"Kräusen vat"**. At the end of the week the Kräusen vat is cleaned and re-pitched at the beginning of the next week.

While this method has significant fermentation benefits, it is impractical for home brewers who do not brew frequently enough or brew a single recipe repeatedly.
