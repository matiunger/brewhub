# Adjusting Salts for Flavor

*From Gordon Strong, "Brewing Better Beer" — Finishing Beer. Part of [[final-adjustments]].*

Brewing salts can be added **at any point, even after fermentation** — though **solubility** becomes an issue post-fermentation. Remember the division of labor:

- **Mash salts** → drive **mash pH** into range.
- **Kettle salts** → primarily **flavor**.

> Like food, beer is **better seasoned while cooking than at the table** — but you *can* add **calcium chloride, calcium sulfate, magnesium sulfate, or sodium chloride** to finished beer for flavor.

## Guidance

- **Sulfates enhance hoppy beers; chloride accentuates malty beers.** See [[salts]] and [[water-chemistry]].
- **Don't overdo it** — too much of any salt makes beer actually taste **salty**.
- Suggested ceilings: lagers no more than **2:1 chloride:sulfate**; hoppy beers no higher than **8–9:1 sulfate:chloride** ("your mileage may vary").

## Method

**Season to taste in a sample glass**, stir well but **avoid aerating**, and see if you sense an improvement. If a change works, **make notes** so you can adjust the water next time you brew. (Strong dislikes excessive water adjustment and normally only adds salts in the mash and boil.)
