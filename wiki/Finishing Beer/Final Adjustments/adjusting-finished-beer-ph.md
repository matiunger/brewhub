# Adjusting Finished Beer pH

*From Gordon Strong, "Brewing Better Beer" — Finishing Beer. Part of [[final-adjustments]].*

Not a common adjustment, but it helps specific problems. **High pH (above 4.5) is more prone to infection.** Proper mashing and fermentation should land it in range, but fermentation problems — or **yeast autolysis** (which raises pH) — can push it high. Lowering pH won't fix the underlying flavor issues, but it **keeps the beer from getting worse**.

## Target Range

Most beer finishes **4.0–4.5** (sours obviously lower). Strong prefers flavor on the **higher side (4.3–4.5)**; German texts recommend **4.2–4.4**. **If it's ≤4.5 and tastes good, don't worry about it.**

**Flavor by pH** (test in a small glass):
- **Above 4.5** → heavier, harsher, lacking freshness.
- **Lowering** → flavor evens out, tastes better.
- **Below 4.0** → thins out, turns tart.

## How to Lower pH

- Use **food-grade 10% phosphoric acid** (safe to drink; used in soft drinks). **Avoid lactic acid** — it can add infection-like off-flavors.
- Unless your meter has ATC, warm the beer to **60°F (16°C)** for an accurate reading.
- **Add acid → stir → measure → taste. Repeat.** When in doubt, stay on the high side.

## How to Raise pH (trickier)

Common chemicals add flavors; neutral ones are hard to handle. **Calcium carbonate / potassium carbonate** raise pH only if dissolved and can leave flavors (test a small sample first). **Potassium hydroxide** raises pH cleanly but is a powerful base and not generally available.
