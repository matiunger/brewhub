# Cask Conditioning

*From Gordon Strong, "Brewing Better Beer" — Finishing Beer. Part of [[finishing-beer]] and [[carbonating-and-packaging]].*

Two ways to cask, depending on how soon you serve and how authentic you want to be.

## The "Phony" Way

Condition and carbonate in a **keg**, transferring to the cask **only for serving**. Advantages: ready on demand, move it freely, no carbonation adjustment to serve. Not authentic — but appropriate **if you can't handle a real cask properly**.

## The Real Way

After fermentation but **before conditioning**, rack into a sanitized, purged cask. Treatment depends on the beer's condition entering the cask:

- **At terminal gravity → must be primed** (skip if fermentable extract remains).
- **Not bright → fine it** (skip if you used a very flocculent strain).
- **Want late hop character → dry hop** (hop plugs are designed for this).

> **Fining and carbonation work at cross purposes.** Either carbonate then fine later (more traditional), or let it drop bright then prime (Strong's preference for convenience). Isinglass is the traditional cask fining; corn sugar is the easiest priming sugar.

**After sealing:** condition at **fermentation temperature** to build carbonation, then move to **cellar temperature** to mature. If you fined, **don't move the cask much** afterward — finings lose effectiveness each time they re-clear the beer.

> **Cask abuse is common in the US** — bars moving casks daily or leaving them warm on the bar, or trucking them to festivals for same-day service. All abuse the beer. **Real cask beer is best served from the cellar, rested.** If you can't handle it properly, use the phony method.

## Homebrew-Scale Cask Gear

- **Pin cask** is easiest (volume ~ a 5-gal Corny keg). A **firkin** is twice as big — better price per volume, but you'll need help moving it.
- Modern casks are usually **stainless**, not wood.
- Oddly named parts seal the cask and manage gas: **shives, spiles, bungs, keystones**. **Taps** serve via gravity or a **beer engine**. A **cask breather** blankets CO₂ at atmospheric pressure to preserve beer not consumed within a few days.

## Barrel Aging
