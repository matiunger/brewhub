# Carbonating & Packaging

*From Gordon Strong, "Brewing Better Beer" — Finishing Beer. Part of [[finishing-beer]]. For carbonation math and keg detail, see [[kegging]] and [[calculating-sugar-additions]].*

Carbonating and packaging are closely related: you **bottle or keg** (and from a keg you can later bottle or fill growlers). **Earlier choices may close off some carbonation options.**

> **Make final adjustments before bottling** (it's permanent); if you **keg**, you can defer adjustments until you bottle or serve. See [[final-adjustments]].

## Bottle Conditioning

Strong mostly **kegs** (convenient, ~10 min vs ~2 hours to bottle 5 gal), but bottle-conditions when it matters:

- **Trappist-style Belgians** — more authentic ("refermented in the bottle"; required for true Trappist beers).
- **Big beers for long aging** — yeast adds stability and a **finer carbonation texture**.

**Method:** rack to a bottling bucket, add **priming sugar**, siphon to bottles, cap, naturally carbonate at room temp **1–2 weeks**, then chill.

**Priming sugar:** usually **corn sugar** (ferments fast, little residual character — top choice). Any sugary fermentable works (brown sugar, DME, honey, Lyle's Golden Syrup) and can add flavor (De Dolle Stille Nacht is primed with honey). Calculate amounts with [[calculating-sugar-additions]].

## Force Carbonation in a Keg

When the beer has dropped bright: rack to a sanitized, **purged** keg, pressurize with CO₂, chill overnight, then force carbonate.

**Strong's rock method:** hook a **chilled** keg to CO₂ at **~30 psi**, hold by both ends and **rock back and forth** (~50 times), listening for gas to bubble in. Return to the fridge, leave several days, then vent excess and sample.

- **Overpressurized?** Attach a pressure-relief valve to the gas-out stem.
- **Underpressurized?** Repeat. (He keeps kegs **slightly over**carbonated — easier to hold carbonation when transferring to bottles/growlers.)

## Natural Carbonation in a Keg

**Prime the keg** like a bottle. More sediment results, but it's a valid natural approach — then serve, jumper to a second keg to keep clear, bottle, or transfer to a cask.

## Bottling from a Keg

Beer is **already carbonated and bright** (unlike bottle conditioning — how most commercial breweries bottle). **Less shelf life**, so **avoid oxygen and store very cold**. Use a counter-pressure filler or the **Blichmann BeerGun** (Strong's favorite) — for archive-quality competition beers or to free up a keg.

**To growlers / 2-L soda bottles:** vent keg pressure, serve via a **keg faucet** (larger bore than a picnic tap, less foam), add a **carbonator cap** for extra pressure; a hose to fill from the bottom reduces foaming.

## Kräusening & Spiese (traditional German)

**Kräusening** adds actively fermenting beer to finished beer for conditioning/carbonation (before or after lagering); **spiese** primes with unfermented wort. Advantage: using the same wort **doesn't dilute flavor**, and fresh yeast reduces green flavors. But it's complex (save wort, make a starter, harder to dose) — Strong skips it normally but might use it to fix a **green-tasting** finished beer. See [[kraeusening]].
