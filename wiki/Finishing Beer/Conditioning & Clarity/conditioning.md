# Conditioning

*From Gordon Strong, "Brewing Better Beer" — Finishing Beer. Part of [[finishing-beer]].*

Conditioning is the phase right after fermentation where **green-beer flavors are reduced** and the beer becomes ready to drink.

## The "Secondary Fermentation" Misnomer

What homebrewers call "secondary fermentation" is really a **conditioning / bright tank**. Breweries move beer off the yeast quickly (to reuse the fermenter), so their secondary matters for clarification and maturing flavor (reducing **acetaldehyde, diacetyl**, and other green flavors from early yeast separation).

- **Homebrewers usually leave beer in the primary longer**, until the yeast drops — so the primary already acted as the secondary, and **a separate secondary is often unnecessary**. No green flavors? Go straight to packaging.

## When a Secondary *Is* Useful

- **Manipulating the beer** without primary trub/yeast risk: dry hopping, oaking, adding fruit/spices.
- **Holding/bulk aging** beer awaiting packaging (not enough clean kegs/bottles).

**To transfer:** purge the receiving vessel with **CO₂**, rack gently off all trub and yeast, and watch the racking tube — **stop when haze/particulates increase**.

## Autolysis Warning

Beer can condition in the primary for a few weeks (like a big bottle-conditioned beer) **if not too warm**. Too long or too warm → **autolysis**: a glutamate/umami flavor that ruins the profile. Larger yeast mass = higher risk — **beware long primaries on repitched yeast**.

## QC at the End of Fermentation

Your first quality-control checkpoint. Before racking off the yeast, smell and taste:

- **Diacetyl / acetaldehyde present?** Too soon to rack — condition longer.
- **Still need the yeast** to do something? Don't separate yet.
- **Autolysis flavors?** On the yeast too long.
- **Fixable items** (e.g. dry hop for more aroma)? Do them now.
- **Check gravity** is terminal; watch for the beer to **drop bright**.
- **Unfixable off-flavors** (infection, papery oxidation, chlorophenols)? Triage — see [[final-adjustments]].

## Strong's Practice

He **kegs**, so he bulk-ages in carboys until fairly bright, then kegs. Prefers house beers **unfiltered** — a little yeast in the keg aids stability and scavenges oxygen, and settled yeast/trub pushes out with the first pour. If he'll **move then serve** a keg, he fines or filters so it doesn't look murky. Fining before kegging? A secondary makes sense; for cask ales, **isinglass + priming sugar** can go in together.
