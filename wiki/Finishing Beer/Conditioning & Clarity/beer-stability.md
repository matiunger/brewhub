# Factors Affecting Beer Stability

*From Gordon Strong, "Brewing Better Beer" — Finishing Beer. Part of [[finishing-beer]].*

The two biggest levers for preserving beer character: **avoid post-fermentation oxygen** and **store cold**. Smaller helpers: avoid large temperature swings, store dark, and minimize physical handling (shaking, vibration).

## Oxygen — The Cardinal Sin

**Oxygen can ruin beer, period — once oxidized, nothing fixes it.** Most beers go dull, stale, tasteless, then papery, harsh, and sour.

- **Exceptions:** some dark beers with natural antioxidants take a little oxidation into pleasant **dark-fruit/sherry** notes; **sour beers** can gain fruitiness.
- **Pale/light beers are less stable** than dark/strong ones (antioxidants preserve them, like reds age better than whites).
- **Defenses:** purge storage vessels with **CO₂ or nitrogen**; keep beer on **yeast** (bottle conditioning) to scavenge oxygen — if not stored too warm. Winemaking sulfites/antioxidants *might* work but risk added flavors.

## Temperature

Cold slows chemical reactions (like oxidation) **2–3× for every 10°C / 18°F cooler**. A beer at 0°C (32°F) keeps 2–3× longer than at 10°C (50°F) — and vastly longer than in a hot 40°C (104°F) garage. Warmth helps natural carbonation and conditioning but speeds staling unless cold-side handling is careful.

## A Note on HSA (Hot-Side Aeration)

Real but **overstated**. Post-fermentation oxygen does the immediate damage — **focus there first**. Avoid hot-side oxygen when convenient, but **don't obsess**; only tackle HSA once your packaging/storage is solid and you actually notice problems.

## pH and Microbial Stability

Lower pH resists infection — commercial brewers target **≤4.5**. But **flavor stability and quality drop as pH falls**, so there's a tradeoff. Strong favors flavor, keeping beer at **4.3–4.5** (varies by style) and using other techniques to avoid infection. See [[adjusting-finished-beer-ph]].
