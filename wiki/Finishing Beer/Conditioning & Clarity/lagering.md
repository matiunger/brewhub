# Lagering

*From Gordon Strong, "Brewing Better Beer" — Finishing Beer. Part of [[finishing-beer]]. See also [[fermenting-lagers]].*

Lagering is the traditional **cold conditioning** technique for mellowing flavors and reducing green-beer compounds — **acetaldehyde, hydrogen sulfide, and diacetyl**. Done cold (near freezing), generally with lager yeast. Altbier and Kölsch get a similar cold-conditioning treatment.

During lagering: flavors mellow, **final attenuation** is reached, and yeast settles out. Beer can be **fined or filtered** during this period for best clarity.

## When Is It Done?

Watch **esters, sulfur, and diacetyl** — all should drop **below threshold**. When the beer tastes right, **stabilize it**: rack, chill, package. Even with a secondary, there's usually enough yeast present to finish conditioning and reduce green flavors.

## How Long & How Cold

- **Traditional German guideline** (Kunze, *Technology Brewing and Malting*): **one week of lagering per degree Plato** of original extract.
- **Cool slowly** — drop from fermentation temp to lagering temp **a few degrees per day**. This avoids stunning the yeast and dropping them out early.
- **Lagering does NOT start with crash cooling.** A fridge/freezer with an adjustable temperature controller makes this easy.
