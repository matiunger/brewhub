# Fast Ferment Test

*From Braukaiser — German brewing and more*

The idea of the **Fast Ferment Test** (FFT) is to completely ferment all fermentable sugars in a small sample of wort. The attenuation that results is the **limit of attenuation** for that wort — it depends only on the wort composition established by the brewing process (mainly mashing) [Narziss, 2005]. When properly done, the final attenuation of the test is not affected by yeast strain, yeast health, pitching rate, temperature, or other fermentation parameters.

> Lager yeasts can ferment some sugars (melibiose and raffinose) that ale yeasts cannot, but these sugars are either absent from brewer's wort or only present in trace amounts.

## How to Perform the Fast Ferment Test

You need a sample of wort and yeast. The wort sample should be enough for a hydrometer reading (6–8 oz / 120–200 ml). The yeast pitching rate for the sample should be 5–10x what you would use for a full batch.

**Quick setup when propagating yeast in a flask:**

1. Decant the spent starter beer
2. Add fresh wort to resuspend the yeast
3. Pitch most of the yeast/wort mixture to the main batch and leave a little in the flask
4. Add more fresh wort to ensure there is enough for a hydrometer reading

**If you can't spare any yeast** from the pitch (e.g. pitching from a vial or pack of dry yeast), dry bread yeast can be used as an alternative. Bread yeast behaves similarly to ale yeast and typically shows a final extract about 0.2–0.3 °P higher than lager yeast. Use 1/2 tsp dry bread yeast per 240 ml (8 oz) of wort. The error versus the actual yeast can be up to 0.5 °P / 2 gravity points.

**Fermentation procedure:**

- Cap the flask or bottle with tin foil or an airlock (an airlock keeps acetobacter down and confirms the sugar is fully fermented)
- Place at a warm location (20 °C / 70 °F or above; warmer for ale yeasts)
- Shake occasionally to keep the yeast in suspension, or place on a stir plate
- Fermentation is typically complete in 1–2 days; give it another 2 days until the sample lies completely flat and no CO2 escapes when shaken
- For high-gravity worts (18+ °P / 1.072+ SG), adding a small amount of air/O2 to the headspace can help kick-start stalled fermentation

**Measure the extract (gravity)** of the sample. This gives the lower limit of the final extract (final gravity) that can be expected from this wort. Depending on yeast characteristics and fermentation conditions, the actual beer FG may be higher. Give it a taste — it generally tastes like bad homebrew due to the higher fermentation temperatures.

## How Does This Test Help With Brewing?

Knowing the limit of attenuation for a wort has so many benefits that performing the test for every batch — not just lagers — is strongly recommended. It has become as essential as taking an OG reading or recording mash temperature.

**Key benefits:**

- **Diagnose unexpected final gravities** — Is the problem mashing or fermentation? A high FG with a high limit of attenuation points to fermentation issues (poor yeast health, underpitching, low temperature). A high FG with a low limit of attenuation points to mashing issues (high saccharification temperature, crystal malts, etc.). See [Understanding Attenuation](understanding-attenuation.md).

- **Start lagering or cold conditioning earlier** — Instead of waiting for gravity readings to stabilize, you are looking for a targeted gravity to be reached. Note that the target FG is not necessarily the FFT result; it may be somewhat higher depending on yeast strain and style.

- **Know your lager's FG before it finishes** — Palmer [Palmer, 2006] described brewing lagers as "flying blind" because you don't know when they are done. With an FFT, you can determine the FG of your lager before the actual batch finishes.

- **Confirm whether a fermentation stall can be fixed** — If yeast stalls before reaching the targeted FG, the FFT confirms whether the yeast can go further. You can then decide to accept it or intervene (warming, rousing, pitching fresh yeast).

- **Safely start cold conditioning before reaching FG** — When the gravity falls close enough to the FFT result that sufficient fermentable sugars remain for carbonation, cold conditioning can safely begin. The known limit of attenuation allows accurate calculation of the sugar needed for bottle conditioning (see *Accurately Calculating Sugar Additions for Carbonation*).

> When that level of precision is needed for gravity readings, consider investing in a hydrometer calibrated for the 0.980–1.020 range, which makes final gravity readings much more precise.

## Performing a Fast Ferment Test on Beer

This variant troubleshoots a high finishing gravity when no FFT was done upfront. The most common form: fermentation has stalled at an unexpectedly high FG and the brewer wants to determine whether a restart is worth attempting.

Pitch a large amount of yeast (bread yeast works here) directly into a small sample of the partially fermented beer, keep warm and rouse regularly, then measure the resulting extract once fermentation is complete. This shows whether the wort itself is capable of attenuating further.

## Appendix: Yeast Dependency of the Fast Ferment Test

The following results from parallel FFTs with different yeasts demonstrate that while small differences exist between strains, they are generally minor:

**Wort A:**

| Yeast | FFT AE |
|---|---|
| WY 2206 (Bavarian Lager) | 2.2 °P |
| Fleischmann Active Dry Bread Yeast | 2.4 °P |

**Wort B:**

| Yeast | FFT AE |
|---|---|
| WY 3056 (Bavarian Wheat) | 2.6 °P |
| Fleischmann Active Dry Bread Yeast | 2.8 °P |

**Wort C:**

| Yeast | FFT AE |
|---|---|
| WY 3056 (Bavarian Wheat) | 2.5 °P |
| WY 3333 (German Wheat) | 2.6 °P |
| WY 3068 (Weihenstephan Wheat) | 2.6 °P |
| Fleischmann Bread Yeast (1/4 tsp / 150 ml) | 2.5 °P |
| Fleischmann Bread Yeast (1/2 tsp / 150 ml) | 2.5 °P |

The best practice is to perform the FFT with the same yeast being pitched to the batch. Bread yeast is an acceptable substitute in a pinch.

> The 0.2 °P difference between WY3056 and bread yeast in Wort B, but not in Wort C, may relate to culture propagation method: slant culture vs. single-cell plate culture.
