# Recipe Formulation

*From Gordon Strong, "Brewing Better Beer" — Envisioning Your Beer (Chapter 6). The hub for designing a recipe: start from the concept, then progressively home in on the final recipe. It's iterative, not strictly linear.*

## Strong's 10-Step Process

1. **Decide on a target concept** — describe it in a phrase ("clone Schneider Aventinus," "oatmeal stout that tastes like oatmeal-raisin cookies").
2. **Determine general stylistic parameters** — OG, FG, ABV, IBU, color.
3. **Determine total fermentables** from the OG target (depends on system efficiency).
4. **Determine total hops** from the IBU target (work in **AAUs**).
5. **Let the flavor profile drive the ingredients** — grain bill, hops, yeast, fermentation, water.
6. **Adjust the grain bill for attenuation** (FG target).
7. **Select a mash method.**
8. **Select a hopping method.**
9. **Adjust water** if necessary.
10. **Account for special ingredients, processes, or techniques.**

Think in phases — ingredient selection, mashing, boiling, fermenting, conditioning — filling in pieces as you go, then review the **end-to-end** recipe.

> **Process beats recipe.** *Via the Birra Podcast, T2E05.* Solid process on ordinary ingredients reliably lands a very good beer; premium ingredients pushed through a sloppy process don't. Treat ingredient selection itself as one more process to execute well — there's little point chasing rarer or pricier ingredients before you've wrung the full potential out of what you're already using, since the best hop in the world contributes nothing if it oxidizes in the dry hop or in the keg (see [[dissolved-oxygen-management]]). This is also why published clone recipes for famous beers (e.g. Russian River's Pliny the Elder) rarely reproduce the original — the gap is in process, not the ingredient list.

## Before You Design: Get Repeatable, Get Familiar

*Via the Birra Podcast, T2E15 — an interview with Sebastián González.*

**Process repeatability comes before recipe design.** If your OG swings from 1.060 to 1.080 batch to batch on the "same" recipe, you're not ready to design recipes yet — you can't reliably aim for a target you can't reliably hit. A skilled brewer isn't defined by making one good beer; it's making the beer you *intended* to make, on purpose, repeatably: you decide the characteristics up front and then land them.

**Build direct sensory familiarity with ingredients before designing around them:**
- Buy small quantities (e.g. ¼ kg) of every malt you might use — chew the grain, smell it, taste it. Do the same for hops and yeast. Build a "sensory library" by comparing notes with other brewers on what they perceive in the same ingredients (see the malt tea test in [[malt-production-and-character]]).
- **Single-hop APA test**: brew the same base recipe every time and change only the hop variety, to build a real feel for each one. An APA works well for this because its lower bitterness lets hop flavor come through clearly. A reference recipe: 90% Pilsner + 10% Munich, 4 g/L whirlpool + 4 g/L dry hop, BU:GU ≈ 0.5 (see [[selecting-hops]] and [[hop-stand-chemistry-and-technique]] for the underlying technique).
- **Split-batch yeast test**: brew one batch of wort, split it across several small fermenters, pitch a different strain in each. This isolates strain character from wort variability far better than comparing across separate brew days (see [[choosing-a-yeast-strain]]).
- **Clone brewing builds the core recipe-design skill**: replicating a known commercial beer forces you to translate a sensory impression (color, caramel character, maltiness, bitterness) into concrete ingredient and process adjustments, iterating until it matches. The milestone that tells you you've built this skill: tasting your clone against the original side by side and genuinely not being able to tell which is which, or which you prefer. That translation skill — sensory impression to liquid — is the same one you need to design an original recipe from imagination rather than from an existing reference.

## Decide on a Target

What's the idea: clone a commercial beer, brew to **BJCP style**, modify an existing recipe, fuse styles, or follow an inspiration/food idea?

- **Cloning** → research hard (brewery site, beeradvocate/ratebeer, style books, forums, your own brewery visit notes). You're assembling puzzle pieces — overall parameters first, then ingredient/process clues. Take others' "clone recipes" with a grain of salt. **Do a structured tasting** of the target and record a detailed profile to compare against.
- **Brewing to style** → re-read the style description, especially overall impression and **balance**; don't tweak minor points at the expense of drinkability.
- However you get there, **define the target profile first.** Components include **processes**, not just ingredients. Goal: a consistent, predictable outcome — how you get there is your business.

## Determine Stylistic Parameters

Set goals for **OG, FG, ABV, IBU, color**. Attenuation, ABV, OG, and FG are all related and drive **balance**. For a clone, you often can't find every number — estimate color from photos, measure a commercial beer's **FG** by de-gassing and using a hydrometer. Hitting the numbers isn't enough to be "in style."

> **Map a style by its neighbors.** A useful way to picture where a beer sits in "style space" is to focus on one style and show only its **nearest neighboring styles** — then note which attribute, when changed, turns it into each adjacent style. It clarifies what actually defines your target and which knobs move it.

## Let Flavor Drive Ingredients (the hard part)

Match **inputs (ingredients) to outputs (final flavors)** — this leans entirely on knowing your ingredients' finished profiles. See [[mastering-ingredients]].

- **Yeast first** — pick by style (some are obvious like Kölsch); for a clone, use the brewery's strain if available (mrmalty.com yeast chart). Origin is Strong's single most important yeast factor. See [[choosing-a-yeast-strain]].
- **Fermentation method follows the yeast** — pick the temperature for the character you want (Strong aims for clean: pitch cool, let it rise). Decide whether fruitiness comes from **yeast or malt** before choosing other ingredients. See [[managing-fermentation]].
- **Grain bill in percentages** (scales easily; matches references like *Designing Great Beers*). Pick by **country of origin** of the style, then blend base/character/crystal/roasted malts for the profile. See [[selecting-malt]] and [[characterizing-malt]].
- **Hops** — first set the **number and quantity** of additions, then varieties. Per 5 gal: ¼ oz very light · ½ oz noticeable · 1 oz strong · 2 oz very strong. Watch total **hop mass** (grassy/vegetal/tannin from too much). See [[selecting-hops]].
- Specify ingredients precisely enough **to place an order** (maltster, hop form). Keep **as-planned and as-brewed** notes separate.
- **Water** — note any stylistic mineral profile (e.g. sulfate for Burton); kettle additions for flavor, on top of mash-pH needs. See [[adjusting-water]].

## Adjust for Attenuation, Then Method

- **Attenuation** — decide whether to add **sugar** (more attenuation) or **dextrins** (CaraPils, oats — less). This is what separates a double IPA from an American barleywine. Think **wort composition first**, yeast spec second. See [[adjusting-final-gravity-and-attenuation]].
- **Mash method** — can add flavor/color (decoction) and raise attenuation (step/decoction); rest temps set wort composition. Many effects are **replaceable** (add sugar/dextrin instead of changing mash temp; aromatic malt instead of decoction). Remember side effects (decoction darkens — bad for hefeweizen). See [[step-mash]] and [[decoction-mash]].
- **Hopping method** — finalize flavor/aroma additions, calculate their IBUs, then set the bittering addition. **FWH** gives big flavor (Strong cuts the amount 20–35%, calculates bitterness as a ~20-min addition). Knockout/dry hops add no IBUs. Recheck total IBU when done. See [[hopping]].
- **Adjust water last, only if needed** — Strong starts from RO and makes minor adjustments, mainly ensuring **calcium** in the mash. **Try low-mineral water.**

## Building Color in Layers

*Via the Birra Podcast, T2E15.* Treat a recipe's target color as the frame you're allowed to play within for malt complexity — the more color budget available, the more caramel/dark/Munich malt you can layer in, and the same color target can be hit with very different underlying malt bills (a lot of low-color caramel malt vs. a little high-color caramel malt give the same color but very different flavor — see [[malt-production-and-character]]).

A useful default technique to avoid the common mistake of over-relying on caramel malt: build up in layers rather than reaching straight for caramel. Start from **Pale malt instead of Pilsner** as the base, add some **Munich**, use **melanoidin malt** instead of caramel for maltiness, and only if a toasted note is wanted, add a small amount (~0.5%) of chocolate malt. This opens up a range of distinct results within the same color target, rather than defaulting to "more caramel" every time. If the style specifically calls for a strong caramel character (e.g. Scottish Ale), skip the layered approach and go straight to the caramel malts that deliver that specific profile instead (see [[designing-malty-beers]]).

**This layering choice also moves your mash pH**, not just flavor — starting from Pale instead of Pilsner lowers mash pH somewhat, which is useful leverage if you're working with high-alkalinity water. Recipe design is "a short blanket": move the malt bill and you've also moved the pH; adjust the pH and you need to recheck color; and both feed into how fermentation behaves. Treat these as one interconnected decision, not three sequential ones made in isolation.

## Priorities When Something's Wrong

*Via the Birra Podcast, T2E15.* **A beer never exceeds its worst mistake** — a serious fermentation flaw sets a quality ceiling that no amount of good malt or good water can lift. Fix problems in priority order, not in parallel or out of order (chasing premium malt while ignoring an oxidation or fermentation problem is solving the wrong thing first):

1. **Sanitation and cleaning protocol** — see [[cleaning]].
2. **Fermentation control** — temperature and pitch/inoculation rate. This is the step most capable of ruining everything else you did well; get it right before optimizing anything downstream (see [[managing-fermentation]]).
3. **Water treatment** — how much this matters depends heavily on your source water's starting point (see [[water-chemistry]], [[water-report-tigre]] for a documented regional example).
4. **Oxygen pickup control** — importance varies by style (a hop-forward, dry-hopped beer is far more exposed than a clean lager — see [[dissolved-oxygen-management]]).

A **great beer is the sum of many small details** done right together: oxygenating wort properly, using good malt, keeping hops vacuum-sealed and fresh. Individually each detail looks minor; together they define the outcome.

**A recipe is ingredients *plus* process — not ingredients alone.** The same grain bill, hops, yeast, and even the same water can produce different beers under different processes; having Russian River's published Pliny the Elder recipe doesn't get you Pliny the Elder without their process and equipment behind it (see the "process beats recipe" note above).

**Know your equipment and adapt your process to it, rather than treating better equipment as the answer.** Without an isobaric (pressurizable) fermenter, that might mean a heavier dry hop dose with more careful handling, or leaning more on a bigger hop stand instead (see [[hopping]], [[hop-stand-chemistry-and-technique]]). This isn't a hardware arms race — it's entirely possible to brew award-quality beer with basic equipment (glass carboys, gelatin fining) if the process fundamentals are solid. If a hoppy beer's aroma keeps falling flat, the first fix to try is learning to purge kegs properly with CO₂ (see [[cold-dry-hopping-and-oxygen-control]]) — a much cheaper fix than buying an isobaric fermenter, and one that may solve the actual problem, which is usually oxygen pickup rather than equipment capability. Hop technique's single biggest lever is simply avoiding oxygen pickup — a beer dosed at 20 g/L that picked up oxygen can show *less* hop aroma than a much lighter dose handled cleanly; before spending more on hops, confirm you're not oxidizing what you already have.

## Sanity Check

Review the finished recipe against your style targets. Software speeds calculations and revisions, but Strong outlines on paper first to keep creativity unconstrained, then hits targets in software. **Keep detailed notes** of how it turned out — and feed lessons into the next batch.
