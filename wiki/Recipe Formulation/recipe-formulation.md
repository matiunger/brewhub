# Recipe Formulation

*From Gordon Strong, "Brewing Better Beer" — Envisioning Your Beer (Chapter 6). The hub for designing a recipe: start from the concept, then progressively home in on the final recipe. It's iterative, not strictly linear.*

## Strong's 10-Step Process

1. **Decide on a target concept** — describe it in a phrase ("clone Schneider Aventinus," "oatmeal stout that tastes like oatmeal-raisin cookies").
2. **Determine general stylistic parameters** — OG, FG, ABV, IBU, color.
3. **Determine total fermentables** from the OG target (depends on system efficiency).
4. **Determine total hops** from the IBU target (work in **AAUs**).
5. **Let the flavor profile drive the ingredients** — grain bill, hops, yeast, fermentation, water.
6. **Adjust the grain bill for attenuation** (FG target).
7. **Select a mash method.**
8. **Select a hopping method.**
9. **Adjust water** if necessary.
10. **Account for special ingredients, processes, or techniques.**

Think in phases — ingredient selection, mashing, boiling, fermenting, conditioning — filling in pieces as you go, then review the **end-to-end** recipe.

## Decide on a Target

What's the idea: clone a commercial beer, brew to **BJCP style**, modify an existing recipe, fuse styles, or follow an inspiration/food idea?

- **Cloning** → research hard (brewery site, beeradvocate/ratebeer, style books, forums, your own brewery visit notes). You're assembling puzzle pieces — overall parameters first, then ingredient/process clues. Take others' "clone recipes" with a grain of salt. **Do a structured tasting** of the target and record a detailed profile to compare against.
- **Brewing to style** → re-read the style description, especially overall impression and **balance**; don't tweak minor points at the expense of drinkability.
- However you get there, **define the target profile first.** Components include **processes**, not just ingredients. Goal: a consistent, predictable outcome — how you get there is your business.

## Determine Stylistic Parameters

Set goals for **OG, FG, ABV, IBU, color**. Attenuation, ABV, OG, and FG are all related and drive **balance**. For a clone, you often can't find every number — estimate color from photos, measure a commercial beer's **FG** by de-gassing and using a hydrometer. Hitting the numbers isn't enough to be "in style."

> **Map a style by its neighbors.** A useful way to picture where a beer sits in "style space" is to focus on one style and show only its **nearest neighboring styles** — then note which attribute, when changed, turns it into each adjacent style. It clarifies what actually defines your target and which knobs move it.

## Let Flavor Drive Ingredients (the hard part)

Match **inputs (ingredients) to outputs (final flavors)** — this leans entirely on knowing your ingredients' finished profiles. See [[mastering-ingredients]].

- **Yeast first** — pick by style (some are obvious like Kölsch); for a clone, use the brewery's strain if available (mrmalty.com yeast chart). Origin is Strong's single most important yeast factor. See [[choosing-a-yeast-strain]].
- **Fermentation method follows the yeast** — pick the temperature for the character you want (Strong aims for clean: pitch cool, let it rise). Decide whether fruitiness comes from **yeast or malt** before choosing other ingredients. See [[managing-fermentation]].
- **Grain bill in percentages** (scales easily; matches references like *Designing Great Beers*). Pick by **country of origin** of the style, then blend base/character/crystal/roasted malts for the profile. See [[selecting-malt]] and [[characterizing-malt]].
- **Hops** — first set the **number and quantity** of additions, then varieties. Per 5 gal: ¼ oz very light · ½ oz noticeable · 1 oz strong · 2 oz very strong. Watch total **hop mass** (grassy/vegetal/tannin from too much). See [[selecting-hops]].
- Specify ingredients precisely enough **to place an order** (maltster, hop form). Keep **as-planned and as-brewed** notes separate.
- **Water** — note any stylistic mineral profile (e.g. sulfate for Burton); kettle additions for flavor, on top of mash-pH needs. See [[adjusting-water]].

## Adjust for Attenuation, Then Method

- **Attenuation** — decide whether to add **sugar** (more attenuation) or **dextrins** (CaraPils, oats — less). This is what separates a double IPA from an American barleywine. Think **wort composition first**, yeast spec second. See [[adjusting-final-gravity-and-attenuation]].
- **Mash method** — can add flavor/color (decoction) and raise attenuation (step/decoction); rest temps set wort composition. Many effects are **replaceable** (add sugar/dextrin instead of changing mash temp; aromatic malt instead of decoction). Remember side effects (decoction darkens — bad for hefeweizen). See [[step-mash]] and [[decoction-mash]].
- **Hopping method** — finalize flavor/aroma additions, calculate their IBUs, then set the bittering addition. **FWH** gives big flavor (Strong cuts the amount 20–35%, calculates bitterness as a ~20-min addition). Knockout/dry hops add no IBUs. Recheck total IBU when done. See [[hopping]].
- **Adjust water last, only if needed** — Strong starts from RO and makes minor adjustments, mainly ensuring **calcium** in the mash. **Try low-mineral water.**

## Sanity Check

Review the finished recipe against your style targets. Software speeds calculations and revisions, but Strong outlines on paper first to keep creativity unconstrained, then hits targets in software. **Keep detailed notes** of how it turned out — and feed lessons into the next batch.
