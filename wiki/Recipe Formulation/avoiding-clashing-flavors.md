# Avoiding Clashing Flavors

*From Gordon Strong, "Brewing Better Beer" — Envisioning Your Beer. Part of [[recipe-formulation]].*

Once a beer is balanced, drinkability still matters — and the surest way to improve it is **avoiding clashing flavors**. Understand the common clashes and how flavors complement each other, and you sidestep most problems. The ones Strong sees most in commercial and competition beers:

| Clash | Why it fails / how to avoid |
|-------|-----------------------------|
| **Phenolics + harshness** | Don't pair harsh hops/grains with phenolic yeast, or stack multiple harshness sources. High-sulfate water makes hops harsh; high-pH water extracts grain harshness; harsh hop varieties and long boils add more. Combine with a Trappist/weizen yeast and it **amplifies**. |
| **Dark malts + citrusy/piney hops** | The acidity clashes; citrus makes dark malts taste like **stale, bad coffee**. This is exactly why black IPAs go **easy on the dark malts**. |
| **Yeast/spice/fruit + bitterness/late hops** | Weizens, witbiers, lambics, fruit/spice beers run **low bitterness** for a reason — too much hop **steps on** the yeast/spice/fruit, or clashes. Don't overhop these unless you know the combo works. |
| **Fruit + spice** | A classic pairing, but combos must work like in food — **plum + cinnamon yes, plum + rosemary no.** |
| **Sour + bitter** | Naturally unappealing (the "lizard brain" reads both as poison markers). Watch hidden acidity from fruit, lots of dark grain, and citrusy hops. **Taste raw ingredients** to characterize their contributions. |
| **Spices + coffee (harsh + harsher)** | Both bring **tannins** (spices are bark/seeds; coffee turns tannic/acidic when boiled). Strong **cold-presses coffee** and adds at knockout; makes **teas from spices** and blends to taste. Spices need **brief heat** to peak (like Indian cooking); long cold steeping draws tannins. Don't pile harshness onto existing hop harshness. |
| **Bitterness + alcohol** | Spiking beer with vodka sharply raises perceived **bitterness**. Under-conditioned high-ABV beer tastes hot; alcohol flavor adds to bitterness. Balance hop (and malt) bitterness against the alcohol's contribution. |
| **Spicy/hot + bitterness/alcohol** | Hot spices (cinnamon, ginger) and chiles **amplify** bitterness and alcohol impression; spiciness can be mistaken for **fusels**, making tasters think the beer is flawed. **Raise residual sweetness** to balance spicy/hot beers. |

## Crystal Malt + American Hops

At the 2004 NHC, Vinnie Cilurzo (Russian River) advised that **crystal malt and American hops don't mix** — keep crystal **below 5%** (ideally 3.5–4%), in the **40–45 °L** range, for American IPA/DIPA. Strong's take: too much crystal makes these beers **too sweet** when the style demands high attenuation — he finds *that* fault more objectionable than the clash, but agrees **darker crystal malts** bring acidity and dark-malt flavors that trigger the clash warnings above.
