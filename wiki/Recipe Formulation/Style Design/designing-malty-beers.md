# Designing Malty Beers: Scotch Ale and the Stout Family

*Via the Birra Podcast, "Cervezas maltosas con Fede Zanetti" (T2E03), tracing the iterative development of a commercial Scotch Ale and Stout family recipes. Companion to [[designing-hoppy-beers]] and [[malt-production-and-character]]; applies the general process in [[recipe-formulation]].*

## Scotch Ale: A Recipe's Evolution

A real recipe's development arc, as a case study in iterative malt-bill refinement: the earliest version was Pale malt + Caramel 60/120 + Special B + roasted barley. Over successive brews it evolved to 7 malts — Pilsner + Pale base, a substantial amount of Munich, a small percentage of Biscuit, a substantial amount of Caramel 60, Melanoidin, and a very small amount of roasted barley — while roasted barley was cut drastically and Cararoma was restricted.

- **Swap part of the Pale malt for Pilsner** for more drinkability (tomabilidad) without losing the malt-forward base.
- **Munich** supplies the characteristic bready flavor that complements the caramel malts (see the Maillard-reaction background in [[malt-production-and-character]]).
- **Biscuit** in a small percentage pushes a "biscuit" note further than base malt alone would.
- **Melanoidin**, the last ingredient added, contributes a straightforward "fermented grain" maltiness — note that melanoidin malt is itself somewhat ester-like in character, worth accounting for when pairing with an already-ester-forward yeast strain like S-04.
- **Roasted barley, used minimally** (a small fraction of the original amount, scaled to batch size), gives copper color with ruby highlights and a dry finish — without dominating the caramel malt flavor.

### Color: Roasted Barley vs. High-Color Caramel

For red/copper beers, a **small amount of roasted barley** gives a ruby highlight and dry finish that high-color caramel malts (120, 140, Cararoma) can't replicate — those give orange/deep amber tones instead, and using enough of them to hit a deep color risks overwhelming the balance. If a red beer's color needs correcting without disturbing its flavor, reach for a touch of roasted barley rather than more high-color caramel.

### Final Gravity: 1.014 as the Drinkability Sweet Spot

Through iteration (FG ranged from 1.008 up to 1.020 across versions), FG 1.014 (at OG 1.050–1.052) was identified as the balance point for a keg-strength Scotch Ale — too high (1.020) reads cloying, too low loses the malt character the style is built around.

**Getting there with S-04**: a high mash temperature (70°C) combined with S-04 produces good dextrin formation that caps attenuation around that FG target — S-04 would attenuate further at a standard mash temperature, but the high mash locks in unfermentable dextrins the strain can't touch, giving body without the strain needing to underperform.

- **Mash pH must stay tight around 5.4** at that high mash temperature — pushing to 5.5–5.6 favors alpha-amylase too strongly at that temperature and finishing gravity can spike toward 1.020. At this combination of high mash temp and specific pH, a single degree of mash temperature or a tenth of a pH point can swing FG from 1.014 to anywhere between 1.011 and 1.020 — treat this as a narrow, carefully-held target, not a rough range.
- **Reduce S-04's ester output with a higher pitch rate**, not just lower temperature — moving from 0.75 to 1.5 million cells/mL (raised gradually, in increments, across test batches) meaningfully cut esters. Fermenting under pressure was tried too but was less effective than the pitch-rate increase for this specific goal.
- **Ferment S-04 cool for this style**: 17°C reduces ester production and lets the malt character lead rather than compete with fruity esters — historically, Scotch ales were mashed high and fermented cool. Don't go as low as 15°C; that's too extreme for S-04 to perform well.
- **Watch higher/fusel alcohols for foam's sake, not just flavor**: fusel alcohols are documented to hurt foam formation and retention, and they're also raw material for further ester production — if a beer shows heavy esters from a stressed fermentation, check whether elevated fusels are riding along with them. Reducing fusels (see [[yeast-metabolism-and-flavor-byproducts]]) improves both the sensory profile and head retention together.

### Water and Hops for Scotch Ale

Start from an **Edinburgh water profile** (available on Brewer's Friend) and adjust sensorially from there: more carbonate to balance the roasted barley's acidity, more chloride for body. A worked example at 1200 L scale: 180 g calcium chloride + 90 g calcium sulfate over the Edinburgh base — the sulfate addition let hop mass drop from 500 g to 450 g while keeping the same perceived bitterness.

For hopping, **use only a single 90-minute bittering addition** with a high-alpha variety (Zeus, Magnum — 14–17% AA), targeting **18–19 IBU** (16 reads sweet, 20 reads too bitter) — a flavor addition at 20–25 minutes was tried and dropped because it competed with the malt character instead of supporting it. Among high-alpha bittering varieties (Magnum 14%, CTZ 15%, Apollo 17%), no perceptible flavor difference showed up when used purely for 90-minute bittering — but a low-alpha variety like Cascade used the same way *is* noticeable, because achieving the same IBU needs two to three times the vegetal mass, which shows up as unwanted herbaceous character. Don't use low-alpha hops for pure bittering duty in a malt-forward beer.

**Boil at least 2 hours** for the Scotch's characteristic butterscotch note — it's the combination of the S-04 "cream" character, the caramel malts, and the extended boil together that produces it, not any single factor alone. A 3-hour boil was tested but didn't justify the extra time over 2 hours; melanoidin malt reinforces the same butterscotch character.

## The Stout Family

### Imperial Stout

- **Perlated (dehusked) roasted barley** is the key move for minimizing astringency in an intense, high-gravity stout — it gives a much more drinkable result on a beer this concentrated while keeping the coffee/chocolate character. Weyermann's huskless Carafa Special line works the same way; Carafa III (huskless) leans more toward coffee/roasty notes specifically.
- **Balance the roast with high-color caramel malts** (Special B, Cara 140) rather than leaving the roasted grain unsupported. Special B in particular gives a strong nutty/dried-fruit note that works well at Imperial strength. Save lower-color Caramel 60 for less intense styles — it doesn't stand up the same way at Imperial Stout gravity.
- **An extreme example**: OG 1.121 finishing at FG 1.045 (theoretical FG was 1.035 with the yeast used — WLP-004, a relatively high-attenuating stout strain) can still balance if the hop bittering is scaled up to match the residual sweetness. This was built for barrel-aging (first-use bourbon barrel), where FG typically drops several more points over time — a strategy specifically suited to barrel-aged, not fresh-packaged, Imperial Stout.

### Dry Stout

A precise, minimal grist: **10% perlated roasted barley + 3–4% Caramel 140 + the remainder Pale/Pilsner base**, aiming for high attenuation with a coffee note leading, a secondary bitter-chocolate note, and a subtle third note of caramel/plum. An even more minimalist version drops the caramel malt entirely: **90% Pale Ale malt + 10% roasted barley**, no caramel at all — caramel malts are better reserved for Porter or Foreign Stout, where more body and sweetness are wanted.

### Water for Roasted/Dark Styles

Roasted malts (especially Carafa III) need **real carbonate hardness** in the water, or the roast character reads as sour/acidic "reheated coffee" rather than balanced. This applies across the stout family, but **Dry Stout is the most sensitive** — its minimal grist leaves almost nowhere to hide a water mismatch: too-hard water, too-soft/RO water left unmineralized, over-hopping, or sparge astringency can each throw it out of balance on their own. Don't run RO water unremineralized, and don't run naturally very soft water unadjusted, for this style. (See [[salts]] for the general Dry Stout water target and [[malt-production-and-character]] for why Maillard-driven roast character depends so much on water chemistry to balance.)
