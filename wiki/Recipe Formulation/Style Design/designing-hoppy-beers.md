# Designing Hoppy Beers: The IPA Family

*Via the Birra Podcast, "Cervezas lupuladas" (T2E01). Applies the general process in [[recipe-formulation]] to the IPA family specifically — same OG/FG/mash/fermentation levers, aimed at each style's balance point.*

The IPA family shares one ingredient (aggressive hopping) but reaches very different balance points depending on malt bill, mash temperature, fermentation temperature, and yeast character. Treat "IPA" as a spectrum of balance decisions, not one recipe scaled up or down.

## English IPA

Built around English hop varieties, light caramel malt for balance and copper color, and an English yeast whose esters play a lead role alongside the hops. The caramel sweetness is there specifically to balance a strong bitterness — this is the one IPA subtype where caramel malt belongs.

## American IPA

The opposite approach: a simple, very pale base malt, a neutral high-attenuation yeast for dryness, and intense hop bitterness, flavor, and aroma carrying the beer on their own. Caramel malt is out of place here — it fights the dry, hop-forward profile the style is going for.

## Session IPA vs. APA — the Same Levers, Different Settings

These two styles are a good study in how small process changes shift balance at similar hopping rates:

| Lever | Session IPA | APA |
|-------|-------------|-----|
| Mash temp | 65–66°C (low body, low FG) | 68–70°C (more dextrins, more body) — or mash low and add dextrin in the boil for the same effect |
| Fermentation temp (same strain, e.g. US-05) | 18–19°C max (clean, dry) | 20–20.5°C (more esters, more complexity) |
| Resulting balance | Drier, bitterness leads | Fuller body, residual sugar tempers the same bitterness load |

The key insight: **the same hop bittering charge reads as less bitter in the APA**, purely because residual sweetness and body counteract it. Perceived bitterness is relative to what's balancing it, not just the IBU number.

## Imperial IPA

Target OG 1.080–1.085 built from Pilsner, Munich, and dextrose — nothing more. High-density wort already carries enough malt character to support heavy hopping; adding complex/melanoidin/caramel malts on top risks sliding out of Imperial IPA and into American Barleywine territory.

Don't stack every hop technique to its maximum — e.g. 40 IBU of bittering *plus* 6 g/L hot-side *plus* 10–20 g/L dry hop tends to produce an overwhelming, invasive bitterness that buries the citrus/fruit/floral character the hopping was supposed to deliver. More is not more, past a point.

**General rule against vegetal notes**: when pushing total hop load high (any IPA subtype), split it into several smaller additions rather than one large one — a single oversized addition is what tends to produce broccoli/onion/garlic vegetal character (see also [[avoiding-clashing-flavors]]).

**Hop character reads strongest in low-alcohol beer.** 8 g/L in a Session IPA can deliver more perceived hop aroma/flavor than 20 g/L in an Imperial IPA — in the lighter beer, hops aren't competing with alcohol, esters, and malt character for attention. Higher OG and ABV make big hop character progressively harder to achieve.

## Red IPA

Distinguish **low-color caramel malt** (sweet, butterscotch, toffee, honey notes) from **high-color caramel malt** (toasted, burnt-caramel, coffee, nutty notes) — same red-ish color range, radically different flavor, and the hop bill should be tuned differently for each. A workable base: mash low, 15–20% low-color caramel malt, and dextrose in the boil to raise the fermentable fraction and keep the finish dry despite the caramel malt load (caramel/specialty malts are rich in unfermentable dextrins, which fights dryness even at a low mash temperature).

## Black IPA

The "black" should be color only — the roast character should be a subtle background note (a hint of coffee/chocolate), not a stout-like torrefied profile competing with the hops. A well-made Black IPA should smell and taste close to an American IPA; tasted blind, it can be mistaken for one. To get dark color without dragging in roast flavor, use techniques like cold steeping, hot steeping, or "cold bruise" extraction on the dark grains — expect to need several test batches to dial in the balance.

## Brown IPA

A step between Red and Black: use Special B or Special W for a nutty, dried-fruit character — more malt complexity than a Red IPA, less roast than a Black IPA.

## Brute IPA

Add an enzyme that breaks down all complex sugars into fermentable ones, driving FG below water (0.996–0.998) — essentially zero body, zero malt sustain. This makes balancing hop intensity against alcohol very difficult without any malt structure to lean on; many examples end up unbalanced. Understand it as closer to "hopped, alcoholic soda water" than a beer style with a malt backbone.

## White IPA

A hybrid of Witbier and American IPA: coriander and bitter orange peel from the Belgian side, American yeast (rather than Belgian) generally preferred so the Belgian character comes from the spicing rather than yeast-driven phenols. Lean citrus-forward on hop selection — it plays well with the wheat base and the coriander/citrus peel spicing. A Belgian yeast is an option too, but produces a noticeably different (more yeast-driven) result.

## NEIPA

- **Yeast**: highly ester-producing strains with lower attenuation than West Coast strains — more residual body and sugar left behind, which is what tempers the perceived bitterness (the bitterness is still there; it's just balanced out rather than absent).
- **Grain bill**: protein-forward, built on wheat and oats for mouthfeel, and for the proteins that contribute to stable haze. Hop character is fruit-forward first, but can lean citrus, resinous, or floral depending on variety choice.
- **Density comparison at the same 6.5% ABV**: American IPA lands around OG 1.062–1.064 / FG 1.010 (dry, bitter-forward); NEIPA lands around OG 1.070 / FG 1.018–1.020 (full-bodied, sweeter, less overtly bitter) — same alcohol, very different drinking experience, purely from mash/grist/yeast choices.
- **Haze stability check**: cold-crash a sample and watch whether the haze settles over time. If it precipitates, the haze isn't stable (usually a protein/polyphenol or process issue); a well-executed NEIPA holds its haze for months.
