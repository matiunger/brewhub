# Adjusting Final Gravity & Attenuation

*From Gordon Strong, "Brewing Better Beer" — Envisioning Your Beer. Part of [[adjusting-recipe-balance]].*

Final gravity measures the **sugars and dextrins** remaining in beer:

- **Sugars** taste **sweet** and add body — sweetness **balances bitterness**.
- **Dextrins** are **not sweet** but add **body, viscosity, chewiness**.
- Both make beer **less refreshing** in excess (whole milk vs water), but their **absence** makes it watery and insipid.

> All else equal, the beer with the **lower finishing gravity and higher attenuation is more drinkable** — so when two recipes are otherwise balanced, lean toward the drier one.

## To Increase Final Gravity

Choose by whether you want more **sweetness** or just more **body**, and whether the attenuation level is right:

- **Add dextrins** — mash-temperature control, mashing starchy adjuncts, or CaraPils/dextrine malt (body, no sweetness).
- **Add crystal malts** — body **and** sweetness.
- **Raise the OG.**

## To Lower Final Gravity

- **Increase wort fermentability** — mash lower to raise the proportion of maltose.
- **Add sugar.**
- **Lower the OG.**

> **Wort composition primarily drives attenuation.** An attenuative yeast helps *somewhat*, but **focus on the wort first**. Proper fermentation management should bring the beer to **near the limit of fermentability**. See [[mash-parameters-attenuation-efficiency]] and [[understanding-attenuation]].
