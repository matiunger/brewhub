# Scaling Recipes

*From Gordon Strong, "Brewing Better Beer" — Envisioning Your Beer. Part of [[adjusting-recipe-balance]].*

To make a **scaled-up** (imperial/double) or **scaled-down** (session) version, scale it **like a batch-size change** — you're adjusting **total gravity points** (batch size × OG). Keep each grain's **percentage** the same and adjust total grist for the points you need.

## Scaling the Hops

- **Bittering:** keep the same **BU:GU ratio** as you scale gravity — that generally preserves balance.
- **Flavor/aroma:** *don't* scale linearly — they have nearly the same impact at different gravities. Strong scales finishing hops to **~25% of the linear adjustment** (double the gravity → ~1.25× the finishing hops, not 2×). Personal preference — experiment.

## The FG Trap (the classic mistake)

**Raising OG also raises FG — and a higher FG isn't always what you want.** Strong sees American brewers botch Belgian styles this way: *a tripel is not a blond barleywine* — it shouldn't be chewy and sweet.

- For **dry styles, set the FG first**, then back into the OG from the ABV you want. Many Belgians finish **below 1.010** (Duvel, Westmalle Tripel start lower than people expect but attenuate high). Same for good double IPAs.
- **Contrast:** a tripel and a strong Scotch ale can share an ABV but have completely different balance because of FG (and bitterness). Scotch ales, barleywines, and some imperial stouts start *and* finish high.

## Drinkability vs Sipper

Same ABV, **lower OG/FG (higher attenuation) → more drinkable**; **higher FG (lower attenuation) → more of a sipper**.

- If **dryness/drinkability** defines the style you're scaling up, **increase wort fermentability** (mash lower) and **add fermentable sugar** (matching the style's flavor) to avoid an excessive FG — the classic double-IPA / Belgian approach.
- **All-malt beers get chewier as they scale** (bock → doppelbock; IPA → barleywine). Want that? Go all-malt. Want to keep it dry? **Mash lower and add sugar.**

> Strong mashes very large beers **fairly low (144–150°F / 62–66°C)** since the high OG already pushes FG up — he treats **excessive sweetness in a big beer as a fault**.
