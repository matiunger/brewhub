# Balancing Bitterness, Alcohol & Tannins

*From Gordon Strong, "Brewing Better Beer" — Envisioning Your Beer. Part of [[adjusting-recipe-balance]].*

## Measured IBUs vs Perceived Bitterness

Bitterness balances maltiness and sweetness — but **measured IBUs ≠ perceived bitterness**. A rich, full, sweet beer carries **far more IBUs** and still seems balanced than a crisp, dry, well-attenuated beer can.

Two ratios to capture perceived bitterness (calculate **both** for every recipe):

| Ratio | Best for |
|-------|----------|
| **BU:GU** (IBU : original gravity) | Comparing relative balance of similar beers/styles; telling if a beer is malt- or bitterness-forward |
| **BU:FG** (IBU : final gravity) | Preserving perceived bitterness as gravity scales; comparing perceived bitterness across beers |

> **Maltiness also masks bitterness** — almost as well as sweetness. Düsseldorf altbier (esp. Zum Uerige) reads far less bitter than its IBUs suggest, not because it's sweet (it's bone dry) but because it's so **malty**. Very malty beers may need **more** IBUs to reach the same perceived bitterness as one made with neutral malt.

## Alcohol & Tannins Change With Age

Balancing alcohol is tricky because its character **depends on age and condition**:

- **Young/under-aged high-ABV beer** → hot, alcoholic, with **extra perceived bitterness** from the alcohol.
- **Properly aged** → much smoother; the alcohol flavor and bitterness **fade**.
- **Tannins** behave like those in red Bordeaux: astringent, puckering, slightly bitter when young; **smooth and complex** with age.

So alcohol/tannin balance is **first a function of age**. Strong's big beers often seem unbalanced for the **first 6 months to 2 years**, then come into balance. If serving young, he adjusts the recipe to **reduce alcohol** (lower gravity) and **tannins** (fewer hops/dark malts, or gentler handling).

## The Moving Target of Big Beers

Sweetness and malt flavor balance alcohol and tannins just as they balance bitterness — which is why a young barleywine can seem **balanced, then unbalanced, then balanced** as it ages:

1. **Young:** residual sugar balances the bitter components.
2. **Conditioning:** continued attenuation makes bitterness more apparent.
3. **Aged:** alcohol and tannins smooth out, bitterness drops.

> Strong brews big beers to age long (he enjoys the developed complexity), trading short-term drinkability for storage — and **"ladders"** his big-beer brewing to always replenish stock before the old batches run out.
