# Salts

## Common Brewing Salts

### Calcium Chloride (CaCl₂)

- Increases Calcium and Chloride
- Enhances malt sweetness and fullness
- Good for: Malty styles, porters, stouts
- Typical use: 1-5 grams per batch

### Calcium Sulfate (Gypsum) (CaSO₄)

- Increases Calcium and Sulfate
- Enhances hop bitterness and crispness
- Good for: IPAs, pale ales, hoppy beers
- Typical use: 2-10 grams per batch

### Magnesium Sulfate (Epsom Salt) (MgSO₄)

- Increases Magnesium and Sulfate
- Can enhance hop character
- Use sparingly - too much creates harsh bitterness
- Typical use: 0-3 grams per batch

### Sodium Chloride (Table Salt) (NaCl)

- Increases Sodium and Chloride
- Rounds out malt flavors
- Use non-iodized salt only
- Typical use: 0-2 grams per batch

### Baking Soda (NaHCO₃)

- Increases Sodium and Bicarbonate
- Raises mash pH
- Use when brewing very dark beers
- Typical use: 0-5 grams per batch

## Target Water Profiles

### Pale Ale / IPA

- Ca: 100-150 ppm
- SO4: 200-400 ppm
- Cl: 50-100 ppm

### Malty Styles (Stout, Porter)

- Ca: 50-100 ppm
- SO4: 50-100 ppm
- Cl: 100-200 ppm

### Light Lagers

- Very soft water
- Minimal adjustments
- Focus on pH control

### Golden / Blonde Ale

*Via the Birra Podcast, T2E04.* Light, low-bitterness beer calls for lightly mineralized water: TDS ≤ 75–80 ppm, sulfate:chloride ratio < 1, sulfate and chloride both around 35–40 ppm (below that, neither ion has much perceptible influence), sodium < 30 ppm. A near-featureless Pilsen-style water profile maximizes drinkability here.

### Scottish / Irish Red

Maltier and more complex despite a modest OG: TDS up to 140–150 ppm, sulfate:chloride ratio < 0.8, sulfate and chloride both in the 50–100 ppm range, carbonates 50–100 ppm (a wide range because it depends on how much and how dark the caramel malt in the grist is — darker caramel malt is more acidic and needs more alkalinity to offset), calcium naturally lands above 75 ppm.

### Dry Stout / Porter

TDS above 180 ppm, sulfate:chloride ratio near or under 1, and enough alkalinity to stand up to the pH drop from roasted malts. Under-mineralized water on a roasty grist gives a "reheated coffee" beer — all roast character, no supporting complexity from esters, hops, or malt sweetness underneath it.

### General Principle

Water mineralization should scale with **style intensity**, not just style category — a bigger, more heavily-hopped or more heavily-malted example of a style needs more mineral support than a session-strength example of the same style. Grist matters too: adding melanoidin or other acidic specialty malts to an otherwise light recipe (e.g. 10–15% melanoidin in a Golden Ale) pulls mash pH down further and needs a bump in alkalinity to compensate — check estimated mash pH against the actual grist rather than assuming the style's usual water profile still applies.
