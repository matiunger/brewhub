# Low Gravity

*From Gordon Strong, "Brewing Better Beer" — Troubleshooting. Part of [[style-related-faults]].*

A lower-than-expected OG throws off the **malt-to-hop balance** (bitterness seems high), makes stronger beers seem mundane, and can leave the beer **watery / lacking malt presence**.

Usually traceable to **poor efficiency** or **bad boil-volume measurement.**

## Diagnostic Walk-Through

**1. Check the malt:** slack (damp) or insect-damaged grain reduces potential extract.

**2. Check the crush:** want intact husks but flour the size of **coarsely ground black pepper**. Finer = faster conversion + higher efficiency. Allow enough time for **full conversion** (do a starch test if unsure — [[iodine-test]]). **Doughing-in** and **stirring the mash** both improve efficiency. See [[preparing-malt]] and [[doughing-in]].

**3. Check mash chemistry:** sufficient **calcium**? Mash **pH 5.2–5.5**? Calibrate thermometer/pH meter/hydrometer. Consider a **step mash**, a **mash-out**, **cutting a checkerboard** into the bed before sparging, and **slower/longer sparging** to raise efficiency. See [[understanding-efficiency]] and [[troubleshooting-brewhouse-efficiency]].

**4. Measure volume + gravity before the boil** with a calibrated stick. It's much easier to fix pre-boil — you can trade off **boil length, starting volume, and starting gravity**:

| Situation | Fix |
|-----------|-----|
| Volume correct, gravity low | **Add fermentables** (extract / sugary adjunct) to the boil |
| Volume low, gravity correct | **Shorten the boil** |
| Volume low, gravity high | **Dilute** with water |

Use your system's **evaporation rate** to set boil length for the target volume. See [[learning-your-system]].
