# Harshness

*From Gordon Strong, "Brewing Better Beer" — Troubleshooting. Part of [[style-related-faults]].*

A common problem in hop-forward beers. Strong attributes **hop-derived harshness to four factors**:

1. **Quantity of hops** — large amounts add vegetal flavors.
2. **Boil time** — long boils extract harsh compounds.
3. **Water chemistry** — high pH, high carbonates, or high sulfate make a pale hoppy beer harsh.
4. **Hop variety** — high-**cohumulone** hops are harsher (**"low" is under ~30%**, per HopUnion data); some (e.g. **Magnum**) are prized for **clean** bitterness.

## Fixes

- **All-late hopping** reduces harshness. See [[hopping]] and [[selecting-hops]].
- **Avoid post-fermentation oxygen** — oxidized hops give harsh bitterness. See [[storing-hops]].
- **Ease off sulfate** — it accentuates bitterness and harshness (especially with **noble hops**). See [[salts]] and [[water-chemistry]].
- **Use less high-alpha hop mass** to cut vegetal harshness.

## Other Sources

- **Dark grains** in long mashes/boils → harshness. **Cold-steep** them or add only at **mash-out**. See [[handling-dark-grains]].
- **Excessive alcohol** → harshness. Lower the gravity (less alcohol) and/or **cellar** longer to smooth it out. See [[balancing-bitterness-alcohol-and-tannins]].
